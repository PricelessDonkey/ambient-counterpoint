Bach's counterpoint and Steve Reich's phase music solve the same problem differently: how do two voices create something greater than their sum? In Bach, voices are shaped by will — leading tones resolve, suspensions release, cadences arrive on schedule. In ambient/phase music, structure emerges from systems — two loops of different lengths drift apart and realign, one note changes per cycle, layers subtract until silence. This chapter translates everything you've built into the looping, evolving, stripping-back language of ambient production.

An arpeggio isn't just a broken chord — it's two melodies interleaved. Take any 8-note arpeggio and split it: odd notes (1, 3, 5, 7) = voice 1, even notes (2, 4, 6, 8) = voice 2. Each sub-melody should make sense on its own — good contour, manageable leaps, a sense of going somewhere.

Before sequencing any arpeggio, extract each sub-melody and listen to it alone. If voice 1 sounds melodic and voice 2 sounds melodic, the arpeggio has built-in counterpoint. If one voice is jagged noise, reorder the notes until both halves sing.

What Good Sub-Melodies Look Like

Voice 1 here (D–F–G–E) arcs up a third then steps back down — smooth, singable. Voice 2 (E–A–F–D) leaps a fourth upward then descends by thirds — energetic entry, stable landing. Both are melodically coherent. The arpeggio carries hidden counterpoint at no extra cost.

The most important discipline in loop-based ambient music: change exactly one pitch every 2–4 repetitions. Not two. Not a chord. One. The listener's ear locks onto the pattern first; a single change registers as a meaningful event rather than a reset.

Alternate which voice evolves. If you changed a note in the top arpeggio on rep 3, change a note in the bottom arpeggio on rep 5. This creates the sensation that the loops are having a slow conversation — each responding to the other's previous move.

Which Note to Change

Prefer changes to interior notes — the ear anchors to the first and last positions in the loop. Changing note 1 or note 8 signals a major structural shift; save those for turning points. Interior changes (notes 3–6) feel like the music breathing and evolving within a stable frame. Changed notes don't have to stay in the scale: a chromatic note like Eb (the Neapolitan second) or C# (the leading tone) used once can shift the harmonic color of the whole loop without breaking the key center.

Run two loops of different lengths simultaneously — say 7 and 8 notes. They start together. But because one is shorter, it completes its cycle first and restarts slightly ahead of the other. After every cycle, they drift further apart. After LCM(7, 8) = 56 notes, they re-align and the whole sequence begins again.

The evolving vertical combinations between the two loops are the composition. You wrote two simple patterns; the system generates the harmonic complexity. This is Reich's basic insight: structure through systematic misalignment.

Setting It Up in Ableton

Clip 1: 7 eighth notes, clip set to loop. Clip 2: 8 eighth notes on a separate track, also looping. Launch both simultaneously. The drift is automatic — no automation needed. Try 5 vs 7 (LCM=35, faster cycling) or 6 vs 7 (LCM=42) for different rates of harmonic change. Smaller LCMs cycle faster and feel more restless; larger ones are meditative.

In Bach, three voices means three pitch lines. In loop music, you can run a velocity accent pattern that cycles independently of the pitch pattern — creating a third layer of rhythmic movement without adding any notes.

The pitch loop has length N; the velocity pattern has length M. When N ≠ M, the accents fall on different notes each cycle. The composition shifts emphasis constantly without the pitch content ever changing.

In Ableton

Draw your 8 notes at uniform velocity. Then go note by note and enter a 5-note velocity pattern: 100, 55, 70, 45, 85, repeating. Note 6 gets the same velocity as note 1 (100). In a standard clip this pattern is fixed — it repeats identically each loop. For true independent cycling (where the accent falls on a different pitch each repetition), you need to write a longer clip equal to the LCM of the two lengths: here, 40 notes (LCM of 8 and 5), with the full expanded velocity sequence written out, or use a cycling velocity MIDI effect. Either way, the interplay of accent pattern against pitch loop creates forward motion that isn't in the pitches themselves.

Most music builds toward a peak by adding layers, raising density, arriving at a climax. Ambient music often inverts this: begin in full bloom and subtract, letting the music dissolve. The endpoint is silence — or near-silence — not a triumphant chord.

The ghost of a removed voice is as important as what remains. Your ear keeps expecting the missing line for several bars after it disappears. This is why subtraction feels intimate — it activates the listener's memory rather than presenting everything at once.

Ghost Voices

When Loop B disappears at bar 27, your ear keeps expecting it for 2–4 bars afterward. Build your subtraction sequence so that the most recently introduced voice is the first to leave. Listeners mourn what they've just met. The last thing standing should be the oldest — the drone, the original motif, the thing that was there from bar 1.

D natural minor (Aeolian) uses C♮ as its seventh — a whole step below the tonic. No magnetic pull, no urgency. This flatness is exactly what ambient music lives in. Your loops spend the whole piece in this floating territory.

C# is the raised seventh — the leading tone, a half step below D, with powerful upward pull. In classical harmony it's always present in the dominant chord. In ambient music, save it for one single peak moment. When it appears after minutes of natural minor, the harmonic tension is enormous — disproportionate to a single altered note.

How to Deploy It

Use C♮ everywhere in your loops, always. At your chosen peak bar — the moment of highest density — change one note to C#. Let it appear in the upper voice, on a metrically strong position. Then, as you subtract layers, restore C# back to C♮. The piece sighs back into Aeolian as the structure dissolves.

The leading tone is a one-use weapon. If C# appears in bar 4, it becomes furniture. If C# appears exactly once — at bar 20, on the highest note, inside the fullest texture — it is an event. Guard it accordingly.

A pedal point is a sustained note — usually in the bass — held steady while harmonies change above it. In Bach it appears at climaxes and cadences: the bass locks onto the dominant or tonic and refuses to move. In ambient music, the pedal is the foundation of the entire piece.

The tension of a pedal comes from harmonic friction. Upper voices move through chords that don't all contain the bass note. When the bass holds D while the upper voice traces F–A–C–Bb–G–D, it passes through consonances and dissonances and back. The drone transforms that journey into something meditative rather than tense — the bass says "we're always home," while the upper voices wander.

Friction Is the Texture

Against a D drone: D–F–A (D minor chord tones) are fully consonant. E creates a dissonant second. G and C create a fourth and seventh — floating, unresolved. Bb creates a warm minor sixth. All of these are usable in ambient music — none need to resolve. The drone holds the tonal center while the upper voice paints varying degrees of tension above it. The unresolved tensions don't demand resolution; they define color.

Open your Ch 10 session (16 bars, 5 tracks — full counterpoint with texture dropout, register spread, and harmonic freeze). Today you dissolve it into ambient loops. You'll end with a 32-bar piece that moves from dense looping counterpoint to near-silence through systematic subtraction.

Step 1 — Extract the Arpeggio Loop (Track 6)

Create Track 6. Take the upper voice melody from bars 1–4 and rewrite it as an 8-note arpeggio loop: D4 E4 F4 A4 G4 F4 E4 D4, all eighth notes, clip length = 1 bar. Loop the clip. Velocity: 80 uniform. Before you continue: extract odd notes (D F G E) and play them alone, then even notes (E A F D) alone. Confirm each sub-melody is coherent. If not, reorder until both halves sing.

Step 2 — Phase Loop (Track 7)

Create Track 7. Program 7 eighth notes: A4 C5 E5 D5 G5 F5 A4. This is a Dm7 arpeggio (D–F–A–C) with passing tones (E, G). At 7 eighth notes the clip is one eighth note shorter than a bar — it drifts by one eighth note per bar. By bar 8 it has shifted a full bar. Do not align it to the grid. Let it run. The phase is the composition.

Step 3 — Drone (Track 8)

Create Track 8. One note: D2, a 32-bar clip (or a looping whole note). Use a pad patch — slow attack, long release. This is your anchor. Everything above it moves. D2 does not.

Step 4 — Addition Phase (Bars 1–16)

Bars 1–4: Track 6 + drone only. Let the loop establish. Bars 5–8: bring in Track 7. Notice the arpeggio on Track 7 immediately starts misaligning from Track 6 — one eighth note of drift per bar. Bars 9–12: change one note in Track 6 — raise note 5 (G4) to A4. The loop shifts from a descending contour to something more circular. Bars 13–16: change one note in Track 7 — raise note 2 (C5) to D5. The upper voice gains a fifth above the tonic.

Step 5 — Peak (Bars 17–24)

Bar 17: in Track 6, change note 4 from A4 to C#4 — the leading tone. This is the only C# in the entire piece. Simultaneously, add a 3-note velocity accent pattern to Track 6: notes 1, 4, and 6 at velocity 100, all others at 60. The 3-note velocity pattern cycles against the 8-note pitch loop — a third rhythmic layer, automatically. This bar is the emotional climax: three loops at different phase positions, C# sounding for the first time, maximum density.

Step 6 — Subtraction Arc (Bars 25–32)

Bar 25: restore C# → C♮ in Track 6. The leading tone releases. Flatten the velocity pattern back to 70 uniform — the third rhythmic layer disappears. Bar 27: mute Track 7. The phase loop is gone; its ghost lingers for 2–3 bars. Bar 29: simplify Track 6 to 4 quarter notes: D4 F4 A4 D4. The arpeggio dissolves into a bare D minor chord, arpeggiated slowly. Bar 31: mute Track 6. Only the drone remains. Bar 32, beat 3: automate the drone to fade to silence over 2 beats.

You now have a 32-bar ambient piece built from the same DNA as the Bach two-voice exercise in Chapter 1. The seed motif D–E–F–A from bar 1 is still the material — dissolved into texture, phased, stripped back to nothing. This is the full arc of the course: from a single line to silence, via Bach.
