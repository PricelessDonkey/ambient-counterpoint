In January 1958, Bill Evans sat at the piano and recorded a solo improvisation over a single sustained chord for nearly seven minutes. He called it Peace Piece. It is not harmonically complex — it is harmonically inert. One chord. Everything else alive. It is the purest realization of the autumnal principle: stasis + warmth + gentle motion.

The Central Fact

The left hand plays a C major 7 pedal ostinato — C and G in the low register, with chord tones above — and repeats this pattern continuously for the entire piece. The right hand improvises freely above it. Evans does not change the chord. Not once. The piece is over 400 bars long and the harmony never moves.

This works because of a principle we've been building toward across every chapter: stasis in one dimension frees every other dimension. When the listener has no harmonic narrative to follow, their attention moves into the phrase shapes, the silences, the register, the density of activity. The static harmony is not a limitation — it is a stage.

The two-zone structure is the whole technique: a completely static bottom layer creates an inhabitable harmonic space, and a completely free top layer provides the motion. Notice that the right hand does not need to be active constantly — the long tones are not less interesting than the runs. They are equally interesting, because the static left hand means there is always something to hear.

What the Left Hand Actually Does

The left hand plays a repeating figure built on C: a low C in the bass, with C–E–G chord tones above it, cycling rhythmically. The chord marking in the score reads Cmaj7 / F/G — the slash chord indicates Evans occasionally voices an F over the G bass, creating a brief sus4 inflection before returning to the root. This tiny modal ambiguity is enough to keep the harmony from feeling completely inert.

The ostinato is not a drone — it has rhythmic shape and a slight groove. But it also does not develop. It begins in bar 1 exactly as it ends in bar 400+. The listener's ear adapts to it as an environmental constant, like room tone. Once you stop hearing it as a changing event and start hearing it as background, the right hand becomes the foreground — and Evans has your full attention.

Why C Major 7?

C major 7 (C–E–G–B) has a particular floating quality among major chords. The major 7th (B) does not pull toward the 4th below it (F) the way it would in a dominant context. It simply exists at the top of the chord voicing, unresolved and undemanding. Combined with the open 5th (C–G) in the bass, the chord sounds stable but not closed — you could stay in it indefinitely, and Evans does.

Our series works in D, not C. The equivalent chord is Dmaj7 (D–F#–A–C#), or, staying in the minor-mode world of this series, Dm(add9) — the chord we began the Build It thread with in Chapter 2. Evans is doing in C major exactly what we've been building toward in D minor.

The Right Hand Breathes

Evans does not improvise continuously. The right-hand activity rises and falls in cycles: long held notes give way to eighth-note runs, runs give way to triplets, triplets cascade downward and settle on a held tone, then silence — then the cycle begins again. This is the macro rhythm of the piece, operating above the level of the bar.

The score makes this visible: bars 1–4 are almost static (long tones only). By bars 5–8, eighth-note figures appear. Bars 9–12 bring triplet runs. Bars 13–16 mark the first dense scale passages. Then Evans backs off. The "Scale Exercise (beginning)" and "Scale Exercise (advanced)" sections represent the moment of maximum density — not the sustained state. After them, the piece breathes back out.

The rests and long tones are not waiting — they are the music. The silence after a dense run lets the reverb tail breathe; the next long tone arrives with more weight because of the silence before it. This breathing structure — dense → sparse → dense — creates large-scale rhythm without a single chord change. Cross-reference: this is the macro form equivalent of the sparse→active→sparse phrase cycle we described in Chapter 11.

Where the Right Hand Goes

Evans's phrases descend. Even the ascending runs — and there are many — are decorative in function: they reach up to a high point before cascading back down. The overall trajectory of each phrase cycle is downward. You can see this in the score: the scale exercise passages (bar 17+) show ascending then descending patterns, but the final arrival is always a lower note, often held. The phrase reaches and then surrenders.

This is precisely the melodic shape described in Chapter 13 — the archetypal autumnal contour. Evans applies it not just to individual phrases but to entire phrase cycles: the whole arc of sparse → dense → sparse traces the same upward-reach and downward-settle shape at a larger scale.

Four Transferable Principles

1. A static harmonic bed creates freedom above it. The left-hand pedal does not just provide harmony — it removes harmonic anxiety. The listener stops asking "where is this going?" and starts inhabiting the present moment. This is the autumnal principle at maximum strength.

2. Phrase cycles create macro rhythm without chord changes. The sparse/active/sparse breathing structure gives the piece a felt shape — a sense of time passing, of something building and releasing — without moving the harmony at all. All the "form" comes from the right-hand density arc.

3. Falling phrases are the shape of surrender. Evans reaches up — always briefly — before coming down. The reaching creates a moment of yearning; the cascade creates the release. You need both for the autumnal feeling. All descent with no reach is just sadness; all reach with no descent is just striving.

4. Silence is structural, not decorative. The rests between Evans's phrases are not pauses — they are the moments when the reverb tail decays and the listener's ear resets, ready for the next phrase to arrive with full weight. Remove the silence and the phrases lose their meaning.

The whole of Peace Piece can be summarized in one instruction: play something beautiful, let it fall, let it settle, let the room breathe, then do it again. The piece is not about what Evans plays. It is about the relationship between what he plays and what he doesn't.

Add a Free Voice Above the Pedal

Your Ableton piece now has three layers from the prior sessions: harmonic bed, textural pad, and bass pedal (D2). Add one more track: the Peace Piece voice. This is the highest-register layer — D5 to A5 range — and it is deliberately the most free and sparse thing in the piece.

Program it with these constraints:

  • No more than 8–10 notes in any 16-bar span. Long held notes count as one note.
  • Every phrase should fall overall — end lower than it began.
  • Leave at least 3 bars of silence between each phrase.
  • Use D Aeolian or D Dorian pitches only. No chromatic passing notes yet.
  • Apply the behind-the-beat nudge from Ch 8 (8–12ms) to every note onset.

Play the full 64-bar arrangement with all four layers running. The Peace Piece voice should feel like something observed from a distance — present but not dominant. If it feels too busy, remove notes until it feels like you've removed too many — that is usually the right amount.
