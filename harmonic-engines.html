Bach's Harmonic
Engines

The chord progressions and bass patterns that power Fortspinnung. These are the roads the spinning melody travels on — each one creates a different flavor of inevitability.

All of these progressions are in D minor for consistency. The melodic sequences from the phrase shapes toolkit ride on top of these harmonic patterns — each sequence repetition lands on the next chord, so harmonic motion and melodic sequencing reinforce each other.
01
Circle of Fifths Descent

Bach's most fundamental Fortspinnung engine. Each root drops a fifth (or rises a fourth — same thing). Your spinning motif rides this progression down, with each sequence repetition landing on the next chord. You don't need the whole circle — even three or four steps give you the feeling of inevitability.

02
The 5–1 Ratchet

The circle of fifths with the screws tightened. You turn each chord into a dominant 7th of the next, adding chromatic leading tones that create half-step magnetism at every link. Each chord gets its own little V→I arrival — this is what tonicization means in practice.

They're not mutually exclusive — one is the enriched version of the other. Bach blends them: structurally important arrivals get the ratchet treatment, passing chords stay diatonic. That mix of diatonic and chromatic within one sequence is why his harmony sounds fluid rather than mechanical.

How to Build Each Link

Every chord's root in a circle of fifths is already the 5th of the next chord. You don't have to go searching — you just re-spell the chord you're on as a dominant 7th (major triad + minor 7th). The chromatic alteration that results is your leading tone, the half-step magnet that makes the arrival inevitable.

Each link only requires changing one or two notes from the diatonic version to tighten the screw. Look at the next chord, realize you're already standing on its dominant, then adjust quality to a dominant 7th. The chromatic note that results — that's your leading tone.
03
Descending Bass Line

The bass walks down the scale stepwise while chords alternate between root position and first inversion above it, creating a smooth cascade. This is the engine behind the Chaconne, countless sarabandes, and the lament bass tradition. In the piano roll: put a steady descending scale in the low register and hang your spinning melody over it.

04
The Deceptive Move

After setting up a strong dominant (A7 heading to D minor), land on B♭ major instead. The bass moves up a half step (A→B♭) rather than down a fifth (A→D). Bach uses this to extend a sentence — you thought the Epilog was about to cadence, but the deceptive resolution kicks you back into another round of Fortspinnung.

05
The Neapolitan Drop

In D minor, suddenly hitting E♭ major (Neapolitan) — a half step above the tonic — creates a dark, dramatic color. Bach places it right before the final cadence. It functions as a souped-up subdominant: it pulls toward the dominant even harder than Gm because of the half-step tension between E♭ and D.

06
Pedal Point

Hold a D in the bass for four or eight bars while upper voices cycle through chords that get increasingly tense — then everything resolves back to D minor in all voices. The pedal creates a gravitational anchor while harmony gets wild above it. Resolution feels massive because the bass has been promising "we're in D" the whole time.
