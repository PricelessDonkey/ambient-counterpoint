Melody without harmony is a river without banks. The two-voice counterpoint you built in Chapters 1–4 floats above an implied harmony, but that harmony has been vague — inferred from context rather than stated. This chapter makes the harmonic structure explicit: what chord progressions Bach reached for first, why they work, and how to put them under your music in Ableton. A strong bass line doesn't just support melody; it creates the gravitational field that makes melody meaningful.

The fundamental harmonic engine

When a chord's root moves down a fifth (or equivalently up a fourth), the ear hears harmonic arrival — a sense of weight settling. One V-I move feels like a cadence. A chain of them feels unstoppable. In D minor, the diatonic circle descends: Dm → Gm → C → Dm (short loop) or the full 8-step journey through all the diatonic roots.

Bach uses this as his default engine. When a passage feels purposeful and inevitable, the bass is almost certainly moving in fifths underneath. For an in-depth breakdown with the full circular layout, see harmonic-engines.html.

Using a short loop vs. the full circle

You don't need all eight chords every time. The most common short loops in D minor: Dm–Gm–C–Dm (i–iv–VII–i), Dm–Am–Gm–Am (i–v–iv–v), or Am–Dm–Gm–Am starting on the dominant. The minimum useful circle move is just two chords: any V-I pair drawn from the chain.

Every time a root moves down a fifth, the ear hears "landing." String enough of these together and you have unstoppable harmonic momentum — each bar becomes an arrival that also launches the next.

Chromaticizing every link in the chain

The diatonic circle of fifths uses the natural minor scale throughout. But you can intensify every step by inserting a secondary dominant — a dominant 7th chord built on the fifth of the target chord — just before you arrive. This injects chromatic tension (a note outside the natural minor scale) that the next chord must resolve.

In D minor, approaching Gm: instead of going there directly, play D7 (D–F#–A–C). The F# is chromatic — D minor has F natural — and it acts as a leading tone that pulls up to G. Then to approach C major: play G7 (G–B–D–F). The B natural is chromatic — D minor has Bb — and it acts as a leading tone that pulls up to C. Each V7 introduces one chromatic note that makes the arrival feel earned rather than routine.

For a detailed visual breakdown of how to build each link, see harmonic-engines.html — the 5-1 Ratchet section.

The chromatic note in each secondary dominant

Each secondary dominant introduces one note outside D natural minor. D7 adds F# (the leading tone to G — D minor has F natural). G7 adds B natural (the leading tone to C — D minor has Bb). C7 adds no chromatic note at all: C–E–G–Bb are all diatonic to D natural minor, so C7 is fully diatonic. A7 adds C# (the leading tone to D — D minor has C natural). These chromatic upper neighbors are what give the ratchet its "winding up" quality: the melody gets pulled tighter before each release.

The 5-1 ratchet transforms a smooth circle of fifths into a tightening coil. Each secondary dominant charges a capacitor; each diatonic arrival discharges it. Stack three or four in a row and the music feels pulled forward like a current.

The most emotionally potent bass pattern in Western music

The lament bass descends stepwise from the tonic to the dominant: in D minor, D–C–Bb–A, one note per bar. This four-note descent has been the foundation of grief, longing, and inexorable fate from Purcell's "Dido's Lament" through countless Baroque and contemporary pieces. The downward motion feels like a weight that cannot be stopped. The dominant arrival on A sets up the return to Dm — the bass sinks to its lowest point and turns around.

Over the lament bass, Bach typically alternates between root position and first-inversion chords to keep the upper voices smooth. The bass note doesn't have to be the root of the chord — it just needs to descend.

Variants and extensions

The basic 4-bar lament can be extended: D–C–Bb–A–G–F#–G–A returns the bass back up, creating a pendulum. Or keep descending: D–C–Bb–A–G–F–E–Dm/F for an 8-bar ground bass. The first-inversion version — D/F–C/E–Bb/D–A/C# — keeps each bass note as the third of the chord above, giving the descent a more mysterious, suspended quality because no single note announces itself as the root.

The lament bass works because stepwise descent is the simplest possible bass motion — no leaps, no surprises — and yet it is inexorable. Every note confirms the fall. The dominant at the bottom is not an ending; it is a place of reckoning before the turn.

Against the natural harmonic grain

Harmonic weight naturally sinks — roots want to descend by fifths or steps, pulling toward the tonic. An ascending bass fights this tendency, which creates a sense of striving, building, or defying gravity. It is less common than descent in Bach, but when he uses it, it creates momentum of a different kind: not the fatalistic pull of the lament bass but an upward surge.

The standard ascending bass in D minor walks up the scale from D3 to A3: D–E–F–G–A. To keep the voice-leading smooth, Bach typically puts the upper voices in first-inversion chords over the passing bass notes (E and G). This way the bass movement is independent of the harmonic root movement — the bass climbs while the harmony still progresses in sensible directions.

In the DAW

In Ableton, an ascending bass line works especially well when your upper voice is also ascending — contrary motion is not required here. Two voices climbing together builds intensity over the phrase. The dominant arrival (A3/A7) at the top naturally calls for a strong downward response in the next bar, whether that is a return to D or a sudden drop into a new texture.

The ascending bass is rare because it is hard — it works against harmonic gravity. But that resistance is the point. Used sparingly, it creates a sense of building pressure that a descending bass cannot match.

Every half step instead of diatonic steps

The diatonic lament bass skips some chromatic notes (D to C is a whole step; C to Bb is also a whole step). The chromatic bass descent fills in every semitone: D–C#–C–B–Bb–A. Six notes, all half steps. This is darker, slower, and more chromatic than the diatonic version — each step feels reluctant, as if something is being pulled down against its will.

In D minor: D3 is the tonic, C# is chromatic (not in natural minor), C is diatonic (VII), B is chromatic (raised 6th — D minor has Bb, not B natural), Bb is the diatonic minor sixth, A is the dominant. The C# and the B each introduce a chromatic pull into the descent — the C# resolves downward to C, and the B gives the line a moment of unexpected brightness before sinking to Bb.

Chords over the chromatic bass

The tricky notes are C# and B. Over C# bass: you can use Dm/C# (D minor chord with C# in the bass — a suspended leading tone beneath), or A/C# (A major in first inversion, which is the V chord with its third in the bass). Over B bass: G/B (G major first inversion) or Em7b5/B work. The goal is to keep the harmonic language coherent while the bass slides chromatically beneath it.

The chromatic descent is the maximum version of harmonic inevitability — nothing is skipped, nothing escapes. Purcell used it as a ground bass looped throughout "Dido's Lament." In a sequencer, it loops hypnotically; try 6 bars of ground bass with a freely evolving melody above.

A day trip vs. moving house

Tonicization is when you briefly treat a non-tonic chord as if it were home — one V-I gesture aimed at it, then you move on. The secondary dominant (Card 2 of this chapter) is the tool: play D7→Gm to tonicize Gm for one moment, then continue elsewhere. Your ear registers Gm as momentarily important, but D minor remains the overarching home. No new key has been established.

Modulation is when you actually leave your home key and set up shop in a new one. Not just one V-I gesture but multiple phrases, full cadences, and melodic content that treats the new key as the reference. The listener forgets D minor for a while. Returning to D minor later feels like a homecoming — because the new key was real enough to generate its own gravitational field.

Which relative keys to visit

From D minor, the most natural modulations are to the relative major (F major — same key signature, feels like stepping into sunlight), the dominant minor (A minor — shares six of seven pitches with D minor, differs only in B vs Bb, and feels brighter), and occasionally the subdominant minor (G minor — darker, sinking further into shadow). Bach rarely modulates to distant keys in a short piece; he visits the nearest relatives and returns.

In ambient and electronic music, modulation works differently: you can spend minutes in a new key, and the return to D minor can hit like a structural event rather than a resolution. Think of Philip Glass moving between modal regions in Glassworks — the key changes are architectural, not cadential.

Tonicization colors a single chord without changing your address. Modulation packs your bags. The difference is duration and commitment: one V-I is a glance; a full phrase with its own cadence is a conversation.

Open your Ch 04 session — the two-voice piece with contrary motion in bar 1, a cross-relation in bar 2, a pedal freeze and suspension preparation in bar 3, and the 4-3 suspension resolving through a chromatic enclosure to D4 in bar 4. Those two voices now become the upper and middle voices. This session, you will add a third MIDI track for the bass — and you will try two different bass lines to hear how radically the harmonic context changes the piece's feel.

New bass track setup

Create a new MIDI track in Ableton. Use a bass-range instrument — a sine-wave sub on your Volca Bass, or a low-register pad. Tune it down an octave from your middle voice so the bass lives in octave 2. The upper voice is in octave 4–5, the middle in octave 3–4, the new bass in octave 2–3. All three voices now have their own register lane.

Option A — Circle of Fifths Bass

Give each bar its own chord root, moving down by fifths:

  • Bar 1: D2 — whole note (Dm, i)
  • Bar 2: G2 — whole note (Gm, iv — a fifth below D)
  • Bar 3: C3 — whole note (C major, VII — a fifth below G)
  • Bar 4: A2 — whole note (A major / A7, V — sets up the Dm return)

The piece now feels purposeful and harmonic — each bar arrives somewhere new. The cross-relation in bar 2 (F# vs F♮) sounds more intentional over Gm because the Gm context makes F natural the expected note, making F# sound like a Dorian shimmer.

Option B — Lament Bass

Replace Option A with a stepwise descent:

  • Bar 1: D2 — whole note (Dm root)
  • Bar 2: C2 — whole note (C major / step down)
  • Bar 3: Bb1 — whole note (Bb major / step down)
  • Bar 4: A1 — whole note (A7 dominant)

The lament bass transforms the piece. Bar 3's pedal freeze in the upper voices now sits over a descending bass — the "stillness" of the pedal is ironic because the ground is sinking beneath it. The suspension and enclosure in bar 4 resolve over the dominant A1, which makes the final D4 arrival feel earned in a way it wasn't before: the bass waited on the dominant while the upper voice resolved.

Compare and listen

Play both versions back-to-back without changing anything else. The upper voices are identical — same notes, same timing, same cross-relation, same suspension. Only the bass changes. Notice:

  • Option A (Circle) feels more harmonically mobile — each bar has a distinct chord identity. The piece travels.
  • Option B (Lament) feels more inevitable and emotionally weighty. The bass defines the mood; the upper voices become a countermelody to the sinking ground.

Keep whichever resonates, or try a hybrid: use the lament bass for the first loop and the circle bass for a variation, revealing that the same melody can carry different harmonic worlds.
