Every chord in tonal music has a job. The augmented sixth chord's job is singular and obsessive: pull the listener toward the dominant with maximum force. It achieves this through one dissonant interval — an augmented sixth — whose two outer notes are half-step magnets pointing inward at the dominant pitch. This chapter covers the three varieties, the voice-leading physics behind them, how Bach deployed them at emotional high points, and how to install one in your own cadences.

Every augmented sixth chord shares one defining feature: the augmented sixth interval. In D minor, this spans Bb (the lowered 6th scale degree) up to G# (the raised 4th scale degree). These two pitches behave like magnets — Bb slides down by a half-step to A, and G# pulls up by a half-step to A. Both outer voices arrive simultaneously on the dominant pitch in contrary motion.

This inward contrary motion is why augmented sixth chords feel more urgent than any other pre-dominant: the outer voices are both converging toward the same target from opposite sides at the same time.

The G# here is not the leading tone of D minor — that is C#. G# has been chromatically raised from the diatonic 4th scale degree (G) to sit exactly one half-step below A. It functions as a leading tone of the dominant: its presence in the pre-dominant chord pre-points the resolution, adding a second half-step pull toward A on top of the pull already supplied by Bb.

All three varieties share the Bb/G# outer shell. They differ in their inner voices — the notes between Bb and G#. Think of the outer shell as the bread, the inner voices as the filling: same sandwich, three fillings.

Italian 6th — It⁶   Bb – D – G#

Three voices. The most common form in Bach. Lean and direct — no extra colour, just the outer-shell pull with the tonic pitch D filling the middle. Often voiced with Bb in the bass (first inversion).

French 6th — Fr⁶   Bb – D – E – G#

Four voices. The E creates a major second against D — crunchy dissonance layered on top of the already-tense augmented sixth. The French 6th has the most unsettled, searching quality of the three. More common in Romantic music than in strict Baroque writing.

German 6th — Gr⁶   Bb – D – F – G#

Four voices. The F adds a perfect fifth above Bb, making the chord enharmonically identical to a Bb dominant 7th chord (Bb–D–F–Ab). Thickest and most symphonic-sounding — and the one that requires special handling in Bach-style writing (see Card 3).

The German 6th contains a perfect fifth: Bb up to F. The dominant A major chord also contains a perfect fifth: A up to E. When Gr⁶ moves directly to V, these two fifths move in parallel — the cardinal sin of Bach-style counterpoint. His solution: route through a cadential I⁶₄ that breaks up the motion.

In the correct path: the F in the German 6th steps down to E within the cadential I⁶₄. Then E remains as the dominant chord arrives — no parallel motion. The cadential I⁶₄ acts as a buffer that breaks up the forbidden interval before the dominant lands.

Augmented sixth chords are more central to Classical-era writing (Mozart, Haydn) and Romanticism (Chopin, Wagner) than to the Baroque. When Bach uses them, the context is specific and the emotional charge is high.

1. The Italian 6th as Passage

Bach's most common use is the Italian 6th in minor-key works — the St. Matthew Passion, the Well-Tempered Clavier — typically to intensify a Phrygian cadence. The lean three-voice Italian 6th fits naturally into Baroque voice-leading without requiring the extra handling the German 6th demands.

2. The German 6th Via the Cadential 6/4

When Bach employs the German 6th, it almost always routes through a cadential I⁶₄ first. This is not a stylistic choice — it is a necessity. The parallel fifths problem is unavoidable on a direct resolution. Later composers (Beethoven, Schubert) occasionally allowed the direct move as an expressive roughness. Bach did not.

3. Emotional Weight — Affekt

In Baroque Affekt theory, each chord carried an emotional character. The dissonant augmented sixth interval — harsh, yearning, unresolved — was associated with extreme grief, physical pain, or the cross (crucifixus). Bach reserved augmented sixth chords for moments where the text or context demanded maximum intensity: the word "Schmerzen" (pain), supplicatory passages, or the climax of a lament bass. The chord is not just a harmonic colour — it is a word.

In the Epilog — the closing bars of a Baroque sentence — cadences follow a consistent four-stage pattern. The augmented sixth is the most powerful form of Stage 1. See Ch 07 — The Sentence for the full Vordersatz–Fortspinnung–Epilog architecture.

The cadential I⁶₄ is not a return to tonic. The bass is already on A — the dominant pitch. The D and F above it are suspensions that delay the C# and E of the full dominant chord. Your ear is still on the way to the cadence, not at it.

The power of augmented sixth chords comes from a precise alignment of forces. Every voice in the chain from It⁶ through V⁷ to i has a specific gravitational direction it must move. Understanding these directions tells you how to voice-lead any chromatic pre-dominant cleanly.

The 7th Must Sigh Downward

In any dominant 7th chord, the 7th is an unstable dissonance that must resolve downward by step. In Baroque Affekt theory this downward stepwise motion was called the Seufzer — the sigh. In A7 resolving to Dm, the G (the 7th) must fall to F. If it leaps upward or stays, the resolution sounds incomplete.

The Leading Tone Must Pull Upward

The leading tone (C# in D minor) is exactly one half-step below the tonic. Its upward pull to D is the strongest single motion in tonal music. In any outer voice that has C#, it resolves to D — no exceptions in Bach.

Two forces operating in opposite directions — the 7th sighing down while the leading tone pulls up — is precisely what makes V⁷→i feel like a physical release. The voices collapse inward toward stable tones. This is the same contrary-motion logic as the augmented sixth's outer shell (Bb↓, G#↑), just one stage later in the chain. The whole cadence is one continuous funnel.

In a circle-of-fifths sequence — where the bass leaps down by fifths — the melody does not follow. If both voices moved the same direction by the same intervals, you would have parallel fifths. Instead, the bass provides structural leaps and the melody provides the linear path through the harmonies.

The Rule

While the bass makes large structural leaps, the melody moves by step or common tone. The melody needs only a note that belongs to the current chord (root, third, or fifth) — and should avoid doubling the exact bass note whenever possible. The third or fifth in the melody keeps the texture fuller than root doubling.

Resolving 7ths Through the Melody

The most elegant use of this independence: the melody carries the 7th of each chord and resolves it downward by step as the bass leaps on to the next root. The bass drives forward; the melody sighs.

Bass note Chord Melody note Logic
G G⁷ F4 The 7th — ready to sigh down
C C E4 F resolves down to the 3rd of C
F F A4 Leap to the 3rd of F (F–A–C)

The bass provides the math — structural 5th leaps outlining the harmonic skeleton. The melody provides the poetry — a linear, mostly-stepwise path through the changing harmonies. They are partners, not twins. The moment they mirror each other's intervals and directions, voices merge — and parallel fifths become unavoidable.

The session is 60 bars. Target: bars 53–60, the consequent phrase from Chapter 13. Right now that phrase closes with a standard V⁷→i cadence. You are going to install an Italian 6th as the pre-dominant, route it through the cadential I⁶₄, and arrive at the tonic with the full four-stage sequence.

Step 1 — Locate bar 58, beat 3

Open your session. Navigate to bar 58. This is the bar before the final V⁷→i resolution. Beat 3 is the insertion point for the augmented sixth.

Step 2 — Italian 6th (bar 58, beat 3)

In your upper-voice track, place Bb4 and G#4 simultaneously on beat 3 of bar 58. In your bass track, place Bb2 on the same beat. This is the Italian 6th: Bb in the bass, D4 (if you have it) and G#4 above. The outer shell Bb/G# is what matters — the pull toward A is already loaded.

Step 3 — Cadential I⁶₄ (bar 59, beat 1)

On beat 1 of bar 59: upper voice moves to D4 + F4, bass moves to A1. This is the cadential I⁶₄ — the tonic chord in second inversion, but functioning as a decorated dominant. Let it ring for one full beat. Your ear should feel tension increase, not release.

Step 4 — V⁷ (bar 59, beat 2)

On beat 2 of bar 59: upper voice moves to G4 — the 7th of A major. Bass stays on A1. G4 is now loaded: it must sigh down to F4 on the next beat. If you have C#4 anywhere in the inner voices, it is loaded in the other direction, ready to pull up to D4.

Step 5 — Tonic arrival (bar 60, beat 1)

On beat 1 of bar 60: upper voice resolves G4 → F4 (the 7th sigh). Any C#4 in inner voices resolves C#4 → D4 (the leading tone pull). Bass leaps to D1 (up a fourth). The piece is now in D minor.

Optional — Picardy Third

In bar 60, change the final F to F#. The piece ends on D major instead of D minor — the raised third brightens the arrival and signals finality. After 60 bars of D minor, that F# lands like a shaft of light through cloud.

Listen to bars 58–60 in isolation. The sequence should feel like a spring coiling (It⁶), tightening one notch further (I⁶₄), snapping open (V⁷), and landing (i). If any step feels loose, verify that Bb moves down to A and G# moves up to A — the contrary-motion arrival of the outer shell is the engine of the whole thing.
