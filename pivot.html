The Core Insight

Every chord in a major key belongs simultaneously to multiple keys. Harmonic motion is the art of choosing which interpretation to lean into — and confirming it with a secondary dominant. Bach's Prelude in C demonstrates every essential move in 35 measures of pure arpeggiation.

The tonic sits between two neighbors on the circle of fifths. It is the dominant of the subdominant (C dominates F), and the subdominant of the dominant (C is IV in G). This symmetry is the engine of all tonal motion.

The Three Relationships Defined

Term Definition In C Major
Subdominant The chord the tonic is dominant of F — because C is V of F
Secondary Dominant The dominant of the current key's dominant D7 — because D7 is V of G
Pivot Chord A chord diatonic to two keys used as a hinge between them Am = vi in C, ii in G

The Pivot Chord — A Door Between Keys

A pivot chord exists naturally in two keys at once. Rather than lurching into a new tonal area, you find the overlap and lean into the new interpretation — then confirm it with a secondary dominant.

Tonicizing the Dominant (toward G)

The vi chord (Am in C major) is simultaneously ii in G major. Because C and G share all notes except one (F vs F#), Am is fully diatonic to both keys.

C major: I → vi → V/V → V C Am D7 G G major: ii → V7 → I ↑ pivot lives here — same chord, new role

The F# in D7 is the moment of commitment. Before it, Am is ambiguous. After it, G is confirmed as a local tonic. One raised note does all the work.

Tonicizing the Subdominant (toward F)

The ii chord (Dm in C major) is simultaneously vi in F major. This points the opposite direction — flat-ward, toward the subdominant.

C major: I → ii → V/IV → IV C Dm C7 F F major: vi → V7 → I ↑ same pivot logic, opposite direction

The vi chord is always the door to the dominant. The ii chord is always the door to the subdominant. They are mirror images across the tonic.

Secondary Dominants & Dominant Chains

A secondary dominant is the dominant of the dominant — the chord that most strongly pulls toward V, rather than toward I. The logic can be stacked indefinitely:

V/V/V → V/V → V → I A7 → D7 → G7 → C Each chord is dominant of the next. Each arrow points one step along the circle of fifths.

This is the circle of fifths made audible as motion. Jazz musicians call extended versions of this chain secondary dominant substitutions. Bach encodes shorter versions throughout his chorales and inventions as a primary tool for generating momentum.

Tritone Substitution — The Half-Step Approach

Any dominant 7th chord can be replaced by the dominant 7th chord a tritone away. The substitution works because both chords contain the same tritone — just with the two notes swapped in their roles:

G7: B (3rd) → F (7th) tritone Db7: F (3rd) → B (7th) same tritone, swapped Both resolve into C with identical voice leading. But Db7 → C moves the bass by half step — chromatic, smooth. G7 → C : bass leaps a fifth (functional) Db7 → C : bass slides a half step (jazz)

The Character of the Subdominant

The dominant creates tension by pulling forward. The subdominant creates something different — warmth, space, an exhale. This emotional asymmetry is structural:

Sharp-ward (dominant) → tension, urgency, forward lean Flat-ward (subdominant) → warmth, space, memory, settling

The subdominant rarely resolves directly to I with the force of V → I. The plagal cadence (IV → I, the "Amen" cadence) feels conclusory rather than dramatic. This is why subdominant motion typically appears after dominant tension — as counterbalance, not climax.

Crucially, by touching both neighbors, Bach recenters the tonic. If he only tonicized G, the piece might feel like it wants to live there. The swing through F confirms that C is the actual gravitational center — neither neighbor can claim it.

Tonicizing the dominant asks a question. Tonicizing the subdominant answers it — but from below, so the final return to I feels like the only place left to land.

Bach's Structural Arc in the Prelude

mm. 1–4 I → ii7 → V7 → I cadence stated mm. 5–11 vi → V/V → V tonicize dominant (sharp-ward) m. 12 dim7 maximum chromatic tension mm. 13–17 IV → ii → V → I subdominant approach → home

This is sonata form in miniature — establish home, move toward the dominant, return via the subdominant — encoded 30 years before Haydn and Mozart formalized it. The Prelude is a thesis statement about how tonal gravity works.
