D minor has a standard harmonic toolkit — but every great composer reaches past it. The chords in this chapter are deliberate choices that darken, brighten, or suspend the expected. Used surgically, they transform functional harmony into something expressive. Used carelessly, they sound like accidents. Learn to hear the difference.

The raised 7th degree — C# in D minor — exerts one of the most powerful gravitational forces in tonal music. A half step below the tonic, it wants to resolve upward to D. This is why harmonic minor exists: the natural 7th (C♮) resolves weakly over a whole step; raising it to C# sharpens the pull to a near-irresistible lean toward tonic.

When you use the V chord in D minor, you automatically get the leading tone: A major (A–C#–E) contains C#, which is what gives the dominant its energy. Always resolve C# to D in the following beat or bar. Leaving it unresolved is possible — for ambiguity or suspension — but it should be a deliberate choice.

Whole step vs. half step resolution to D

Raise the 4th scale degree — G becomes G# over a D chord — and you briefly enter Lydian territory. The tritone above the root (D to G# spans 6 semitones) creates an ethereal shimmer: suspended, otherworldly, the sound of film scores and ambient electronics. Tigran Hamasyan uses it. Esbjörn Svensson built careers on it.

The rule is: don't stay long. The ♯11 works as a fleeting color on a weak beat or as a chromatic passing tone. Sustain it too long and it sounds like you've modulated. In ambient music, you can hold it longer — but it must resolve eventually. Land it on beat 2 or 3, let it shimmer, then step down to G♮ or up to A.

Deployment rule

Place G# in a high register on a weak beat, in the melodic voice only. Resolve it down by half step (G# → G♮) or up by half step (G# → A). Never double it in another voice. Never put it in the bass.

In practice

Add a single G# to the upper voice on beat 2 or 3 of a sustained Dm chord. Two beats later, step down to G♮. This is one of the most effective "make it sound modern" gestures available — and it has historical precedent in Lydian mode church music and modal jazz.

The Neapolitan chord (♭II) is a major triad built on the flattened 2nd scale degree. In D minor: E♭ major (E♭–G–B♭). It functions as a supercharged subdominant — darker than iv (G minor), more foreign-sounding, and it resolves with a satisfying drop to the dominant.

Bach used it most often in first inversion (♭II⁶, the "Neapolitan sixth"), with G in the bass — the bass voice ascends smoothly G → A into the dominant. In ambient productions, root position works well for a heavier, more dramatic arrival. The characteristic cadential move is Dm → E♭ → A → Dm: the E♭ major chord materializes from "elsewhere," and the pull toward tonic overwhelms it.

The Neapolitan cadential move

The augmented sixth chords are the most dramatically chromatic pre-dominants in the tonal toolkit. All three varieties share one essential feature: the augmented sixth interval between B♭ and G# (10 semitones). This dissonant outer span resolves by contrary motion — B♭ steps down a half step to A while G# pulls up a half step to A — driving irresistibly into the dominant.

In D minor, the three variants differ only in their inner voices. The Italian is the leanest (three notes); the French adds E for a dominant-7th flavor; the German adds F for maximum richness. All three resolve to the dominant A.

Three voicings — one resolution
Which to use when

Italian: lightest, most transparent — good in sparse two-voice textures. French: the jazziest — the added E gives it a dominant-7th flavor. German: the richest and most lush — best for climactic pre-cadential moments. Resolve the German through A in second inversion (A⁶₄) to avoid parallel fifths, then to root-position A.

Modal mixture is the practice of borrowing chords from the parallel key — using D major chords in a D minor piece, or D minor chords in a D major piece. The borrowed chords carry the emotional color of the other mode: major chords feel like "borrowed light," minor chords like "borrowed darkness."

In a D minor piece, the most useful loans from D major are chords with B♮ (brighter) or F# (brighter) in place of B♭ or F♮. In a D major piece, borrowing from D minor means swapping in G minor (iv) instead of G major (IV), or reaching for B♭ major (♭VI) — immediately bluesier, darker, more restless.

The mixture palette in D

End a minor-key piece on a major tonic and you've used the Picardy third — the raised 3rd that transforms D minor (D–F–A) into D major (D–F#–A) at the final cadence. Bach used this constantly. It reads as resolution into light after a journey through darkness: the piece fought its way home, and the arrival is brighter than expected.

In ambient production, the Picardy third is an extraordinarily effective closing gesture. After bars of D minor, let your final chord land on D major. The F# — the raised third that was foreign to D minor throughout — now stays in the tonic. What was restless becomes still. The minor feeling dissolves.

D minor vs. D major at the final chord
The Picardy third says: the journey is over. Whatever darkness the piece moved through, it arrives in light. Use it for endings that feel earned — not as a throwaway gesture, but as the final word.

Open your Ch 05 session — three MIDI tracks with the two-voice counterpoint and bass. This exercise adds three harmonic color events to the existing 4-bar piece.

Step 1 — Neapolitan before the cadence

In bar 3 (currently C or B♭ harmony), replace beat 3 with an E♭ major chord: add E♭ and G to the upper voice and E♭ in the bass. On beat 4, move to the dominant — A in the bass, C# in the upper voice. The cadence in bar 4 now arrives with far more weight because it was earned through the Neapolitan drop.

Step 2 — Modal mixture in bar 2

Bar 2 currently has G minor or C major harmony. Borrow a chord from D major: on beat 1 of bar 2, use G major instead of G minor — play B♮ instead of B♭ in the upper voice. Just one note changes, but the sound briefly brightens. On beat 3, return to B♭ as the harmony continues. This is "borrowed light" dropping into D minor's natural color.

Step 3 — Picardy third at the end

In bar 4, change the final tonic chord from D minor to D major: raise F♮ to F# in the upper voice and in any doubled chord tones. Compare the ending with F♮ and with F#: switch it off, listen, then switch it on. The difference is stark — the piece arrives in light instead of minor resignation.

Your session now has a 4-bar D minor piece with a ♭II Neapolitan approach to the cadence, one beat of borrowed major IV for brightness, and a Picardy third ending. These three gestures cover the harmonic color vocabulary of the entire baroque style in four bars.
