Everything in this series — leap versus step, antecedent and consequent, chromatic color chords, inner-voice dialogue, tension and cadential release — is abstract until you see it working in a real piece. Schubert's Minuet D334 packs all of it into sixteen bars. We move through the A section phrase by phrase, naming each technique as it appears and pointing back to where you've already studied it in depth.

Schubert Minuet D334 is an early piano minuet in A major, 3/4 time, Allegretto — elegant, dance-like, brisk without rushing. The A section runs sixteen bars to the first repeat and divides cleanly into two eight-bar phrases. The B section (after the repeat) modulates and returns; we focus on the A section, where the core techniques are concentrated.

Form: Parallel Period

The A section is built as a parallel period: two phrases that open with identical or near-identical material but close differently. The first phrase is the antecedent — it ends unresolved on a half cadence (V), leaving the ear suspended. The second phrase is the consequent — it recycles the same opening idea, then closes with a perfect authentic cadence (V→I), completing the thought.

This antecedent-consequent pairing is the backbone of Classical phrase writing. It's built on the same call-and-response logic as the Vordersatz-Epilog structure from Ch 07, scaled down to eight bars each. Bach would recognise the architecture immediately.

Two Motifs: x and y

The entire melody grows from two small ideas. Motif x is an expressive upward leap — bold, open, directional. It launches each phrase on the pickup beat. Motif y is a falling stepwise figure — smooth, graceful, descending through three or four steps after the leap. Together they give the melody its characteristic shape: surge upward, then pour back down.

This is the leap-step contour from Ch 01 — the same logic that drives Bach's single-line writing. The leap (motif x) injects energy and direction; the stepwise fall (motif y) releases it smoothly. Schubert recycles both across the consequent phrase, providing unity while the harmony shifts underneath.

The antecedent phrase is kept harmonically direct — the accompaniment is described as simple and unobtrusive, never competing with the melody. The bass moves by step or by root motion (4th/5th leaps), and the inner parts fill in chord tones quietly. Eight bars, tonic to dominant.

I → V43 → I6 → I: The Breathing Tonic

The phrase opens on I (A major: A–C#–E). The first harmonic move is to V43 — the dominant seventh (E7) in second inversion, with B in the bass. The bass line descends: A→B is actually an ascent, but V43 is often used as a passing chord where the bass moves stepwise, here B moving to C# → A. The phrase immediately returns via I6 (first-inversion tonic, C# in bass) back to root-position I.

The effect is a brief excursion that returns to tonic within two or three bars — the harmony breathes out and back in. I6 (first inversion) is the key tool here, from Ch 05: it keeps the bass moving stepwise rather than leaping, maintaining momentum. Bach uses I6 constantly in his descending bass lines for exactly this reason.

V7 → I → viiø7/V → V: Toward the Half Cadence

The phrase intensifies through a second V7 → I exchange, then arrives at its most dramatic moment: viiø7/V. This is a half-diminished seventh chord built on the leading tone of the dominant. In A major, V = E major; the leading tone of E is D#. So viiø7/V = D#–F##–A–C# (F## = enharmonic G♮). The F## is chromatic to A major and creates a fierce pull toward E, which arrives immediately.

Schubert marks this chord fz (sforzando — a sudden, sharp accent) to underscore the surprise. This is the chromatic leading-tone seventh from Ch 06, applied to a secondary target: instead of pulling to I, it pulls to V. The same logic as viiø7 → I, shifted up a fifth. The moment is brief but arresting — exactly the "harmonic color" technique of Ch 06, placed strategically at the phrase's highest tension point.

The phrase closes on V (E major) — the half cadence. The ear hangs in suspension. The antecedent has asked its question; only the consequent can answer it.

The consequent phrase opens with the same motifs as the antecedent — but Schubert immediately introduces a new harmonic colour. The phrase uses two techniques not seen in the antecedent: a secondary dominant that briefly tonicizes a new chord, and a cadential six-four that suspends the final resolution before releasing it.

V43/ii: A Colourful Secondary Dominant

The consequent opens on V43/ii — the dominant seventh of the supertonic (ii = B minor in A major), in second inversion. In A major, V of B minor = F# major. The full F#7 chord is F#–A#–C#–E. In second inversion, C# sits in the bass with E, F#, and A# above. That A# — a semitone above A natural — is chromatic to A major and gives the chord its striking colour. Your ear briefly hears it pulling toward B minor.

This is tonicization from Ch 05: one V–i move in a secondary key, then immediately out. V43/ii resolves to ii (B minor), treating that chord as a temporary home for a single beat before the progression pulls back to A major. The result is a momentary harmonic surprise — the same opening material sounds completely different over this chord. Schubert gets maximum effect from a single chromatic note.

Reply in the Tenor

During the V7 bar in the consequent, the tenor voice — an inner voice in the bass staff — echoes motif x from the melody: the same upward leap, in the same rhythm, a third or sixth lower. The melody stated its idea eight bars ago; now an inner voice picks it up. This is the simplest form of imitation — not a strict canon, but a one-bar voice dialogue.

The effect is a brief opening of the texture: for one bar the piece feels like two voices speaking rather than melody plus backdrop. This is exactly the contrapuntal independence of Ch 03 and the proto-imitation of Ch 12. Schubert keeps it short — one bar of response, then the texture returns to its supporting role — but it's enough to make the consequent feel richer than the antecedent.

I6/4 → V7 → I: The Cadential Six-Four

The phrase culminates in the most powerful tension-release move in tonal music. Before resolving to I, Schubert inserts a cadential six-four — the tonic chord in second inversion (A/E: E in the bass, A a perfect fourth above, C# a major sixth above). This chord looks like tonic but sounds like dominant: the bass sits on E (the dominant pitch) while the upper voices hold A and C#, which are dissonant against the bass and must resolve downward.

A falls to G# and C# stays as V7 forms (E–G#–B–D). Then the leading tone G# rises to A and D resolves down to C# as the phrase lands on I. The resolution is total — every voice moves into the tonic chord by half step or whole step, with no loose ends. This is the suspension logic of Ch 04 operating at the phrase level: prepare the dissonance (I6/4), clash (V7), resolve (I).

The cadential six-four works by occupying the same bass note as V while holding the tonic chord's upper pitches. The listener expects I — they hear I's notes — but the bass says V. The resolution of A→G# (upper voice falls) and the bass staying on E into full V7 is the moment the dissonance crystallises. Then V7→I completes the cadence. The ear has been stretched and released in the space of two bars. See Ch 04 for the full suspension anatomy.

The accompaniment in D334 is deliberately restrained. The inner voices never try to be interesting on their own terms — except for the one bar of tenor reply, they exist purely to support the melody with chord tones and smooth bass motion. This restraint is itself a technique.

Contrary Motion at Every Cadence

At each V→I resolution, watch the outer voices: the bass moves downward (E→A, dominant root to tonic root) while the soprano moves upward or inward into the tonic chord. These two outer voices converge from opposite directions, creating the sense of arrival that makes a cadence feel inevitable. This is contrary motion at the cadence point, the most basic rule from Ch 03 — and it works identically in Bach's chorales, in Classical sonatas, and in Schubert's minuets.

The contrary motion is not a special effect — it's the default. When both voices move in the same direction into a cadence, the arrival feels coincidental. When they converge, it feels inevitable. Same technique, same logic, three centuries of composers.

Unobtrusive Accompaniment as a Compositional Choice

The inner voices in D334 play block chords — no independent melodic lines, no rhythmic interest, no competing motion. This is not laziness; it is strategic restraint. A melody this lyrical and directional needs acoustic space around it. The accompaniment's job is to make the chord structure audible without drawing attention to itself.

Bach knew this in his slower movements and aria accompaniments: when the melody carries the structural weight, inner voices provide harmonic clarity without crowding the texture. Dense accompaniment texture would dilute the expressive power of motifs x and y. The restraint is the craft. See Ch 10 on space and breathing.

Smooth Inner Voice Motion

Even while staying quiet, the inner voices move by step wherever possible — no leaps, no awkward jumps. When the bass moves by fourth or fifth (root motion), the inner parts compensate with stepwise or common-tone movement, maintaining smooth voice leading throughout. This is the Ch 03 principle of voice independence: each voice has its own smooth melodic logic, even if that logic is simply "move as little as possible." The V43 → I6 bass motion (B→C#→A) is exactly this: the bass keeps moving stepwise instead of leaping, keeping the texture fluid even as harmonies change.

D334's A section is sixteen bars. Here is the complete inventory of techniques — where each one appears in this piece, and where it appeared earlier in this series.

Melody and Phrase Architecture

Ch 01 — Single Line: Motif x is a leap; motif y is stepwise fall. The 70/30 ratio (mostly steps, some leaps) holds exactly. The pickup note (anacrusis) launches the phrase on an upbeat. Contour shape: leap up, pour down — a compact arch.

Ch 07 — The Sentence: The parallel period is the Classical equivalent of the Baroque sentence. Antecedent = the Vordersatz's opening statement. Half cadence = the hinge point. Consequent = the Epilog that completes the resolution. The structural logic is identical; only the surface style differs between Bach and Schubert.

Ch 08 — Motivic Development: The consequent phrase reuses motifs x and y over different harmony. Same material, new harmonic context — the simplest form of development. No new notes required; just change what's underneath.

Harmony and Chromaticism

Ch 05 — Harmonic Foundations: I6 keeps the bass stepwise. V7→I is the structural spine. The ii chord in the consequent is the pre-dominant gathering energy for V. First-inversion chords are the smoothing mechanism throughout.

Ch 05 — Tonicization: V43/ii treats B minor as a local tonic for one bar. One V–i move in the secondary key, no commitment, immediately out. The A# in that chord is the chromatic fingerprint that signals "we are briefly elsewhere."

Ch 06 — Harmonic Color: viiø7/V (D#ø7) is the chromatic leading-tone seventh of the dominant — a close relative of the augmented sixth chords in Ch 06. The fz accent is Schubert telling the performer: this chord is supposed to surprise you. The A# in V43/ii is modal color from outside the key signature. Both serve exactly the same function as Ch 06's color chords: momentary disorientation before snapping back into focus.

Voice Leading and Texture

Ch 03 — Two Voices: Contrary motion at cadences. Smooth inner voices. Tenor reply as one-bar imitation — voice exchange and dialogue in miniature.

Ch 04 — Tension and Release: The cadential six-four (I6/4) is the central tension device of this chapter's vocabulary. The 4th above the bass (A over E) creates a dissonance that must resolve to the 3rd (G#). The resolution — I6/4 → V7 → I — is the most structurally powerful tension-release in tonal music.

Ch 10 — Space and Breathing: The unobtrusive accompaniment gives the melody room. Restraint in the inner voices is not absence — it is control of density. The one moment of textural richness (tenor reply) stands out precisely because everything else is so quiet.

Ch 12 — Canons and Rounds: The tenor reply is proto-imitation: melody states a shape, inner voice echoes it one or two beats later. Even one bar of imitation creates the sensation of contrapuntal dialogue. A full canon is just this moment extended and made structural.

A great short piece does not need to introduce new techniques — it needs to place familiar ones with perfect timing. The viiø7/V lands just as the ear has settled into routine, right before the half cadence. The V43/ii opens the consequent with the same motifs but freshly coloured harmony. The cadential six-four suspends the resolution the listener has been expecting for eight bars. Every technique is positioned where it will register: not too early, not buried, not piled on top of another surprise. The craft is in the placement.

The session is now 44 bars. Add a parallel period as bars 45–60, applying D334's phrase architecture to your D minor material. Antecedent: bars 45–52. Consequent: bars 53–60.

Bars 45–52: Antecedent (half cadence on V)

Open bar 45 on the pickup beat with a single note — your motif x equivalent. Choose a note from the tonic chord (D, F, or A) and leap up a 5th or 6th on beat 1 of bar 46. Let motif y fall back stepwise over bars 46–47.

Harmonise bars 45–50 with: i → V43 → i6 → i → V7 → i. In D minor: i = D minor (D–F–A), V43 = A7/E (E in bass, G–A–C# above), i6 = Dm/F (F in bass). Keep the accompanying track chords simple — beats 1 and 2, no independent motion.

Bars 50–52: push toward the half cadence on A (V). Add viiø7/V — in D minor, V = A major, so viiø7/V = G#ø7 (G#–B–D–F). Write it as a chord on beat 1 of bar 51, velocity 110 (your fz). Resolve to A major on bar 52. End the antecedent there — leave it open.

Bars 53–60: Consequent (secondary dominant → perfect cadence)

Open bar 53 with motif x and y again — same shape. But this time harmonise bar 53 with V43/iv: in D minor, iv = G minor; V of G minor = D major; D7 in second inversion = A in the bass (D7/A: A–C–D–F#). The F# is your chromatic colour note — it isn't in D minor's natural scale. Let the listener feel it.

Bar 54: resolve to iv (G minor: G–B♭–D). Then bars 55–56: V7 (A7). During this bar, add a tenor reply: have an existing inner track play the motif x leap — just two notes, the leap up — at a lower octave. One bar of imitation is enough.

Bar 57: cadential six-four — i6/4 in D minor: A in the bass (V scale degree), D a 4th above, F a 6th above. Hold it a full beat — feel the suspension. Bar 58: V7 (A7), with G# in the melody resolving the tension. Bars 59–60: i (D minor) — the perfect cadence. The question asked in bar 52 is finally answered.
