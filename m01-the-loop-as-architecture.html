It's structure. In Bach, a phrase is a journey — it starts somewhere, develops, and arrives at a cadence. In minimalism, the loop is the destination. There is no development arc, no cadential goal, no sense that the music needs to get anywhere. Instead, the interest comes from what repeats, how it repeats, and what tiny changes accumulate over time. This chapter is about treating the loop not as a convenience but as the fundamental architectural unit — the way Bach treated the phrase.

Bach's compositional unit is the phrase — a musical sentence with a beginning, a middle, and a cadential end. Each phrase points forward; the cadence resolves something and opens the next phrase. The music is directional. You always feel that something is being worked out.

The minimalist's unit is the loop. A loop has no beginning or end by design — it cycles. Instead of pointing forward, it folds back on itself. The listener isn't waiting for a resolution; they're settling into a pattern. The music is centripetal rather than centrifugal.

This is a fundamental shift in compositional thinking. When you write a loop, you're not asking "where does this go?" — you're asking "what is worth repeating, and for how long?" The loop doesn't lead to the next idea. It is the idea.

The key contrast

In a Bach phrase, variety is structural — the Fortspinnung exists to transform the opening idea. In a loop, variety is perceptual. The same notes heard the second time carry a different weight than the first time. Repetition creates expectation, and expectation creates the conditions for surprise.

The phrase points outward; the loop folds inward. Both are valid compositional strategies — but they require completely different instincts when you're writing.

Loop length changes how the music feels, even when the notes are identical. A 1-bar loop feels like wallpaper — it's so short and so repetitive that the ear stops tracking it and treats it as texture. A 4-bar loop feels like a riff — enough content that the ear follows it consciously but familiar enough to feel inevitable. An 8-bar loop feels like a section, carrying structural weight.

This is the minimalist's primary formal tool: not modulation, not development, not contrast — just loop length. Changing the length of a loop changes its role in the hierarchy of the piece.

The density illusion

The same four-note cell placed in a 1-bar loop versus a 4-bar loop creates a very different perception of density. In the 1-bar version, the notes seem packed together — even though the second half of the bar is empty silence. In the 4-bar version, the same notes float in space. Loop length controls perceived density independently of note count.

Length rule of thumb

1 bar = texture. 2–3 bars = motif. 4 bars = riff. 8 bars = section. When you're unsure of your loop's role, try doubling or halving its length and see which frame it fits into.

Most loops fail the 16-repetition test — they sound fine the first two times, then become either boring or irritating. The difference between a loop that sustains interest and one that doesn't comes down to internal structure.

A good loop has two competing qualities in tension: enough repetition within the loop to feel inevitable (so the ear locks on and gets comfortable), and enough asymmetry to stay interesting (so the ear keeps tracking it). Pure repetition is a metronome. Pure irregularity is noise. The sweet spot is a pattern the ear can predict on the macro level but not entirely at the micro level.

The lock-on threshold

For a loop to "click" perceptually, the listener needs about 2–4 repetitions to internalize the pattern. Before that threshold, they're still parsing the material. After it, they've locked on — and now any deviation, however small, will register as interesting. Design your loop to be lockable. Short cells, clear rhythm, predictable pitch contour. Then exploit the lock-on state for variation.

Internal asymmetry

Compare: four equal 8th notes (D4–F4–G4–A4) versus a dotted 8th, a 16th, a rest, and a quarter. Both are one bar long with the same pitches. The second has rhythmic shape — a direction, a lean, a place where it lands. The ear needs that shape to follow the loop actively rather than filing it away as background. Pure rhythmic evenness is the enemy of conscious attention.

The design question — before asking "which notes?" ask "does this have a shape?" A loop needs a perceptible center of gravity — a moment where it lands, even briefly. Without that landing point, the loop is formless and quickly tuned out.

When a loop stops feeling interesting, the instinct is to change the notes. Resist this. Before you touch pitch or rhythm, exhaust the two dimensions that are invisible in the MIDI: timbre and velocity.

A loop played with flat, metronomic velocities sounds mechanical even if it's a beautiful cell. Introduce a velocity curve — accents on beats 1 and 3, softer subdivisions — and suddenly the same loop feels human and alive. The notes haven't changed. The musical feel has completely changed.

Timbre over time

Glass and Riley compositions often evolve by opening a filter, brightening an envelope, or shifting between registers — the notes are constant, but the sound transforms. In Ableton: automate the filter cutoff over 8 bars, or use an LFO on the attack time. The listener hears development even though you're playing the same loop. This is timbral variation as form.

Velocity as a third voice

In Bach, a third voice would require a third melodic line. In a loop-based context, a carefully shaped velocity curve can imply a third dimension of interest without adding any notes. Try this: on a 4-note 8th-note cell, set velocities to 90–55–70–45. Run it for 16 bars. The accented notes form a ghost melody above the pitch content. See ch11 of the Bach series for the velocity-as-voice technique used in ambient counterpoint.

The order of operations

1. Get the cell. 2. Shape the velocity. 3. Shape the timbre (filter, envelope). 4. Then — only then — consider changing a note. Most loops that feel finished after step 1 are actually unfinished; the interesting work is in steps 2 and 3.

A Baroque phrase has a clear beginning and a clear cadential end. A loop, by definition, has no beginning and no end — it cycles. But there is still a seam: the invisible point where the last beat of the loop stitches back to the first beat. A poorly designed seam announces itself as a seam. A well-designed seam is inaudible.

The seam problem has two components: rhythmic (does the last note's duration lead naturally into the first beat?) and melodic (does the pitch of the last note connect with the pitch of the first note?). Both need to work for the loop to feel seamless.

Rhythmic seam

If your cell ends with a short note followed by silence, the loop point falls in the middle of that silence. The listener may hear the restart as a rhythmic hiccup — a brief stutter where the pattern resumes. Solutions: end the cell on a note that sustains to the loop point (no gap), or design the silence before the loop point to be musically meaningful (a rest that functions as anticipation). Silence that leans forward is different from silence that just sits there.

Melodic seam

The last pitch of the loop connects back to the first pitch. If that connection is a large leap, the seam is audible — the listener hears an abrupt jump rather than a continuous line. The smoothest seams end the cell on a note close to the first pitch: ideally a step away, so the loop sounds like it was already in motion when you arrived.

A seamless loop doesn't sound like it starts — it sounds like it's already happening when you tune in. The difference is entirely in how the last beat points to the first.

Session setup

Open Ableton. Set tempo to 100 BPM, 4/4. Create a new MIDI track and assign a pad or electric keys sound — something with a medium attack and gentle release. This is your working session for the whole series; save it now.

Step 1 — Enter the seed cell

Create a new 1-bar MIDI clip. Enter four 8th notes starting on beat 1: D4 – F4 – G4 – A4. Leave beats 3 and 4 empty (8 eighth-note slots; fill only the first four). Set all velocities to 80. Loop the clip and listen.

Step 2 — Test the loop length

Duplicate the clip and extend it to 2 bars. In bar 2, move the cell so it starts on beat 2 instead of beat 1 (shift all four notes right by one quarter note). Loop the 2-bar version. Listen to both versions for at least 16 repetitions each. Notice: the 1-bar version feels compressed; the 2-bar version has more space and the rhythmic offset creates a forward lean.

Step 3 — Choose your length

Settle on whichever length feels like something rather than nothing — the one where your ear keeps tracking it after 8 repetitions. Label the clip "M01 seed" and save. You'll build the rest of the series on top of this session.

What you have: A 1- or 2-bar loop in D Dorian — D4, F4, G4, A4 — at 100 BPM. The cell sits in the upper octave. The second half of the bar (or bars) is open. This open space is the most important thing you built today; future chapters will fill it deliberately.
