CHAPTER 09 ◇ HOCKET ◇ INTERLOCKING VOICES ◇ CHAPTER 09 ◇ HOCKET ◇ INTERLOCKING VOICES ◇ Hocket: Interlocking Voices Two tracks, one melody — together they become more than either. Hocket is the art of splitting a single melody between two voices so that neither plays it complete. Notes alternate: voice A plays the odd ones, voice B plays the even ones. The result is density from sparseness — a medieval contrapuntal trick that sits at the heart of Reich's minimalism.
The Medieval-Modern Technique

The word hocket (from French hoquet, hiccup) names a technique as old as the 13th century: two voices share a single melody by taking turns, one resting when the other sounds. Neither voice plays the whole melody alone. Together they interlock perfectly — notes dovetailed so that every beat is covered, every gap filled by the other voice.

Reich rediscovered this logic and made it central to minimalism. His piano, marimba, and keyboard works often split melodic material across two players who interlock like gears. The technique scales naturally to two Ableton tracks.

Why Hocket Sounds Different from Unison

When both voices play the same melody in unison, the ear hears one thing. When they hocket — alternating notes, different timbres — the ear hears two independent streams and the combined melody at the same time. The split creates a perceptual doubling that unison cannot. This is density produced by subtraction, not addition.

The effect is strongest when the two voices have distinct timbres: pad vs. plucked, bell vs. bass, breath vs. string. The timbre contrast lets the ear track each stream separately while it reconstructs the whole.
Splitting the MIDI Clip

The mechanical process is simple. Take any MIDI clip containing a looped melody. Duplicate the track. Rename them Hocket A and Hocket B. In Hocket A's clip, delete every even-numbered note (2nd, 4th, 6th, …). In Hocket B's clip, delete every odd-numbered note (1st, 3rd, 5th, …). Both clips remain the same total length — they just each carry half the notes.

A few mechanical details to get right: both clips must have identical loop lengths so they stay in sync. Mute the original track. Play A and B together — together they should reproduce the original melody exactly, but now split across two timbres.

The Process
1. Duplicate the melody track
2. Rename → "Hocket A" and "Hocket B"
3. Hocket A: delete notes 2, 4, 6, 8 …
4. Hocket B: delete notes 1, 3, 5, 7 …
5. Assign different timbres to each track
6. Play both together
Register as a Hocket Variable

The simplest hocket keeps both voices at the same pitch register. But a more interesting variant shifts one voice up (or down) an octave. If Hocket A plays D4 and G4, and Hocket B plays F5 and A5 (an octave higher), the combined melody leaps between registers — exactly the compound melody and implied polyphony technique from the Bach series.

The ear now hears three things simultaneously: the low voice, the high voice, and the implied melody that stitches them together across the octave gap. One of the richest textures available from two MIDI tracks.

For the Bach counterpart — how register separation implies two independent voices from a single line — see Ch 02 — Implied Polyphony.

Velocity Hocket

There is a softer version that requires only one track. Keep all notes in a single MIDI clip but alternate velocities: odd notes at full velocity (90–100), even notes at ghost velocity (25–35). The loud notes pop as melody; the ghost notes drop into background accompaniment. The ear parses this as two layers even though it is one. This is the velocity hocket — useful when you want the effect without doubling your track count.

Hocket is the inverse of counterpoint: instead of adding a second independent voice, you split a single voice across two tracks. The independence is built in by subtraction.
Session State After M08

Your session has four active layers: the melody displacement arc (Disp-0 through Disp-3), Phase B, the M08 polyrhythm pattern, and the pulse. The melody displacement arc is the primary melodic voice.

This Session

Open your M08 session. Locate the main melody track — the one containing the Disp-0 clip (or whichever displacement clip is currently active as your primary melody). Mute it for now.

Step 1. Duplicate the melody track twice. Rename the two duplicates Hocket A and Hocket B. Delete the original melody track (or leave it muted — your choice).

Step 2. Open the MIDI clip in Hocket A. Delete every even-numbered note — 2nd, 4th, 6th, and so on through the loop. Open the MIDI clip in Hocket B and delete every odd-numbered note — 1st, 3rd, 5th. Both clips stay the same bar length.

Step 3. Assign different timbres: Hocket A gets the pad sound (soft, sustained), Hocket B gets a plucked or staccato sound (bell, kalimba, muted key). Play them together alongside Phase B and the pulse. The original melody is there — but now it shimmers between two voices.

Step 4 (optional but recommended). In Hocket B's MIDI clip, transpose all notes up one octave. Now the melody leaps between registers — the register-hocket compound melody from Card 43. Listen for the implied zigzag line the ear traces between the two voices.

When you are satisfied: save your session. You now have five active layers — Hocket A, Hocket B, Phase B, M08 poly, and pulse — each carved from the same D-minor material.
