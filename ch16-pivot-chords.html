The tonic doesn't stand alone. It's flanked by two neighbors on the circle of fifths, each exerting gravitational pull. Knowing how to pivot between keys using a shared chord, and how to amplify arrival with a secondary dominant, is what turns a chord progression into a directed journey with a sense of departure and return. Bach's Prelude in C Major encodes every essential move in 35 measures of pure arpeggiation — a thesis statement about how tonal gravity works, written 30 years before Haydn formalized sonata form.

Every tonic sits between two neighbors on the circle of fifths. In D minor, G minor is one step flat-ward (D is the dominant of G), and A is one step sharp-ward (A is the dominant of D). This isn't geography — it's a force diagram.

The tonic holds a double identity: it is simultaneously the dominant of the subdominant (D dominates G) and the subdominant of the dominant (D is IV in A major). Motion sharp-ward creates tension and forward momentum. Motion flat-ward creates warmth, space, a settling back. Both directions are available from any tonic at any time.

The circle as force diagram

Moving one step sharp-ward on the circle of fifths increases tension. Moving one step flat-ward releases it. Every tonal composition is a navigation between these two forces — departure into tension, return through release.

Three terms describe the moves that make harmonic navigation possible. Each is a different tool for crossing from one key to another.

Subdominant

The chord the tonic is dominant of. In D minor, Gm is the subdominant because D is the fifth of G. Subdominant motion moves flat-ward on the circle — toward warmth and memory. The plagal cadence (iv → i) is the most familiar gesture in this direction.

Secondary Dominant

The dominant of the dominant — the chord that most strongly pulls toward V rather than toward I. In D minor, E7 is the secondary dominant (V of V, or V/V): its active chromatic tone (G#) is the leading tone of A, pulling sharply toward the expected A arrival. You can stack this logic: B7 is V/V/V, the dominant of E7.

Pivot Chord

A chord that is diatonic to two keys simultaneously, used as a hinge between them. Rather than lurching into a new key, you find a chord that belongs to both the old and new keys, reinterpret it in the new context, and confirm the new key with a secondary dominant. In D minor / D major, Bm is a pivot: it is vi in D major and ii in A major — the door to the dominant.

The pivot chord is the smoothest possible key change because there is no actual "change" — you are already in both keys at once. The listener doesn't notice the modulation until the secondary dominant confirms the new tonic.

Tonicizing the Dominant (toward A)

In D major (the parallel major of our home key), Bm is vi. In A major, Bm is ii. Both keys share this chord with no alteration. You pivot on Bm, then confirm A with E7 — its dominant seventh. The G# in E7 is the moment of commitment: Bm has no G#, but E7 does — and G# is the leading tone of A, locking the ear into the new key.

The pivot lives entirely in the ambiguity between Dm and A major. Before the F# of E7 arrives, the listener is in both keys. The moment E7 sounds, the ear locks onto A as a local tonic.

Tonicizing the Subdominant (toward Gm)

The same logic runs in the opposite direction. Am is v in D minor and simultaneously ii in G minor — a chord that belongs to both keys with no alteration needed. Pivot on Am, then confirm Gm with D7 — its dominant seventh. The F# in D7 is the moment of commitment: F# is chromatic to both D minor and G minor, and its pull toward G locks the ear into the new key.

In major keys, vi is the door sharp-ward (to the dominant) and ii is the door flat-ward (to the subdominant) — exact mirror images. In minor, vi still opens the sharp-ward path (borrowed from parallel major). Flat-ward, look to the v chord: Am is v in D minor and ii in G minor, opening the same door by a different mechanism.

The pattern is consistent across keys. Sharp-ward, vi becomes ii in the dominant key. Flat-ward, ii becomes vi in the subdominant key — this works cleanly in major; in minor, use the v chord, which becomes ii in the subdominant key. Commit these shapes to muscle memory and you can pivot from any tonic.

A secondary dominant is the dominant of the dominant. The logic stacks: every chord in the circle of fifths can act as a dominant toward the next one sharp-ward. The result is a chain of consecutive dominant sevenths, each resolving into the next, building momentum with each link.

Each arrow crosses one fifth. B7 → E7 → A7 → Dm traces three steps along the circle, each step sharpening the tension. Jazz musicians call extended chains "secondary dominant substitutions." Bach uses shorter two-link versions throughout his chorales as a primary tool for generating forward momentum.

How far back to start

One secondary dominant (E7 before A7) adds urgency to the dominant arrival. Two (B7 → E7 → A7) creates a longer runway — useful for phrase climaxes. Three or more starts to feel like a sequence and takes on its own formal weight. One is surgical; three is a structural event.

Every link needs a leading tone

The D# in B7 resolves up to E. The G# in E7 resolves up to A. The C# in A7 resolves up to D. Each raised seventh adds one sharp to the temporary key — making the arrival feel earned from further away. When you stack three, you've crossed three temporary keys in a single gesture.

Any dominant seventh chord can be replaced by the dominant seventh chord a tritone (six semitones) away. The substitution works because both chords share the same tritone — the tension-bearing interval that defines dominant sevenths — with the two voices that form it simply swapping roles.

In D minor, A7 is the standard dominant. Eb7 is its tritone substitute — the bass moves by half step (Eb → D) instead of leaping a fifth (A → D). Both chords contain the same C#/G tritone; the voices that form it simply trade assignments. The resolution is identical in voice-leading terms; only the bass motion changes, from the functional leap to the chromatic slide.

Where to use it

Tritone substitution is a jazz and modern harmonic technique — it sounds anachronistic in strict Baroque style. But in ambient and electronic music it creates a silky half-step arrival that feels inevitable. Use it where a standard V → i would feel too blunt: cadences after long dominant pedals, inner-section arrivals, any moment where you want the resolution to feel inevitable rather than forceful.

The dominant creates tension by pulling forward. The subdominant creates something structurally different — warmth, space, an exhale. This emotional asymmetry is not subjective: it's structural. The dominant wants to resolve urgently. The subdominant wants to rest.

The subdominant rarely resolves directly to I with the force of V → i. The plagal cadence (iv → i, the "Amen" cadence) feels conclusory rather than dramatic — an exhale, not a climax. This is why subdominant motion typically appears after dominant tension: as counterbalance, not as the main event.

Crucially, touching both neighbors recenters the tonic. If a piece only tonicizes A, it risks feeling like the music wants to live in A permanently. Swinging through G minor and back confirms that D is the actual gravitational center — neither neighbor can claim it as a satellite. The final return to Dm feels like the only possible landing place.

Bach's structural deployment

In the Prelude in C Major, Bach moves sharp-ward first (toward G major, bars 5–11), then arrives at maximum chromatic tension (a diminished seventh, bar 12), then swings flat-ward through F major before returning home. The sharp-ward departure asks a question. The flat-ward approach answers it — from below, so that the final return to C feels inescapable. This is miniature sonata form, encoded 30 years before Haydn formalized it.

Tonicizing the dominant asks a question. Tonicizing the subdominant answers it — but from below, so the final return to i feels like the only place left to land.

The Prelude in C Major (WTC Book I) demonstrates every concept in this chapter in 35 measures of pure arpeggiation — no melody, no counterpoint, just harmonic rhythm. Its structure is the thesis statement of tonal music.

This is the tonal bracket: by touching both the dominant (sharp-ward) and the subdominant (flat-ward) before returning home, Bach confirms that C is the gravitational center — not G, not F. The same architecture governs a Bach chorale, a Haydn sonata, a jazz standard, and a 16-bar phrase in Ableton. The scale changes; the logic doesn't.

Applying the arc in D minor

In D minor: establish Dm (bars 1–4). Move toward A (secondary dominant chain, bars 5–8). Hit a diminished or augmented sixth at peak tension (bar 9). Return via Gm (flat-ward approach, bars 10–12). Land on Dm (bar 13). The bracket is complete. Both neighbors have been touched; the tonic has been recentered by triangulation.

Open your Ch15 Ableton session — the 46-bar arrangement (Hook → Verse → Pre-chorus → Chorus → Bridge → Final Chorus). You'll plant two harmonic pivots: a secondary dominant chain approaching the Chorus, and a subdominant tonicization inside the Bridge.

Move 1 — Secondary dominant into the Chorus

The Verse ends around bar 16 of your arrangement before the Pre-chorus begins. Find the last two bars of the Verse and edit the harmony as follows:

Second-to-last Verse bar, beat 1: Change the bass note to B (natural). In your upper voices, keep whatever D-minor material is there — Bm is vi in D, so no violent clash. This is the pivot: you're silently stepping into A major territory.

Second-to-last Verse bar, beat 3: Add an E in the bass and G# in an inner voice. This is E7 — the secondary dominant of A, V/V. The G# signals the shift: it's the leading tone of A, absent from D minor, and its arrival tells the ear that A is the incoming tonic.

Final Verse bar: Let A arrive as a local tonic (or A major chord). The Pre-chorus then pushes back toward Dm from this A arrival. You now have a ii → V7 → I secondary dominant approach (Bm → E7 → A) flowing into the Pre-chorus.

Move 2 — Subdominant tonicization in the Bridge

Your Bridge is already in F major (the Ch15 +3 semitone transposition). F major is D minor's relative major — but from a circle-of-fifths standpoint, F is also the subdominant side of C, which is the subtonic of D minor. You can reinforce the flat-ward feeling by briefly tonicizing Gm inside the Bridge.

Bridge bar 5 (roughly bar 38 of arrangement): Add a D7 chord (D–F#–A–C). D7 is the secondary dominant of Gm — V7 of iv. The F# is the chromatic clue that Gm is about to arrive.

Bridge bar 6: Resolve to Gm. One bar of Gm before the final two bars of dominant pedal. The subdominant has been tonicized; the flat-ward excursion is complete.

After both edits

Play the full arrangement from top. Notice: the Verse-to-Chorus transition now has a two-chord runway (Bm → E7) that sharpens the Chorus arrival. The Bridge now has a brief Gm tonicization that deepens the flat-ward character before the final return. The tonic has been recentered — you've touched both neighbors. Your session is now 46 bars with a miniature tonal bracket built in.
