Close voicings pack notes into a single octave, creating walls of sound. Open voicings spread those same notes across two or more octaves so each pitch resonates individually. Quartal voicings — chords built from stacked fourths — hover between major and minor entirely. Together these techniques produce the luminous, suspended quality of Coltrane, Sinephro, and late-period ambient music.

The field vs. the wall

Close voicings — all notes packed within a single octave — sound dense and enclosed. The notes press against each other harmonically, creating a focused, directional sound. Open voicings spread the same chord across two or more octaves; the gaps between notes allow each pitch to ring individually. The chord sounds like a field, not a wall.

The visual difference is immediate in a piano roll: a close voicing appears as a tight cluster of blocks stacked within 60–80px of vertical space; an open voicing spreads those same three notes across 200px or more, with air between them.

Stacked fourths

Conventional Western harmony stacks thirds: D–F#–A is a major triad (major 3rd + minor 3rd). Quartal voicings stack fourths instead: D–G–C places each note a perfect fourth apart. The result is a chord that is neither obviously major nor minor. It hovers. McCoy Tyner used quartal voicings constantly on A Love Supreme; Nala Sinephro's work is built on them. They have a resonant, open quality because 4ths are less directionally charged than 3rds.

Voice leading in quartal progressions

Even when chords don't follow functional harmony, inner voices should move efficiently — by half-step or whole-step when possible. Quartal voicings connect most smoothly when one voice moves by step and the others stay or move by a 4th. This maintains the open, resonant quality without creating harsh, lurching transitions between chords. The quartal language stays consistent; the motion sounds inevitable.

The tension that becomes the point

Sus2 (D–E–A) and sus4 (D–G–A) are conventionally treated as tensions that resolve to major or minor. In functional harmony, a sus4 chord anticipates the resolution of its fourth down to the third. But if held long enough — 4 bars, 8 bars — the suspended chord stops feeling like tension and becomes the destination.

This is one of the defining techniques of ambient and drone-influenced music: the suspension is the chord. The ear recalibrates. What felt unresolved after one bar feels complete after eight. Nala Sinephro's Space 1.8 treats sus2 voicings as tonal home bases, never resolving them. The hovering quality is the point, not a problem to fix.

Duration as reframing

The same suspension chord can feel like unrest or like serenity depending entirely on how long it lasts. At one bar: tension. At eight bars: the new tonic. This is not a trick — it reflects how the brain's harmonic expectation system works. Once a context is held long enough, it stops being a deviation and becomes the norm. Ecstatic music exploits this recalibration systematically.

Two triads, one texture

A polychord stacks two triads a 2nd or 3rd apart, creating shimmer and density that neither triad alone possesses. A D major triad over a G bass creates Gsus2(add5). A C major triad over a D bass creates something close to a D dominant 9th — luminous and harmonically ambiguous. This is the shimmer quality of Bon Iver's layered harmonics and much of late-period Arvo Pärt.

Superimposition in D Mixolydian

The simplest polychord in D Mixolydian: play a C major triad in a high pad register while the bass emphasizes D. The C major notes (C, E, G) all belong to D Mixolydian, so there is no clash — only additional harmonic color. The C adds a ♭7; the E adds a 9th; the G adds the 4th. Together they create a warm, luminous extension of the D harmony without explicitly naming a chord quality.

Voice the four quartal chords

Create a new MIDI track with a long-attack, long-release pad sound — strings, choir, or soft synth pad. Program a 4-bar MIDI clip with these quartal voicings, one chord per bar:

  • Bar 1: D–G–C — lowest note D3, middle G3 or G4, top note C5
  • Bar 2: G–C–F# — lowest note G3, middle C4, top note F#4 or F#5 (top interval is an augmented 4th — a standard quartal variant that keeps all notes in D Mixolydian)
  • Bar 3: A–D–G — lowest note A3, middle D4, top note G4
  • Bar 4: D–G–C — return to bar 1 voicing

Spread is critical: lowest note around D3, top note around C5 — over an octave of vertical space. Each chord lasts the full bar.
