# Minimalist Composition: Tricks & Techniques for Ableton
### A Chapter Outline

*Companion to the Bach Baroque series. Each chapter covers a core concept with a "Build It" exercise demonstrated via Ableton piano roll.*

---

## Part I: Foundations

**Chapter 1 — The Loop as Architecture**
How minimalists treat repetition not as laziness but as structure. Contrast with Bach's development-driven form. The loop is the unit of composition, not the phrase or the theme.
*Build it: Create a single 2-bar pattern and explore how length, timbre, and velocity alone carry weight.*

**Chapter 2 — Loop Craft: Designing a Cell Worth Repeating**
The secret sauce. What makes a loop compelling to listen to on its own? Note choice within a small cell, internal rhythm, register, and negative space. This is the foundation everything else builds on.
*Build it: Design three 4-note cells — one rhythmically inert, one with internal contrast, one with strategic silence — and compare how they hold up over 16 repetitions.*

**Chapter 3 — Micro-Variation: The Art of the Almost-Identical Loop**
The difference between a loop that feels alive and one that feels mechanical. One note changed, one velocity nudged, one pitch dropped by a semitone every 8 bars. Glass does this constantly.
*Build it: Take a 4-bar loop and create three near-identical variants. Arrange them in sequence and annotate the differences with piano roll screenshots.*

---

## Part II: Rhythm

**Chapter 4 — The Pulse as Foundation**
The eighth-note or sixteenth-note motor underlying most minimalist work. How to use it without sounding mechanical — velocity humanization, ghost notes, and the role of consistent subdivision.
*Build it: A steady 16th-note pulse with subtle velocity curves shaping the phrase feel.*

**Chapter 5 — Additive & Subtractive Process**
Building complexity by adding one note at a time — or removing. Reich's *Piano Phase*, Glass's evolving arpeggios. Process as composition.
*Build it: Start with a single-note ostinato and add one pitch per 4 bars. Then reverse and subtract.*

**Chapter 6 — Phase Shifting**
Reich's core trick: two identical loops drifting out of sync. Understanding interference patterns and how they generate emergent melody.
*Build it: Duplicate a MIDI clip, nudge the second by a 16th note, and map the resulting rhythmic collisions.*

**Chapter 7 — Rhythmic Displacement**
Shifting a motif by one beat or subdivision against a fixed grid. Creates polyrhythmic texture without changing any notes.
*Build it: A 3-note cell that starts one 8th note later each repetition, plotted across 8 bars.*

**Chapter 8 — Polyrhythm & Cross-Rhythm**
Layering different cycle lengths: 3 against 4, a 6-note pattern over an 8-note grid. The difference between displacement (same length, shifted) and polyrhythm (different lengths, simultaneous).
*Build it: A piano roll showing a 3-against-4 pattern across two MIDI tracks, with the coincidence points annotated.*

**Chapter 9 — Hocket: Interlocking Voices**
Notes passed between two voices so neither plays a complete melody alone — together they interlock into one. Central to Reich. Creates density from sparseness.
*Build it: Split a single melodic line across two MIDI tracks so the notes alternate. Demonstrate how register and timbre affect the result.*

## Part III: Harmony

**Chapter 10 — Minimalist Harmony: Open Voicings & Modal Chords**
The actual vocabulary: sus chords, added 9ths, open 5ths, avoiding the leading tone, parallel motion. Why minimalist harmony has its specific sound and how to construct it.
*Build it: Voicing worksheet — take three standard triads and rewrite them as open, modal-flavored minimalist chords.*

**Chapter 11 — Diatonic Stasis & Modal Color**
Avoiding functional harmony in favour of modal ambiguity — Dorian, Mixolydian, Aeolian. How to create movement without tension and resolution. The two-chord vamp as a complete harmonic world.
*Build it: A two-chord vamp (I–VII or I–VI) that never resolves, sustained over 16 bars.*

**Chapter 12 — The Arpeggiated Field**
Glass's signature: broken chords cycling over long harmonic durations. How to choose voicings that stay interesting over many repetitions. The relationship between arpeggio shape and harmonic implication.
*Build it: A 6-note arpeggio over a slow ii–V–I with pedal tone, demonstrating how voicing choice changes the character.*

**Chapter 13 — Slow Harmonic Rhythm**
Minimalism's harmonic patience — chords that hold for 4, 8, or 16 bars. How to write melody and arpeggio lines that sustain interest across a long harmonic span.
*Build it: A 16-bar loop where harmony changes only twice. Focus on what happens melodically in the space.*

---

## Part IV: Texture & Form

**Chapter 14 — Textural Layering**
How Glass and Reich build density: each new layer adds timbre or rhythm, not new melody. The "adding instruments" technique as arrangement strategy.
*Build it: 4 MIDI tracks, all on the same loop, each in a different register and with a different attack envelope.*

**Chapter 15 — Sectional Form & The Art of Staying**
Large-scale structure in minimalism: sequences of *states* rather than narrative arcs. When to introduce a new section, how long to stay, what counts as contrast. Transition as event.
*Build it: Sketch a 3-section piece plan (A → A' → B) with transition strategies annotated in Ableton arrangement view.*

**Chapter 16 — Evolution Without Development**
The macro-level question: how do you make a 5-minute piece feel purposeful without Fortspinnung or Sonata form? Techniques: gradual filtering, slow automation, timbral drift, dynamic arc.
*Build it: Take a 4-bar loop and design a 32-bar arrangement using only automation to create a sense of arc.*
