Bach Counterpoint

A working reference for voice leading, phrase architecture, and harmonic thinking drawn from Bach.

Chapters
01
A Single Line

Start with nothing but one voice and make it sing. Stepwise motion, leaps, contour shapes, rhythmic variety, sequences, and the hidden structure inside a Bach run.

Open
02
Making One Line Sound Like Two

Compound melody, register separation, and arpeggiation as implied harmony. One monophonic line that the ear hears as two independent voices.

Open
03
Adding a Second Voice

Real counterpoint begins here. Contrary motion, parallel thirds and sixths, oblique pedal freeze, voice exchange, and what parallel fifths and octaves actually sound like — and why to avoid them.

Open
04
Tension and Release Between Voices

Suspensions, suspension chains, chromatic enclosures, diminished intervals, and cross relations — the techniques that make counterpoint expressive rather than merely correct.

Open
05
Harmonic Foundations

Circle of fifths descent, the 5-1 ratchet, lament bass, ascending bass, chromatic descent, and tonicization vs. modulation — the chord progressions that give your counterpoint somewhere to live.

Open
06
Harmonic Color

Leading tone pull, the Lydian ♯11, Neapolitan drop, augmented sixth chords, modal mixture, and the Picardy third — the chromatic gestures that make harmony expressive rather than merely functional.

Open
07
The Sentence

Vordersatz, Fortspinnung, and Epilog — the three-part Baroque sentence that turns a motif into a paragraph, chains into key journeys, and resolves through deceptive cadences and asymmetric lengths.

Open
08
Motivic Development

Maximum mileage from minimum material. Inversion, retrograde, fragmentation, augmentation, stretto, and invertible counterpoint — the transformation toolkit that turns a four-note seed into a full movement.

Open
09
Large-Scale Form

How sentences chain into movements. Ritornello form, fugue exposition, episodes, the nesting principle, and arch form — the structural containers that give a long piece its sense of arrival and return.

Open
10
Space and Breathing

Making dense music feel open. Six techniques — texture dropout, long notes, sequences, harmonic stillness, register spread, and voice rests — for creating breathing room inside continuous counterpoint.

Open
11
From Bach to Ambient

Translating ten chapters of counterpoint into loops, phase displacement, and space. Voice-leading inside arpeggios, one-note-per-cycle evolution, phase drift, velocity as a third voice, and the subtraction arc.

Open
12
Canons & Rounds

Imitation, rounds, canons at unison and interval, stretto, and phase music as rhythmic canon — the forms between a single motif and a fugue.
