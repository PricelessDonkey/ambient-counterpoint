Displacement shifts a pattern in time; polyrhythm runs two patterns of different lengths at the same time. Where phase shifting produces slow drift, polyrhythm produces a periodic interference pattern — a predictable collision point where both cycles coincide. Understanding coincidence points gives you rhythmic architecture: you know exactly when the ground will reset and everything will line up again.
Two ways to create rhythmic complexity

Displacement takes a single pattern and shifts it against the grid. The pattern length stays the same; only its position changes. The cycle resets when the displaced version has traveled a full pattern-length — which can be a very long time if the shift is not a simple fraction of the loop.

Polyrhythm runs two patterns of genuinely different lengths simultaneously. A 3-beat pattern against a 4-beat grid. A 5-beat phrase against an 8-beat bar. Neither pattern changes; they simply run at different speeds or have different period lengths.

The key difference

Polyrhythm produces a coincidence point — a moment where both patterns land on beat 1 together — at a predictable interval: the least common multiple (LCM) of the two lengths. A 3-against-4 coincides every 12 beats. A 5-against-8 every 40. That coincidence is your structural anchor: the rhythmic "home base" the listener feels even without consciously counting.

Displacement has a coincidence too, but it's usually much longer and less perceptible as a structural event. Polyrhythm makes the coincidence a feature.
The foundational cross-rhythm

A 3-beat pattern played simultaneously with a 4-beat grid. The combined period is 12 beats (LCM of 3 and 4) — exactly 3 bars of 4/4. The two layers coincide at beat 1, then don't meet again until beat 13. In between, the 3-beat pattern is always slightly ahead of or behind where the 4-beat grid expects an accent.

In practice: program a 3-note MIDI clip (e.g., three quarter notes). In a 4/4 session, the clip wraps around the barline on its third repetition. The grid never lets it "sit still."

The yellow dot marks the coincidence point — beat 1 of the 12-beat cycle, where both patterns land simultaneously. This happens once per 12 beats. The next coincidence is beat 13 (start of the 4th bar).
A 6-note loop over an 8-beat grid

Philip Glass's keyboard arpeggios typically use 6-note patterns cycling against an 8-beat (2-bar) phrase. LCM(6, 8) = 24 beats — 6 bars of 4/4. The two cycles only coincide every 24 beats, which at 120 BPM is exactly 12 seconds. This creates the characteristic Glass texture: always cycling, always slightly "off," with an underlying periodicity that the ear feels but rarely consciously catches.

The 6-note loop is the same as saying "the pattern wraps every 6 eighth notes" while the bar wraps every 8 eighth notes. The pattern visits every beat position over 24 beats before resetting.

One coincidence in 24 beats. The 6-note cycle always lands slightly off from the 8-beat phrase boundary — that perpetual near-miss is the Glass texture.
What the numbers tell you

The LCM of your two cycle lengths determines how long your polyrhythm runs before it resets. Choosing lengths that share no common factors (coprime numbers, especially primes) creates very long periods and dense interference. Choosing lengths that share a common factor creates shorter, more regular coincidences.

Practical guide

Short period (frequent coincidence): 2 against 4, 3 against 6 — coincides every 12 or 6 beats. Feels regular, almost metric. Use when you want periodic cadential weight.

Medium period: 3 against 4 (12 beats), 4 against 6 (12 beats), 3 against 8 (24 beats). The Glass/Reich sweet spot: frequent enough to be felt, rare enough to be eventful.

Long period (rare coincidence): 5 against 8 (40 beats), 7 against 12 (84 beats). The coincidence becomes a structural event — a bar line you've composed into the music rather than placed there. Use for long-form pieces where you need macro-level arrival points.

Prime cycle lengths maximize period length. A 7-note loop against a 4/4 grid (LCM = 28 beats = 7 bars) gives you an entire 7-bar arc before the reset — essentially a composed phrase length from a mechanical rule.
The composite rhythm is the composition

When two polyrhythmic layers play together, the combined set of onset points forms a resultant rhythm — a composite pattern that's usually more interesting than either layer alone. Steve Reich's Clapping Music is built entirely around the resultant: two clappers performing the same pattern, one shifted bar by bar, so the listener hears the shifting composite as the music.

For 3-against-4: the 4-beat layer hits beats 1, 5, 9; the 3-beat layer hits beats 1, 4, 7, 10. Combined, the resultant hits at beats 1, 4, 5, 7, 9, 10 — a 6-onset pattern over 12 beats with an irregular but self-consistent rhythm.

Six onsets over 12 beats: densely clustered in the first half (1, 4, 5), sparse in the middle (7), then two in a row at 9–10, then silence. This uneven clustering — not possible from a single repeating pattern — is the emergent personality of the polyrhythm.

Program each polyrhythmic layer independently, then bounce or record the combined output to hear the resultant as a single rhythm. That combined rhythm often works well as a third layer — a "conductor" pattern that implies the full texture.
Add a polyrhythmic third voice

Open your M07 session. You currently have: the main melody with four displacement clips (Disp-0 through Disp-3), Phase B at its chosen offset, and the M04 pulse track.

  1. Add a third MIDI track. Choose a spare timbre — something distinct from the melody and pulse (a short pluck, a bell, a muted key).
  2. Create a 1-bar MIDI clip called M08 poly. Program three quarter notes: D4 on beat 1, A4 on beat 2, F4 on beat 3. Leave beat 4 empty. This is a 3-beat pattern inside a 4/4 bar — it wraps around the barline on every repeat.
  3. Loop this clip in Session View alongside your existing layers.
  4. Find the coincidence point: the moment where M08 poly's D4 and the main melody both land on beat 1 simultaneously. Because the 3-beat pattern fills 3 of 4 beats, the coincidence occurs every 4 bars (beat 1 of bar 5, 9, 13…). Mark that bar in Arrangement View with a locator labeled "POLY ANCHOR."
  5. Adjust the M08 poly volume so the polyrhythmic voice is audible but subordinate — it should add rhythmic complexity without overwhelming the melody.

You now have three independent rhythmic layers: melody/displacement, Phase B, and a 3-against-4 polyrhythm. The POLY ANCHOR locator marks your structural reset point — you'll return to it in M15 when building large-scale form.
