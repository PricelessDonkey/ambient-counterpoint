The Baroque Sentence

How Bach structures melody at the mid-level — bigger than a motif, smaller than a movement. One continuous flow where the current keeps shifting direction.

The Baroque sentence is a three-part structure: an opening statement, a spinning-out phase, and a cadential landing. It's Bach's version of a paragraph — a self-contained unit of musical thought that can be chained with others to build larger forms.

02
How the Epilog Lands

The Epilog works through contrast with everything before it. The Fortspinnung has been running on autopilot — the listener riding the momentum, predicting the next sequence. To signal "we're landing," Bach does three things at once:

The sequential pattern stops

The listener has been predicting "the next one starts a step lower." When that prediction breaks, attention snaps back. The pattern stopping is the signal.

The rhythm decelerates

If the spinning-out was in continuous eighths, the cadence suddenly hits a quarter note or half note. That longer duration feels like a foot coming down.

The harmony funnels into a cadence

The bass moves to the dominant (A in D minor), typically with a 4-3 suspension on top — holding D over the A chord, then resolving to C# — and lands on the tonic. That suspension is almost a Bach signature at cadence points. One last moment of tension before release.

03
Sentences as Paragraphs

Each three-part sentence is a building block, not a whole piece. Think of it as Bach's version of a paragraph. A movement chains several sentences together, each one tonicizing a different key:

Notice: the sentences don't have to be the same length. The first might be short — just establishing the idea. The middle ones grow longer with more elaborate spinning. The final one is often the longest, with the most chromatic tension before the big resolution. That asymmetry keeps the form alive.

04
The Nesting Principle

Fortspinnung feels different from sonata form or verse-chorus because those later forms are about contrast between distinct sections. Bach's approach is more like a river — all one continuous flow, but the current keeps shifting direction.

The sentences blur into each other because the Epilog of one can overlap with the Vordersatz of the next, the same way individual motifs overlap within the Fortspinnung itself.

It's nesting — the same principle at every level. Within a phrase, the tail of one motif becomes the head of the next. Within a movement, the cadence of one sentence becomes the opening of the next. The music is always handing off without a seam, a river that never stops flowing but constantly shifts direction.

Piano Roll Cheatsheet
Start with a 4-note motif

Three stepwise notes + one leap. This is your Vordersatz. Keep it simple — everything else grows from here.

Spin out 2–3 sequences

Copy the shape, paste it one scale step lower each time. Use the landing note of each as the starting note of the next. This is your Fortspinnung.

Break the pattern to land

Stop the sequence. Place a longer note. Add a C♯ pulling to D (or whatever your leading tone is). Land on the tonic with extra duration or a lower octave. This is your Epilog.

Chain sentences through keys

First sentence cadences on i (D minor). Second on III (F major). Third on v (A minor). Final sentence returns home to i with the biggest cadence. Each sentence can reuse the same motif — just transformed for the new key.
