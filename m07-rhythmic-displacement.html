The same four notes, shifted one subdivision at a time. Displacement creates new melodic emphasis, syncopated energy, and rhythmic variety from a single motif — no new pitches required. This chapter covers what displacement is, how it differs from syncopation and phase shifting, and how the reset point determines the length of your variation cycle.
What rhythmic displacement is

Rhythmic displacement takes a complete motif and shifts its start point by one subdivision per repetition or section. The notes stay identical — only their relationship to the metric grid changes. A displaced cell is still the same sequence; it just arrives earlier or later than the bar expects.

This is different from phase shifting (M06), which uses two simultaneous tracks drifting against each other. Displacement is a single voice that you explicitly move to a new grid position. You control when and how far it shifts — the process is composed, not mechanical.

Syncopation vs. displacement

Syncopation is a stable off-beat accent — the pattern stays fixed, but an accent falls between beats. It has a regular, predictable relationship to the bar. Displacement is a moving accent: as the shifted cell cycles through the bar, the strong note rotates through different positions. Over time, every note in the cell will serve as the downbeat arrival.

Syncopation feels groovy and settled. Displacement feels unsettled and generative — like the phrase is searching for its footing while the grid holds firm underneath.
Rotating which note feels like the downbeat

In the seed cell D–F–G–A, D normally falls on beat 1 — it functions as the root arrival. Each displacement variant brings a different note to beat 1: the +0 offset keeps D there, the +1 8th offset puts A there, +2 8ths puts G, and +3 8ths puts F. Across the four variants, beat 1 rotates through all four pitches — D→A→G→F. Because the 4-note cell (4 eighth notes) divides evenly into the 4-beat bar (2 cycles per bar), within any single displaced clip the same note always falls on beat 1 every bar — the rotation only becomes audible when you move between displacement variants.

This creates a subtle form of variation that sounds like reharmonization — the same four notes, but the ear latches onto different ones as structural. No new material needed.

Each 8-note cycle (four 8th-note pitches × two cycles per bar at 88px per cycle in a 176px bar) causes the alignment with beat 1 to shift by one cell-note position. Over four displacement variants, every note in the cell has served as the beat-1 arrival.
How many steps before the phrase realigns

Every displaced cell will eventually return to its original position relative to the bar — this is the reset point. The length of the journey depends on the cell length relative to the bar length. Specifically: reset = LCM(cell length, bar length), measured in the smallest subdivision you're working with.

A 3-beat cell in a 4/4 bar (4-beat bar) resets after 12 beats — 3 bars. The cell repeats 4 times; the bar repeats 3 times; they coincide once at beat 13. A longer reset means a longer, more unpredictable journey before the phrase "settles."

This is why odd-length cells are interesting: a 3-note cell in 4/4, a 5-note cell in a 16-step loop, a 7-step phrase over a 4-bar form. The longer the reset, the more variety from a single motif.

Changing the cell length changes the reset period entirely. A 5-note cell over a 4-beat bar (5 8ths against 8 8ths) resets after LCM(5,8) = 40 8th notes — 5 bars. More notes, longer journey, more variation before the pattern returns to its original alignment.
Hold the metric grid with dynamics

When the pitch pattern shifts, the listener can lose track of where beat 1 is. One solution: keep a strong velocity accent on beat 1 of the bar regardless of which cell note happens to fall there. The pitch content feels displaced and wandering; the velocity accent provides a stable pulse reference.

This creates polyrhythmic tension: the melodic shape implies one kind of downbeat (wherever D falls) and the metric accent insists on another (wherever beat 1 is). When they align — at the reset point — there's a momentary sense of resolution.

When velocity accent and pitch content align — when D finally lands on beat 1 — the moment feels like a resolution. You can use this compositionally: displace the melody for several bars, then let the reset bring D back to beat 1 at a structural moment in the arrangement.
Four displacement variants from one clip

Open your M06 session. You now have three layers: the M05 additive arc (32-bar arrangement), Phase B (a duplicate of the full D–F–G–A pattern at your chosen phase offset), and the M04 pulse track as a rhythmic constant.

  1. Duplicate the main melody clip (not Phase B). Rename the copies: Disp-0, Disp-1, Disp-2, Disp-3.
  2. In Disp-1: select all notes and nudge them right by 1 8th note. The clip now starts at beat 1.5.
  3. In Disp-2: nudge all notes right by 2 8th notes (beat 2).
  4. In Disp-3: nudge all notes right by 3 8th notes (beat 2.5).
  5. In Arrangement View, lay out the four clips as 4-bar blocks in sequence: Disp-0 × 4 bars → Disp-1 × 4 bars → Disp-2 × 4 bars → Disp-3 × 4 bars.
  6. Run Phase B simultaneously across all 16 bars. Listen to how the phase offset and the displacement interact — the combination produces rhythmic patterns neither track creates alone.
  7. Keep the M04 pulse track running throughout as a reference grid. Notice which displacement variants create the most tension with beat 1, and which feel most "resolved."

Your session now has four displacement variants cycling through the melodic material, layered against Phase B and the pulse. This is enough material to begin thinking about large-scale form — the subject of later chapters.
