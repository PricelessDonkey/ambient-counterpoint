Harmonic rhythm is the rate at which chords change. It is the single most overlooked ingredient in autumnal music — and the most powerful. Most producers program chord changes too fast, borrowing habits from jazz, pop, and classical music where harmony drives narrative. Autumnal music does something different: it slows harmonic rhythm until the listener stops waiting for the next chord and starts living inside the current one. This chapter explains what harmonic rhythm is, why slowing it transforms the emotional quality of any progression, and how to use it as a large-scale formal device — controlling shape without conventional form.
The rate of change — not the chords themselves

Harmonic rhythm is the speed at which chords change — how many chord changes occur per bar, per minute, or per phrase. It is independent of which chords you use. The same progression (Dm → C → G → B♭) produces completely different emotional results depending on whether each chord lasts one beat, one bar, two bars, or eight bars.

Fast harmonic rhythm (one chord per beat or per bar) is the norm in jazz, classical, and pop. The listener's ear tracks chord changes as events — each arrival and departure registers, and the music feels like it is narrating, moving, resolving toward something. Slow harmonic rhythm (one chord every two to eight bars) is the norm in ambient and autumnal music. The ear stops tracking arrivals and starts inhabiting the present sound.

From tracking to inhabiting

When harmonic rhythm is fast, the listener's attention tracks chord changes as events. Each arrival and departure registers consciously or unconsciously. The ear is always slightly ahead — anticipating what comes next. This is the narrative mode of listening: the listener is a traveler moving through musical time.

When harmonic rhythm slows, something different happens. After four or eight bars on the same chord, the ear stops waiting for the next arrival. The current chord becomes the entire environment. The listener stops tracking and starts inhabiting. This is the difference between watching a landscape through a car window and standing still inside it.

Bill Evans's extreme case

Bill Evans's Peace Piece (1958) sustains a single C pedal point for over six minutes in the left hand while the right hand improvises freely above it. There is one chord. The harmonic rhythm is effectively zero. The piece is not boring — it is transfixing. This is the clearest demonstration that harmonic interest and harmonic rhythm are independent variables.

How slow is slow enough?

Most producers program chord changes too fast. The first-draft instinct is to treat harmonic rhythm as a pop template: one chord per bar, maybe two bars for the tonic. Autumnal music requires deliberately resisting this instinct.

A workable autumnal minimum: one chord every two bars at 70 BPM. That is approximately 6.9 seconds per chord — long enough for the ear to settle but short enough to feel like the piece is still moving. Many great autumnal pieces use four to eight bars per chord. Beyond eight bars, you are entering drone territory, which has its own logic.

The productive question

Rather than asking "how long should each chord be," ask: what is the shortest acceptable duration for this chord? The answer will be different for each chord in your progression. A chord that resolves tension (like returning to Dm after B♭) can bear more length than a transitional chord (like G, which wants to move). Let each chord's harmonic weight determine its duration.

For context, Bach's chorales change harmony roughly every beat or every half-bar — a much faster harmonic rhythm serving a different musical purpose. See Ch 03 — Two Voices for how Bach uses chorale-rate harmonic rhythm to drive contrapuntal forward motion.
The non-harmonic dimensions

Slow harmonic rhythm is not silence. When harmony stops moving, interest must come from elsewhere. The dimensions that carry a piece forward when chords are held:

  • Melody — a slow, considered melodic line can provide all the motion a progression needs. The melody creates local harmonic rhythm even when the underlying chords don't change.
  • Register shifts — moving a pad up an octave, bringing the bass in or out, adding a high-register counter-melody. Vertical space is a form of motion.
  • Velocity arcs — a held chord whose velocity slowly rises and falls has an interior shape. This is not the same as a static chord.
  • Timbral evolution — a filter opening gradually, a reverb tail lengthening, a pad slowly brightening and dimming. These are the equivalents of harmonic motion in the timbral dimension.
  • Silence — removing notes rather than adding them. A long chord that fades to near-silence before the next arrival creates anticipation without changing the harmony.
Trusting stillness

The hardest skill in autumnal music is resisting the impulse to fill every moment. When a chord has been present for four bars and nothing has changed, the producer's instinct is often to add something — a new melody note, a fill, a counter-rhythm. Sometimes this is right. Often, the most autumnal response is to wait one more bar. The chord, heard long enough, reveals interior qualities that only patience unlocks.
Shape through acceleration and deceleration

Harmonic rhythm is not just a texture setting — it is a formal tool. By controlling when harmonic rhythm briefly accelerates and when it decelerates, you control the large-scale emotional shape of a piece without relying on conventional formal devices (verse/chorus, tension/release, development/recapitulation).

The principle: a long chord creates openness and rest; a brief acceleration (two or three chords arriving in quick succession) creates climax or tension; a return to long chords creates resolution and settling. This is the form of many great ambient pieces, including Eno's 1/1 and Arvo Pärt's Spiegel im Spiegel.

For large-scale form through harmonic pacing in the Bach tradition, see Ch 09 — Large-Scale Form. For how minimalist loop structures create gravity through repetition, see M01 — The Loop as Architecture.

The shape curve

The curve above is the shape of countless great autumnal pieces. Long stretches of slow harmonic rhythm, one moment of brief acceleration (the piece "wakes up"), then a return to slowness. The listener experiences this as an emotional arc — not told, but felt.

Control harmonic rhythm and you control form. Two chords per minute is not too few. The question is not "how fast?" but "what is happening in the non-harmonic dimensions while the chords breathe?"
Double the chord durations — trust the stillness

Open your "Autumnal Harmony v3" session from A06 — 36 bars of the autumnal progression ending with the Lydian episode at bars 33–36.

Double the duration of every chord clip. A clip that occupied 1 bar becomes 2 bars; a clip that occupied 2 bars becomes 4 bars. The Lydian episode (2 bars of Fmaj7♯11) becomes 4 bars, followed by 4 bars of Dm(add9) as the return. You now have 72 bars at 70 BPM — roughly four minutes of music.

Play it from beginning to end without stopping. While you listen:

  • Notice where it feels too long — which chord moments generate restlessness.
  • For each restless moment: ask is it the chord itself, or is it that nothing else is moving?
  • Do not change the chords yet. Write down the bar numbers that feel restless — these are the moments where melody or texture will later do their work.

Save the session as "Autumnal Harmony v4 — Slow". This 72-bar bed is the harmonic skeleton for the remaining chapters. Everything we add — melody, lateness, timbre, texture — lives on top of this slow-moving architecture.
