CHAPTER A13 ◇ FALLING LINES ◇ STEPWISE DESCENT ◇ THE MINOR 3RD SIGH ◇ REACH AND SURRENDER ◇ PHRASES DISCOVERED Falling Lines Autumn is a downward season. Lines that fall feel right. Autumnal melodies descend. Not always, not dramatically — but the overall trajectory of a phrase is downward, and the emotional weight of the music arrives with each step of the descent. This chapter is about the melodic shape of autumn: why falling lines feel right, how to construct them, and what makes a single upward leap so powerful when it comes.

Autumnal melodies tend to descend more than they ascend. Phrases end by descending, and the overall trajectory across a section gradually settles lower. This is not a rule — it is a tendency that emerges from the emotional register autumnal music inhabits.

Compare spring music: upward lines, leading tones pulled toward resolution, climaxes that arrive at high points. Then compare autumn: stepwise descent, settling, arriving below the starting point. Spring music climbs toward something. Autumn music exhales.

The downward bias does not mean the melody never rises. It means that rises are subordinate, that the net movement of any phrase is downward, and that the highest note of a phrase is rarely its last. The line reaches up briefly — and falls back.

The most autumnal melodic shape is simple stepwise descent. D4–C4–B♭3–A3: three steps down from the tonic, spanning a perfect fourth. At 70 BPM in half notes, this descent takes four bars. It feels like exhaling slowly.

This is the shape of Bill Evans's right-hand phrases in Peace Piece — not exact pitches, but the gesture: beginning somewhere in the upper register, stepping down, settling. No leaps, no drama, just gravity followed patiently.

The melody for this piece uses the same gravity: A4 → G4 → A4 → G4 → F4 → E4 → F4 → D4. Eight notes, eight bars. The small returns (A4 again in bar 3, F4 again in bar 7) are not contradictions — they are breath. The overall direction is unmistakably down.

The dashed line is not in the music — it is the gravity underneath it, the arc toward which the phrase tends. The small returns in bars 3 and 7 are audible as breath before the next descent, not as reversals of direction. Cross-reference: stepwise motion and avoiding leaps, Ch 01 — Single Line.

Dropping a minor third — F4 to D4, G4 to E4, C4 to A3 — is a melodic sigh. Small interval, large emotional payload. The minor third fall appears at phrase endings in Brahms, Sibelius, and throughout folk music. It has the quality of breath released: not a collapse, just a settling.

The minor third is small enough to feel inevitable and large enough to feel intentional. It does not demand resolution the way a leading tone does, and it does not feel dramatic the way a leap of a sixth would. It simply arrives — a step and a half below where it started.

When in doubt about how to end an autumnal phrase, fall a minor third. The phrase will feel complete without feeling closed — the exact quality autumnal music requires.

Rare, but powerful: an upward leap of a fourth or fifth creates a single moment of yearning before the line falls back stepwise. The classic autumnal melodic shape is stepwise descent → upward leap → stepwise descent again. Reach, then surrender.

The leap is not a climax — it is a reaching, a moment of wanting something that the phrase then releases. The descent after the leap carries greater emotional weight because of the leap that preceded it. Without the leap, the second descent is simply more of the same. With it, the descent becomes a return — an acceptance.

Use this shape sparingly. One upward leap per phrase is enough. A melody with many leaps is not autumnal — it is agitated. The single leap works precisely because everything else is falling.

The leap here is a fifth — D4 up to A4 — which lands on a note already in the harmonic vocabulary of the piece. The arrival back at A4 feels familiar but now earned: the same note that opened the phrase is now a gift the melody returns to before giving it up entirely.

Starting on beat 2, beat 3, or an upbeat creates a sense that the melody was already happening before you noticed it. Autumnal melodies feel discovered rather than announced.

When a melody begins on beat 1, it announces itself. The downbeat is a declaration: "the melody starts now." An autumnal melody does not want to announce itself. It wants to have already begun — to be found mid-thought, already on its way somewhere else.

Starting on beat 2.5 (the "and" of beat 2) is particularly effective: it is neither the primary downbeat nor the obvious weak beat. It is a place the grid mostly ignores. The listener's ear finds the melody there as if stumbling upon it, rather than expecting it.

This connects to the behind-the-beat timing from A08 — Behind the Beat: nudge lateness affects when within a beat a note lands; phrase entry timing affects which beat the phrase chooses. Both produce the same quality — music that feels inhabited rather than programmed. Cross-reference: phrase construction and the sentence, Ch 07 — The Sentence.

Placement details
  • Start on beat 2.5 of bar 1 — not beat 1. The melody begins mid-bar, discovered rather than announced.
  • Each note: half-note duration, then silence until the next bar. Silence occupies most of each bar.
  • Apply +10ms nudge to every note — the behind-the-beat timing from A08 continues throughout the melody.
  • The A4 in bar 1 is new — it replaces or absorbs the D5 you placed in A08. You may keep D5 as a preceding upbeat note if it fits; or remove it and let A4 begin the arc cleanly. Trust your ear.
Check the shape

Play the full 8-bar melody over the harmonic bed. It should feel as if it was already descending before you noticed it. The arrival on D4 in bar 8 should feel inevitable — earned by seven bars of gravity before it.

The two small returns (A4 in bar 3, F4 in bar 7) are breath, not reversals. If they feel like interruptions rather than pauses, nudge them slightly later in the bar — +20ms instead of +10ms.
