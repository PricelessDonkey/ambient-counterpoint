Functional harmony is a story with a destination — every chord points toward the next, and the whole piece is a journey toward rest. Modal harmony is a room. You're not going anywhere; you're inhabiting a sound-world. This chapter covers the shift from narrative to immersive listening, the two-chord vamp as a complete compositional universe, and the D Dorian voicings that make it work.
The Difference in Listening Mode

Functional harmony — Bach, jazz, most pop — uses tension and resolution. The V chord wants to go to I. Every chord in the progression is either building tension or releasing it. The listener's ear tracks this narrative: "Where are we going? Are we there yet?"

Modal harmony sets up a tonal center (D) and moves between chords without creating strong directional pull. There's no "wanting." D Dorian gives you seven notes and a set of chords that can all coexist without any of them urgently demanding to go somewhere else.

The perceptual shift this enables: once the listener stops waiting for resolution, they start listening to texture, timbre, and rhythm instead of harmonic narrative. That shift is the compositional goal.
Oscillation, Not Progression

Dm7 → G is not a progression going somewhere. It's an oscillation — two states alternating, neither winning. The listener's ear eventually stops tracking the change as movement and starts hearing it as texture.

This oscillation can sustain an entire piece. Glass's *Metamorphosis* uses essentially two harmonic states for extended periods. Reich's *Music for 18 Musicians* moves between a small set of chords over 90 minutes. The two-chord vamp isn't a limitation — it's a compositional commitment to depth over breadth.

Other viable two-chord pairs in D Dorian: Dm → C, Dm → F, Dm → Am. Each pair has a different color and a different amount of distance between the two states. The further apart (harmonically) the two chords, the more the oscillation "breathes."
The Perceptual Shift

Functional listening is an active, goal-directed process. The ear tracks harmonic narrative the way the eye tracks a moving object. It's engaged but slightly anxious — always waiting for the next move.

Immersive listening is different. Once the listener gives up waiting for resolution (usually after 16–32 bars of modal stasis), the attention shifts to texture, timbre, internal motion, and the subtle changes happening inside the loop. The same four bars become richer, not more boring.

Glass counts on this perceptual shift. His early works — *Einstein on the Beach*, *Music in 12 Parts* — train the listener into immersive mode through sheer duration. Shorter works (5–10 minutes) rely on setting up the modal world quickly and trusting the listener to follow.
Four Common Two-Chord Pairs

D Dorian's characteristic note is B natural — the major 6th that distinguishes it from D natural minor (which has B♭). Any chord that includes B natural signals Dorian clearly. The i → IV vamp (Dm → G) is the strongest signal: the G major chord contains B natural.
The Mildest Possible Harmonic Change

Root motion — moving the bass from one note to another — is the strongest kind of harmonic change. When the bass moves from D to G, every listener hears it as a chord change regardless of what's happening above.

The static bass technique removes root motion entirely: hold D2 as a drone and move upper voices through chord tones only. The harmony changes (the color shifts from Dm7 to Gsus4) but the bass never moves. The listener hears harmonic color changes without the harmonic momentum that root motion creates.

This is the mildest possible harmonic motion — a step below the two-chord vamp in terms of harmonic restlessness. Use it when you want the texture to feel nearly static while still shifting color subtly. Bach uses the same technique as pedal point; see Harmonic Engines for the contrapuntal context. Minimalism extends this from a brief technique to the entire harmonic language.

The D Dorian two-chord vamp is not a stepping stone to more complex harmony — it is complete in itself. Glass built a career on it. The richness comes from what happens inside the loop, not from adding chords.
Upgrade the Chord Track to a Full Vamp

Open your M10 session. You have six active layers: Hocket A, Hocket B, Phase B, the M08 poly pattern, the pulse, and the M10 chord track (D open 5th / Gsus4, 4 bars).

Replace or extend the M10 chord clip with a full 8-bar vamp:

  • Bars 1–4: Dm7 — D3, F3, A3, C4 (whole notes, one per bar)
  • Bars 5–8: Gsus4 — G3, C4, D4 (whole notes, one per bar)

Loop this 8-bar chord progression. Run all other tracks over it.

Listen for the Dorian color: The G chord brings B natural into the harmony (implied in the Gsus4 context, clearly present if you expand to G major — G3, B3, D4). The Dm7 has C natural. Neither chord wins — they oscillate. That ambiguity is the target sound.

If the chords compete with the hocket melody, bring chord velocity down to 55–65. The chords should be felt more than heard — a foundation, not a feature.

You now have six layers built on a full 8-bar Dorian vamp. The chord track has graduated from open voicings to a complete harmonic statement. Future chapters will add arpeggios and textural evolution over this foundation.
