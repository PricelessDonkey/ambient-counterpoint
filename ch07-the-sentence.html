The Sentence

How motifs grow into paragraphs — presentation, development, and cadential landing.

A single sentence is the smallest unit of musical narrative: a motif is stated, spun out through sequences, then brought to rest at a cadence. Bach deploys the same three zones — Vordersatz, Fortspinnung, Epilog — at every scale, from a 4-bar phrase to a 40-bar movement. This chapter maps the structure, shows how sentences chain into journeys across keys, and reveals the deceptive resolution as a tool for extending and deepening the arc.
31
Vordersatz → Fortspinnung → Epilog

Every Baroque sentence has three zones. The Vordersatz (front phrase) presents the motif clearly — typically 2 bars, on stable harmony. The Fortspinnung (spinning out) develops that motif through sequential repetition, pushing through related harmonies. The Epilog stops the pattern, decelerates, and lands on a cadence.

Three-Zone Overview

The three zones need not be equal. A sentence of 8 bars might be 2 + 4 + 2, or 1 + 5 + 2, or even 2 + 2 + 4 if the cadence needs room to breathe. What matters is that each zone has a clear character and function.

32
How the Epilog Lands

The Epilog is not just "the end" — it is a deliberate gear-change. Four things happen simultaneously: (1) the sequential pattern stops; (2) the rhythm decelerates (shorter notes give way to longer ones); (3) the harmony funnels toward dominant then tonic; (4) a 4–3 suspension marks the final approach.

The 4–3 Suspension Signature

The 4–3 suspension is the Epilog's calling card: the upper voice holds the 4th above the bass while the harmony moves to the dominant, then resolves down to the 3rd. It creates a moment of held breath before the cadence exhales. In D minor over an A bass: upper voice D5 (held — the perfect 4th above A) → resolves to C♯5 (the major 3rd, harmonic minor leading tone).

Epilog checklist
Last sequential repetition ends → rhythm lengthens → bass moves to dominant → upper voice suspends 4th → resolves to 3rd → tonic arrival
33
The Deceptive Resolution

When the dominant arrives at the end of the Epilog and the listener expects tonic — give them VI instead (in D minor: the dominant A resolves to B♭ major, the diatonic major chord on the 6th degree). This is a deceptive cadence. The sentence does not end; instead it pivots into a new phrase or a cadential extension.

The Rewind Technique

After a deceptive cadence, Bach often "rewinds" — he restates the Epilog approach from a slightly earlier point, as if correcting a false start. The second attempt at the cadence lands harder because the listener has already felt the near-miss.

Deceptive → rewind → true cadence
Epilog arrives at V → resolves to VI (deceptive) → brief restatement of V approach → resolves to i (true cadence)

In ambient music this pattern is invaluable: the deceptive cadence extends the piece without simply repeating material, and the rewind creates the sense that resolution was always coming — it just needed more space.

34
Sentences as Paragraphs

A Baroque sentence is a self-contained unit of musical thought — equivalent to a paragraph in prose. It has a topic (the motif), development (Fortspinnung), and a conclusion (Epilog). Like a paragraph, it can stand alone or chain with others.

Crucially, a sentence is not a whole piece. It is a building block. A short piece has 2–3 sentences; a fugue movement has many. Each sentence advances the narrative: it establishes a key, explores tension, and resolves — then the next sentence picks up at a new pitch level.

Sentence = paragraph rule
End of sentence = full cadence. End of piece = final sentence in home key. Mid-piece sentences end on related keys (III, v, VI) — never a dead stop, always a handoff.

The Key Journey

A multi-sentence movement travels through a sequence of related keys — one key center per sentence. In D minor the standard journey moves through the relative major and parallel minor before returning home:

Each new key is reached by treating the final chord of the preceding Epilog as a pivot — a chord that belongs to both the old key and the new. The move to III (F major) is seamless because D minor and F major share nearly all pitches. The move to v (A minor) tightens tension before the home return.

36
Asymmetric Sentence Lengths

Sentences in a movement are almost never the same length. The first sentence is short — it establishes, nothing more. Later sentences expand as material develops and tensions accumulate. The final sentence is the longest: it needs room for a full Fortspinnung, a deceptive cadence, the rewind, and the true cadence.

The pattern: establish short, develop longer, resolve longest. A final sentence shorter than the preceding ones feels rushed. A final sentence that sprawls longest feels earned.

Short sentence to open, longer to develop, longest to close — with a deceptive cadence and rewind that earn the final resolution.

Build It — The First Full Sentence

Open your Ch 06 session (4 bars, 3 tracks: upper voice, lower voice, bass — ending with Neapolitan → dominant → Picardy third). You are expanding this into a full 8-bar Baroque sentence.

Step 1 — Extend to 8 bars

In your DAW, extend all three tracks to 8 bars. Your Ch 06 bars 1–2 become the Vordersatz. Bars 3–4 (from Ch 06) begin the Fortspinnung. Now you will add bars 5–8.

Step 2 — Fortspinnung (bars 3–6)

Continue the circle-of-fifths bass: D2 → G2 → C2 → F2 → B♭1 → E♭2 → A1. One root per bar. The sequence follows perfect fourths through bar 6 (D→G→C→F→B♭→E♭), then breaks the pattern deliberately: E♭ leaps to A1 (dominant) to end the Fortspinnung and trigger the Epilog — a standard Baroque cut-off. The upper voices ride the sequence — transpose the Ch 06 upper voice motif down by step each bar. Keep eighth-note motion throughout. The Fortspinnung should feel like it could go on forever; that's the point.

Step 3 — Epilog with deceptive resolution (bars 7–8)

Bar 7: Stop the sequence. Bass arrives on A1 (dominant). Upper voice holds D5 (the perfect 4th above A) — your 4–3 suspension. Let it ring for 2 beats, then resolve D5 → C♯5 on beat 3 (harmonic minor leading tone). Rhythm slows to quarter notes.

Bar 8, beats 1–2: Instead of landing on Dm, resolve to B♭ major (deceptive — VI). Upper voice: D5 (step up from C♯5, landing on the root of B♭ major). Bass: B♭1. Let it ring.

Bar 8, beats 3–4: Rewind — return to V (bass A1, upper voice D5 → C♯5). Land on Dm on beat 4 or let it fall into bar 9 as the true cadence. Add F#4 in the upper voice for the Picardy third from Ch 06.

You now have an 8-bar Baroque sentence: 2-bar Vordersatz, 4-bar Fortspinnung over a circle-of-fifths bass, and a 2-bar Epilog with 4–3 suspension (D5 → C♯5 over A bass), deceptive cadence, and rewind to the true ending.
