Bach never threw away a motif — he put it through a transformation machine. Inversion, retrograde, fragmentation, augmentation, stretto: these are not tricks but a systematic toolkit for deriving an entire movement from a single seed idea. The four-note figure you wrote in Chapter 1 already contains everything you need to fill any length of music.

Melodic Inversion

Flip every interval upside down. If the motif goes up a major second then up a minor third, the inversion goes down a major second then down a minor third. The shape mirrors around its starting pitch.

Four canonical transformations

Every motif has four standard orientations. Original and retrograde share the same set of intervals forward and backward; inversion and retrograde-inversion do the same with flipped intervals. The diagrams below use tonal inversion — intervals are mirrored by diatonic step within D minor rather than by exact semitone count, keeping all notes in the key. (Real inversion preserves exact interval sizes and may introduce accidentals outside the key.)

Mirror the shape, keep the character
Inversion sounds like the same motif seen in a mirror — listeners recognize it subconsciously without consciously noticing the flip. This is why inversion feels like development rather than departure.
38

Retrograde

Play the motif backwards. The retrograde of D E F A is A F E D. Unlike inversion, retrograde is harder for listeners to consciously recognize — but it maintains the same set of intervals and therefore the same harmonic "flavor."

Retrograde is most effective when the melodic rhythm is also reversed, so the long notes that used to end the motif now begin it. This creates a sense of arrival-then-departure, the reverse of the original's departure-then-arrival shape.

Retrograde as cadential turn
Because retrograde ends on the motif's opening pitch, it naturally functions as a return gesture. Use it to circle back to the tonic at phrase endings — the music feels like it has "completed its arc."
39

Fragmentation

As musical tension builds, present only the first two or three notes of the motif. The motif appears to "break apart under pressure." Listeners fill in the rest from memory, which creates active engagement. The gap where the remaining notes should be becomes its own expressive device.

Motif dissolving across eight bars

Start with the full motif, then drop one note per section. By bars 7–8 only a single sustained pitch remains — maximum compression, maximum tension before release.

Absence as presence
When a listener has heard the motif four times, they anticipate the fourth note. Playing only three notes makes the missing fourth note ring out in the imagination — often louder than if you had played it.
40

Augmentation and Diminution

Augmentation doubles every note value — the motif moves at half speed, taking on a grand, monumental character. Diminution halves every note value — the motif accelerates, urgent and compressed. The pitches are identical; only time changes.

Same motif, three speeds

Augmentation is particularly powerful for final cadences — the motif expands into the cadence, slowing the world down as if the music is aware it is ending. Bach uses this in fugue codas to great effect.

Augmentation at cadences, diminution in development
Use augmentation to signal arrival and finality. Use diminution to create drive and urgency in the middle of a phrase. The same notes do opposite emotional work depending on their speed.
41

Stretto

In a stretto, a second voice enters with the motif before the first voice has finished. The entries pile up, overlapping each other — voices tumbling over one another in increasing urgency. Bach uses stretto to build the climax of a fugue, compressing the space between entries as tension peaks.

Voices entering before the previous one finishes

The overlap zone is the stretto's tension core — two voices are simultaneously in the middle of the same melodic idea. Combining the original form with its inversion creates the densest stretto texture because the voices move in contrary motion, avoiding parallel octaves or fifths.

Tighten the offset to increase tension
Bach often begins stretto entries 4 beats apart, then 2 beats apart, then 1 beat apart as the climax approaches. Shortening the gap between entries is itself a form of rhythmic compression — the chase accelerating.
42

Invertible Counterpoint

Two melodies are in invertible counterpoint if they work just as well when swapped — the upper voice becomes the lower and vice versa. Bach designs his subjects and countersubjects this way, allowing him to flip the texture freely without creating forbidden parallels or awkward voice crossing.

Designing for double use

The test: take your two voices and transpose one of them up or down an octave so their relative positions swap. If the result still sounds good — no parallel fifths or octaves, no awkward leaps — you have invertible counterpoint.

Practically, this means avoiding intervals of a fourth between the voices. When the voices flip, a fourth becomes a fifth — and parallel fourths in the original produce forbidden parallel fifths when inverted. A fifth, by contrast, becomes a fourth when inverted — harmless. Writing mostly in thirds and sixths avoids the problem entirely, since thirds invert to sixths and vice versa.

Write in thirds and sixths, swap for free
A passage dominated by thirds and sixths between voices inverts cleanly into sixths and thirds. This is why Bach's countersubjects tend toward these intervals — he needs them to work both above and below the subject.
Relation to ambient music

In loop-based music, invertible counterpoint means a two-voice loop can be recomposed mid-track simply by moving one layer up an octave. The harmonic relationship stays intact; the perceived "melody" shifts. It is a zero-effort textural variation that sounds like substantial compositional change.

Build It

Open your Ch 07 session — 8 bars, 3 MIDI tracks. You will add a second 8-bar section (bars 9–16) as a motivic development episode, using the 4-note seed motif D E F A from Chapter 1 as your raw material.

Bars 9–10 — Inversion

Add a 4th MIDI track. Play the inverted form of the motif: D C B♭ G (starting on D4, descending by the same intervals that the original ascends). Loop it over two bars. Let the existing harmony from Ch07 ring underneath — the inversion will land differently against each chord change.

Bars 11–12 — Fragmentation

Trim the motif to its first three notes: D E F. Keep repeating this fragment, varying the rhythm slightly each time. Leave a gap where the A should be. Automate a subtle filter or reverb swell to mark where the missing note "would" have arrived.

Bars 13–14 — Stretto

Use two tracks simultaneously. Track A plays the original motif D E F A. Track B (pitched using your inversion) enters 2 beats after Track A — before Track A has finished. Set both tracks at a slightly lower velocity than the melody to keep the texture transparent. You should hear them chasing each other.

Bars 15–16 — Augmentation cadence

Double every note value. The motif D E F A now takes the full 2 bars: D4 as a half note in bar 15, then E4–F4 as quarter notes, then A4 as a half note landing on bar 16 beat 1. Hold the A4 through the downbeat, then resolve to D4 in the second half of bar 16. The piece is now 16 bars: the Ch07 sentence followed by a motivic development episode.
