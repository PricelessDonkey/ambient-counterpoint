A block chord is a moment. An arpeggio is a texture. When you spread a chord's notes across continuous 8th or 16th notes, the chord stops being an event and becomes a field — a sonic environment the listener inhabits. This is the connective tissue of Glass's keyboard writing, and one of the most practical tools for building sustained minimalist texture in Ableton.
From Chord to Texture

Instead of playing a chord as a block (all notes simultaneously), you cycle through its notes as 8th or 16th notes continuously. The chord becomes a texture — not a moment but a field the listener enters.

Glass uses this in Metamorphosis, Glassworks, and The Hours. The arpeggio runs for minutes without stopping. The harmonic content (which chord is implied) changes, but the rhythmic continuity never does. This creates an environment rather than a narrative.

In a loop-based Ableton session, the arpeggiated field is ideal: it loops seamlessly, it implies harmony without a separate chord instrument, and its steady 8th-note pulse weaves between the hocket melody and the pulse without competing with either.
Order and Register Determine Everything

The same four notes (D–F–A–C) produce radically different textures depending on voicing: which octave, which order, which direction. A root-position ascending arpeggio (D4–F4–A4–C5) sounds different from a spread pattern (D4–A4–F4–C5) or an alternating up/down pattern (D4–C5–F4–A4).

The three main voicing types and what they do:

  • Root-position ascending — clear, ladder-like. Strong harmonic statement. Works well as the first introduction of the arpeggio.
  • Spread — wide leaps, airy. The root and 5th anchor the bottom; color notes float above. Creates space for a melody.
  • Alternating up/down — the Glass signature. Notes weave around a central register. Dense but not cluttered. Works at fast tempos.

The voicing should leave room for the melody — choose one that occupies the middle register and doesn't eat into the hocket's pitch range (D4–G4).
A Complete Harmonic Statement from One Track

A continuous arpeggio of D–F–A–C spells out Dm7 to any listener's ear, even with no separate chord instrument. The brain reconstructs the chord from the sequence of pitches: it hears the pattern, recognizes the intervals, and assembles the harmonic meaning.

This means: one MIDI track playing an arpeggio + a bass drone below = a complete harmonic statement. You don't need a separate chord track. The arpeggio is the chord, unfolded in time.

In practice this matters for mix clarity: a block chord + melody in similar registers creates competition. An arpeggio sits in a specific register and time-slot pattern, leaving more sonic space for other voices.
Rhythmic Continuity Through the Transition

When the underlying harmony changes — from Dm7 to Gsus4 — the arpeggio voicing shifts with it, but the rhythmic pulse never stops. The 8th notes continue unbroken across the bar line. The listener hears a harmonic color change inside a continuous rhythmic field.

This is what makes Glass's arpeggios feel smooth even over large harmonic shifts. The rhythm is the constant; the pitches are the variable. The transition is heard as a color change, not as a break.

In Ableton: two adjacent clips in the chord track, one per chord. The arpeggio clips are the same rhythmic length; only the pitch content changes. At the clip boundary, the rhythm continues and the pitches shift.
Where the Arpeggio Lives in a Full Texture

In a complete minimalist texture, each layer owns a register. The arpeggio's natural home is the middle register — roughly octaves 3 and 4 (C3–C5). The bass drone lives below it (D1–D2); the sparse melody floats above it (D4–D5 or higher).

This placement is not accidental. The arpeggio is connective tissue: it fills the harmonic and registral space between bass and melody, preventing the texture from feeling hollow. But it should remain in the background — audible but not foregrounded. Velocity matters: the arpeggio at 70–80 velocity sits behind a melody at 90–100.

Rule: if the arpeggio is competing with your melody, it's either too high in register or too loud. Move it down an octave before reducing velocity — register is more powerful than volume in creating foreground/background separation.

The arpeggiated field turns a harmonic decision (what chord) into a textural decision (what timbre and density). Once the arpeggio is running, the listener stops hearing "chord changes" and starts hearing "texture evolving." That shift is the point.
Replace the Chord Track with a 6-Note Arpeggio

Open your M11 session. You have six active layers: Hocket A, Hocket B, Phase B, M08 poly, pulse, and the 8-bar Dm7 → Gsus4 chord vamp (whole notes).

Replace or supplement the M11 chord track with a new MIDI track using a bright, medium-attack timbre (electric piano, mallet, marimba, or a plucked synth).

Create two clips (one per chord, matching the 4-bar chord regions):

  • Dm7 arpeggio clip (4 bars): D4–F4–A4–C5–A4–F4, cycling as 8th notes. Each 6-note cycle takes 3 beats; the pattern repeats with a 1-beat gap, or you can double it to fill the bar more completely.
  • Gsus4 arpeggio clip (4 bars): G3–C4–D4–G4–C4–D4, same 8th-note principle.

Place them back-to-back to form the 8-bar loop. The 8th-note pulse should be unbroken across the chord boundary.

Check the mix: Play against Hocket A and B. Does the arpeggio sit under the hocket melody or compete with it? If it competes, lower arpeggio velocity to 65–75, or shift the whole arpeggio down one octave (D3–F3–A3–C4). The hocket should remain the primary voice.

You now have an arpeggiated field running continuously across the Dorian vamp. The session has graduated from block chords to a continuous textural weave — six layers with distinct roles: pulse, phase, polyrhythm, hocket melody, and arpeggiated harmony.
