Pop song structure and Baroque formal logic are the same psychological system, separated by 300 years. The hook is a Vordersatz. The verse spins it out like Fortspinnung. The pre-chorus delays resolution with a deceptive cadence. The chorus is the Epilog — the earned landing. The bridge is a fugal episode. This chapter maps those identities explicitly and shows you how to build pop hooks, verses, and choruses using the exact tools Bach used.

A hook is a Vordersatz — an opening statement that contains a distinctive interval or rhythm and implies a harmonic move, all in two to four bars. Bach's opening motifs are hooks. They're short, they have one thing that catches the ear, and they immediately establish the tonal center.

What makes a hook hook

Three things: a distinctive interval (a leap, a syncopation, a chromatic note the ear doesn't expect), a short length (two to four bars — if it takes longer to say, it's already a verse), and a harmonic move (it leaves the tonic, implying where it wants to go). Most pop hooks do all three. Most Bach Kopfthema do all three. Same thing.

Hook vs. melody

The hook is the seed. The melody is the elaboration of that seed. In Bach, the opening motif gets developed, inverted, fragmented, and sequenced — but the hook itself stays the same thing every time it returns. In pop, the chorus hook appears unchanged while the verses explore surrounding territory. The hook's job is to be immediately recognizable, every single time.

The leap from F4 to A4 is the catch. Steps are comfortable and blur together; one unexpected interval is memorable. Every durable hook has exactly one thing like this — the moment the ear snags on.

The verse takes the hook's energy and spins it out. In Bach this is Fortspinnung — the opening motif is sequenced down by step two or three times, riding a circle-of-fifths or descending bass. In a pop verse, the same mechanism operates: the harmonic pattern keeps moving, the melody develops the hook's shape, and there's a forward pull toward the chorus.

How to build a verse from a hook

Take the hook shape and transpose it down by one step. Do that two or three more times. You now have a verse. Repetition of the shape creates familiarity; downward transposition creates gentle descent and forward motion toward the pre-chorus. Bach does this in every spinning section — you've been doing it since Ch 1 and Ch 7.

The bass drives it

A circle-of-fifths bass (D→G→C→F→Bb→A) feels rolling and inevitable. A lament bass (D→C→Bb→A, stepwise descent) feels focused and melancholy. Either way: melodic repetition over harmonic motion is the sound of a verse. See Ch 5 and Ch 7 for the bass engines in detail.

You don't need to write new melody for a verse. Sequence the hook downward two or three times over a moving bass. The listener hears "the same thing, going somewhere" — familiar but in motion. That tension is what makes a verse feel like forward momentum rather than repetition.

The pre-chorus has one job: make the chorus feel inevitable. Bach's tool is the deceptive cadence — V wants to resolve to i but lands on bVI instead, creating a delay that makes the eventual arrival hit harder. That V→bVI move is the structural pre-chorus in baroque music.

Fragmentation as tension

As the cadence approaches, Bach breaks the motif into smaller and smaller fragments. If the hook is four notes, the pre-chorus uses only the first two, repeating with increasing rhythmic density. The motif "disintegrates under pressure" — which is exactly the feeling of a pre-chorus build. See the deceptive cadence and rewind technique in Ch 7 for the full mechanics.

Harmonic rhythm acceleration

Verses use one chord per bar. Pre-choruses accelerate: one chord per beat, or two per beat. Faster chord changes = more tension. Combined with the deceptive cadence, this creates a physical sense of being held back before the release.

The deceptive cadence (V→bVI) is the most powerful pre-chorus tool. Your ear expects the tonic. It gets Bb major instead — shock, delay. The longer you hold off, the harder the chorus lands. Bach has been doing this since the Baroque. Pop producers do it instinctively. Now you do it on purpose.

The chorus lands hard because it's an earned resolution. The listener has been denied the tonic for eight to twelve bars. Now it arrives — widest voicing, all voices active, full register. This is Bach's cadential Epilog: the passage where tension finally collapses onto the tonic, usually with a 4-3 suspension over the dominant.

Strong chorus progressions in D minor

Three essential chorus loops: i–VII–VI–V (Dm–C–Bb–A, the "Andalusian cadence" — descending bass, unstoppable pull toward A), i–iv–VII–III (Dm–Gm–C–F, more hopeful, lifts toward the relative major), i–v–iv–i (Dm–Am–Gm–Dm, darkest, turns inward). All three connect directly to the harmonic engines in Ch 5.

Register and texture at the chorus

The chorus should be the widest voicing in the piece — bass in the low register, melody in the high register, inner voices filling the middle. Where a verse might use one or two voices, the chorus uses all of them. This register expansion is itself part of the emotional release: the sound physically opens up. See Ch 10 for the register-as-structure principles.

The chorus doesn't need new melody. The hook, returned at full register over the earned harmonic resolution, is the chorus. The emotional impact comes entirely from releasing the tension built in the verse and pre-chorus — not from new melodic content.

The bridge breaks the pattern, provides new information, and makes the final chorus feel fresh rather than repetitive. In Bach's fugues, episodes do exactly this: free material appears between subject entries, often modulating to a related key, built from theme fragments, before the subject returns at the tonic. A pop bridge is a fugue episode that ends on a V chord.

Where to go harmonically

The most natural bridge destination from D minor is its relative major, F major. The ear knows F major from D minor (same notes, different home base), so the shift feels surprising but grounded. Four bars in F major, develop a fragment of the hook, then two bars of pure A major (dominant pedal) — and the final chorus arrives like a returning hero.

Texture reduction in the bridge

Strip the texture down. If the chorus is all voices wide open, the bridge should thin to one or two voices — just the hook fragment and bass, or the hook alone. The textural contrast makes the final chorus's return feel enormous. This is the subtraction arc from Ch 10 used as a formal structural device rather than a fade.

The bridge must end on V (A major). Everything else is flexible — the key, the texture, the material. But if it doesn't land on V before the final chorus, the chorus can't arrive with maximum force. The dominant is a coiled spring; the chorus is the release.

Open your Ch 14 session (60 bars, 9 tracks). You're going to use Ableton's arrangement view locators to map pop structure onto your existing material — the same notes, reorganized into verse-chorus form.

Step 1 — Mark your hook (bars 1–4)

Bars 1–4 are your Vordersatz — D4 E4 F4 A4 G4 seed motif with lower voice A3 G3 F3 D3. This is your hook. In arrangement view, right-click bar 1 and add a locator: "HOOK". The leap F4→A4 is the catch — the distinctive interval. Note it.

Step 2 — Mark your verse (bars 5–16)

Bars 5–16 contain the Fortspinnung sequences and motivic development. The sequence in bars 5–8 takes the hook shape and moves it downward — that's verse motion. Add a locator at bar 5: "VERSE 1". Add one at bar 9: "VERSE 1 (development)".

Step 3 — Build a 4-bar pre-chorus (new material)

Create Track 10. Write a 4-bar pre-chorus fragment: use only D4 E4 (the first two notes of the hook) repeating with increasing density — quarter notes bars 1–2, eighth notes bar 3, sixteenth notes bar 4. Harmony: V (A) → bVI (Bb) → V → V with a held A major on beat 4. Add locator: "PRE-CHORUS". This is the V→bVI deceptive delay built into the arrangement.

Step 4 — Chorus from the parallel period (bars 45–52)

Copy bars 45–52 (your antecedent phrase) to the position after the pre-chorus. This is your chorus — it has the full harmonic arrival with the It⁶ → I⁶₄ → V⁷ → i cadence from Ch 14. Spread voices to maximum register: upper track up one octave, bass down one octave. Add locator: "CHORUS". The Italian sixth you added in Ch 14 is now your pre-chorus cadential punch.

Step 5 — Bridge from the canon (bars 33–40)

Copy bars 33–40 (your canon section, Track 6 + Track 9) to the position after the first chorus. This is your bridge — the canon texture is naturally thinner than the full chorus, and the imitative entries give it a sense of spinning in place (episode logic). Transpose all canon pitches up 3 semitones (into F major, the relative major). The final 2 bars: mute everything except bass; hold A1 as a dominant pedal. Add locator: "BRIDGE".

Step 6 — Final chorus

Copy the chorus block again after the bridge. This is the final chorus — all voices, full register, nothing muted. Let it run 8 bars then cut to silence. The piece now reads: Hook (4) → Verse (12) → Pre-chorus (4) → Chorus (8) → Bridge (10) → Final Chorus (8). You've had this material since Ch 1. It was always a pop song.
