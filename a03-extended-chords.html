Extended chords — add9, maj7, min7 — are one of the core tools of the autumnal palette. They blur the hard edges of a triad, introduce ambiguity between chord and scale, and create the hovering, suspended quality that distinguishes autumnal harmony from ordinary minor-key writing. This chapter explains the four key extensions, when to use plain triads for contrast, and how to re-voice your harmonic skeleton with extensions on top.
D–F–A–E: The Chord That Hovers

Adding a 9th to a minor chord changes its emotional weight entirely. Dm(add9) = D–F–A–E. The E is the 9th above the root — it blurs the boundary between the chord and the scale. The listener's ear isn't sure whether it's hearing a chord or a cluster of closely related notes. That ambiguity is autumnal.

The 9th doesn't demand resolution. Unlike a leading tone, it has no obligation — it can sit, sustained, for bars at a time. This is what makes Dm(add9) the natural starting point for autumnal harmony: it's a minor chord that has learned not to go anywhere.

Voice the E in the upper register — D3, F3, A3, E4 is the standard autumnal voicing. The E4 on top creates space between the chord members and lets the 9th ring clearly above everything else. A close-position Dm(add9) with E buried inside the chord loses most of the effect.

Chord vs Scale Ambiguity

The 9th creates a specific kind of ambiguity: with D–F–A–E sounding together, the ear can't commit to hearing a four-note chord or just four notes from the D Aeolian scale. That's a productive uncertainty. It stops the listener from "naming" the chord and just lets them feel it. Autumnal music often works by denying the listener the ability to label what they're hearing.
The Minor Triad with Wistfulness on Top

Dm(maj7) = D–F–A–C#. This is the chord that defines Bill Evans's harmonic world. The minor triad provides sadness; the major 7th (C# in a D context) adds wistfulness — that particular feeling of something almost within reach but not quite there.

The C# creates a tiny internal tension inside the chord: it's a semitone below D, giving the chord a slight upward lean without any urgency to resolve. This is the difference between wistfulness and longing — wistfulness accepts distance, longing fights it. Dm(maj7) accepts.

Use Dm(maj7) sparingly. It's a highly colored chord — it does a specific emotional thing, and overuse turns it into a mannerism. One or two moments per piece, at structurally significant points, is the right dose.

Bill Evans's Signature

Evans used the minor-major 7th chord and related extensions as a kind of emotional fingerprint. The quality is: minor sadness, major brightness, suspended together without resolution. That suspended quality — holding both at once — is the essence of the autumnal sound. Cross-reference: A15 covers Peace Piece in detail, where Evans builds an entire piece from this quality.
D–F–A–C: Neutral and at Ease

Dm7 = D–F–A–C natural. This is the most neutral extended minor chord. It's darker than add9 (no E brightening the top), warmer than plain Dm (the C creates a soft 7th above the root), and without the wistfulness of Dm(maj7). Dm7 is the "resting" chord: not static, not emotionally colored, just at ease.

The C natural above the D minor triad is the ♭7 — a note completely within D Aeolian. It extends the chord without introducing any foreign pitch. This means Dm7 sits seamlessly inside the mode. Where Dm(add9) creates ambiguity and Dm(maj7) creates wistfulness, Dm7 simply settles.

When to Use Each

Think of the three minor extensions as a spectrum of restlessness: Dm7 is at rest; Dm(add9) is hovering; Dm(maj7) is wistful. Use Dm7 when you want the tonic to feel complete but not static. Use Dm(add9) at the opening or at suspension points. Use Dm(maj7) only at moments of specific emotional weight — the places where you want the listener to feel something catch.
Cmaj7, Bbmaj7, Fmaj7

The major 7th on a major chord — Cmaj7, Bbmaj7, Fmaj7 — removes the hardness from the major triad. A plain C major chord has a settled, clear quality. Cmaj7 (C–E–G–B) keeps the warmth but adds a suspended shimmer: the B natural is only a semitone below C, giving the chord a very gentle internal lean without resolution pressure.

Bbmaj7 (B♭–D–F–A) is particularly autumnal. The A natural on top of a B♭ chord softens the ♭VI color significantly — instead of landing with warmth and slight weight, Bbmaj7 drifts. The major 7th transforms the ♭VI's heaviness into suspension.

Fmaj7 (F–A–C–E) is a special case. The E natural at the top is simultaneously the major 7th of F and the 9th above D. In a D-centered piece, Fmaj7 feels like it belongs to both F and D at once — a chord that's in two places at the same time. This double loyalty is highly autumnal.

Avoiding Gmaj7

One exception: in D Dorian, avoid Gmaj7. G major (IV) is the Dorian fingerprint chord because of its plain, clear warmth. Adding the F# major 7th (Gmaj7 = G–B–D–F#) tips the chord into jazz territory — the F# is a strong color that sounds more like a standard ii–V–I jazz voicing than an autumnal moment. Use G(add9) = G–B–D–A instead: same warmth, no jazz association.
The Landing After the Float

Extended chords can become homogeneous. If every chord in a piece is an add9 or maj7, the harmonic texture flattens — nothing stands out, and the floating quality that made those chords special becomes the new baseline. The listener stops noticing it.

A plain Dm after a series of extended chords sounds like bedrock: grounded, arrived, finished. It is one of the most effective contrast moves in autumnal music. The extended chords create the float; the plain triad creates the landing.

Where to Land
End of a harmonic cycle
If your progression loops (Dm → Cmaj7 → Dm → Bbmaj7 → Dm), consider making the final Dm a plain triad — no add9, no maj7. The return to the root is confirmed. Then the loop restarts with the extended version.
After the longest chord
If a chord has been sitting for 4 bars or more, the extended version may have already lost its edge. Try replacing it with a plain triad — the simplicity can feel like a relief rather than a reduction.
At a structural break
When a new section begins — after a long intro, before a melodic entry — a plain tonic triad can function as a reset. It says: here is where we are. Everything that follows will float from this foundation.
The Softening Concept

Think of the triad as a shape with defined edges. The add9 version is the same shape, slightly expanded — its boundary is less clear, its sound more diffuse. Extended chords blur edges; plain triads restore them. You need both: the blur is only meaningful against the definition.

Re-voicing Each Chord

Work through each chord and confirm the extensions are voiced with the 9th or maj7 on top, in the upper register. The target voicings:

  • Dm(add9) — D3, F3, A3, E4. The E4 must be clearly the highest note. No change if you already have it here.
  • Cmaj7 — C3, E3, G3, B3. The B3 (major 7th) is on top. Listen to how it softens the C major chord's clarity.
  • Dm (plain) — D3, F3, A3. Leave this as a plain triad — this is the landing chord that follows the extended ones. Resist the urge to extend it.
  • G(add9) — G3, B3, D4, A4. The A4 (add9) is on top. Note: do not use Gmaj7 here — F# sounds too jazzy. G(add9) keeps the Dorian warmth without the jazz association.
  • Bbmaj7 — B♭2, D3, F3, A3. The A3 (major 7th) on top transforms ♭VI's weight into drift.

Listen Through

Play the full 16-bar skeleton. Listen to how the texture shifts from stated to suspended. The Dm(add9) opening floats. The Cmaj7 keeps the shimmer. The plain Dm (bars 7–8) lands. The G(add9) gives warmth. The Bbmaj7 drifts. Then back to the floating Dm(add9).
