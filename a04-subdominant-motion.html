CHAPTER A04 ◇ SUBDOMINANT MOTION ◇ CADENCES THAT SETTLE ◇ CHAPTER A04 ◇ SUBDOMINANT MOTION ◇ CADENCES THAT SETTLE ◇ Subdominant Motion Instead of tension–resolution, try weight–rest. The dominant chord (V) creates urgency — a pull that demands resolution. Subdominant-direction cadences (♭VII–i, IV–i, ♭VI–i) do something different: they let the tonic arrive without drama. This chapter is about replacing the expectation-fulfillment loop with the gentler sensation of settling — a stone coming to rest, not a resolution.
Two kinds of pull

The dominant chord (A major or A7 in D) exerts a specific kind of harmonic pressure: it contains C#, the leading tone that wants to rise to D by a half step. This creates an expectation that must be resolved. The tension is felt physically.

The subdominant direction — chords built on or around the IV, ♭VI, and ♭VII — creates a different pull. It gravitates toward the tonic not by tension but by weight. There is no leading tone, no half-step urgency. The tonic feels like a place to rest, not a place to fall to.

This distinction is the harmonic engine of autumnal music. Every technique in this series exploits subdominant gravity. If your piece feels too dramatic or too resolved, check whether a V chord snuck in.
C major → Dm in D

C major to Dm is the most common autumnal cadence. C natural has no leading-tone relationship to D — there is no C# pulling upward, no half-step urgency. The motion feels horizontal: the chord shifts rather than resolves.

The sensation is of sitting down. The tonic arrives, but without drama. This is why ♭VII–i is ubiquitous in ambient music, post-rock, and folk — it signals arrival without the formal weight of a perfect cadence.

In D Aeolian, C major is the ♭VII chord. In D Dorian it occupies the same structural position. Either way, the motion is gentle. Use it to close phrases, return to tonic after excursions, or end a section without announcing it.
G major → Dm in D

The plagal cadence — IV to i — is the amen cadence of church music. Moving from the major IV to the minor tonic has a quality of gentleness and completion. It is warm rather than melancholy, resolved rather than floating.

In D Dorian (where G major is the diatonic IV), this cadence is native and common. In D Aeolian, G minor is the diatonic IV — so using G major here is already a touch of modal colour, a slightly warmer light before the Aeolian darkness of Dm.

The plagal arch is slightly more settled than ♭VII–i. Where the drift feels like a gentle landing, the plagal feels like a hand placed gently on a surface. Use it for moments of warmth and rest, especially after active passages.

See Ch 05 — Cadential Types for a full taxonomy of Bach cadences, including how he avoids the plagal in favour of the perfect authentic.
B♭ major → Dm in D

The darkest of the subdominant cadences. B♭ major to Dm has a slight downward pull: the root Bb descends by half step to A — the fifth of Dm — a genuine semitone resolution that gives the motion its melancholy quality. D and F are common tones shared between both chords, making the voice leading exceptionally smooth. Neither ♭VII–i nor IV–i has this half-step root pull.

Use ♭VI–i for moments of wistful or bittersweet arrival — the emotional equivalent of exhaling slowly. It is sadder than the plagal, more introspective than the drift. In extended progressions, it makes the finest closing gesture before the final tonic.

B♭ major is native to D Aeolian (the ♭VI chord). It is slightly foreign in D Dorian, which raises the sixth degree to B natural. This means ♭VI–i in a Dorian context carries a faint borrowed quality — a hint of darkness from the parallel minor.
What A major does to the mood

A major (or A7) contains C#, the leading tone a half step below D. This note wants to resolve upward, and the ear hears it as a directed tension. When the dominant resolves to tonic, the effect is functional: drama fulfilled, expectation met.

In autumnal music, this is usually wrong. The fulfilled expectation pulls the listener out of stasis and into narrative. The piece suddenly has a climax, a before and after. This is the opposite of the environmental quality we are building.

The softened dominant — A minor or Am7 — is acceptable. Without C# the leading-tone urgency disappears. Am7 (A–C–E–G) functions more like a colour chord than a functional dominant; the C natural softens the pull entirely.

Reserve A major for an explicit climax moment — a single point of arrival, if the piece has one. Most autumnal pieces should have at most one, and some should have none. If your progression contains multiple A major chords, the mood will tip toward the dramatic regardless of how slow your harmonic rhythm is.

Settling vs resolving — a visual comparison

The harmonic rhythm map below shows the 8-bar loop from the Build It exercise. Each block is one bar. Notice that Dm appears four times — but it arrives differently each time depending on which chord precedes it.

Each of the three subdominant cadences occurs once in the loop. The ♭VII (Cmaj7) drifts to Dm at bar 4. The IV (G add9) arches gently to Dm at bar 6. The ♭VI (Bbmaj7) closes with wistful weight at bar 8. All three return to the same tonic — but the emotional character of each arrival is different.

Notice that there is no A major anywhere in the loop. The absence of the leading tone is why it doesn't feel like it's going anywhere. It simply turns.

Subdominant cadences don't resolve — they settle. The distinction is felt rather than heard: one has a destination, the other has a resting place. Autumnal music lives in the resting place.

Dm(add9) × 2 bars → Cmaj7 × 1 → Dm × 1 → G(add9) × 1 → Dm × 1 → Bbmaj7 × 1 → Dm × 1

You already have all five clips voiced from A03. Arrange them in this order in a new Scene. Keep the same warm pad sound, same voicings.

Listen for the absence of urgency

Loop the 8 bars and let it run for 3–4 minutes. Notice what doesn't happen: there is no moment of arrival, no fulfilled expectation. The tonic appears three times in 8 bars — each time with a different quality (after drift, after arch, after wistful weight) — but none of them feel like endings.

If anything feels too dramatic, check whether the pad voicing is placing a leading-tone (C#) in the upper register by accident. Remove it if found.
