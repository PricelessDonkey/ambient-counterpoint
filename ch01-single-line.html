A Single Line

Before counterpoint, before harmony — just one voice, moving through time with intention.

Everything starts here. A single melody line is the hardest thing to write well. There's nowhere to hide — no chord stack to fill the space, no second voice to carry the weight. But Bach's melodic thinking is also the most transferable skill in this whole system: the same rules that make a solo line compelling are what make two-voice counterpoint, harmonic progressions, and even ambient loops work. Get this right, and the rest follows.
1
Stepwise Motion & Leaps — The 70/30 Ratio

Bach's melodies move mostly by step — adjacent scale degrees, one note at a time. Roughly 70% stepwise, 30% leaps. That ratio creates the feeling of a line that has somewhere to go without constantly lurching.

Steps create connection. They feel inevitable — your ear can track them without effort. Leaps create drama. They pull attention, introduce energy that needs to be resolved. The melody earns its leaps by spending the rest of the time stepping.

The leap rule

After a leap, step back in the opposite direction. If you leap up a fifth (D4 → A4), the next note should step down (A4 → G4). The leap creates tension; the step deflates it. Leap away, step back. Bach almost never leaps in the same direction twice in a row.

Steps are the default. Leaps are the event. After the event, return to default — step in the opposite direction. Your ear expects it, and when you deliver, the leap feels resolved.
2
The Five Contour Shapes

Every Bach phrase has a shape — a contour you can draw as a silhouette. There are five recurring templates. Internalising these gives you a menu to reach for when starting a new phrase. See phrase-shapes.html for detailed SVG diagrams of each.

How to use the shapes

Pick a shape before you start writing. It gives you a target — you know roughly where the phrase is going before you commit to the first note. The arch is the most universal; it rises to a peak and returns. The leap-step is the most dramatic opener. The circle is your arpeggio shape — chord tones stacked in time, then a step-wise descent to land.

3
Rhythmic Variety

Pitch gets most of the attention, but rhythm is what separates a melody from a scale. Three patterns do most of the work in Bach's melodic writing:

Long–short–short (♩ ♪♪)

One beat note followed by two half-beat notes. Creates forward momentum — the long note establishes, the short pair rushes toward the next downbeat. In a 4/4 DAW grid: a quarter note on beat 1, two eighths on beats 2-and and 3. This is Bach's default.

Short–short–long (♪♪ ♩)

The reverse: two quick pickups that arrive on a longer held note. Creates a gathering, arriving feeling. Use this when you want the phrase to settle — especially before a cadence.

Anacrusis — the pickup note

Starting a phrase on the "and" of beat 4 rather than beat 1. The melody begins slightly before the bar, which means it lands on beat 1 with a sense of inevitability. In Ableton: place your first MIDI note at bar X, beat 4-and (the last eighth of the previous bar). The strong beat arrival hits harder because you earned it with the pickup.

Rhythmic displacement

Take a phrase and shift every note one eighth note later. The pitches are identical — the rhythm is offset. The phrase suddenly sounds like it's leaning forward, or slightly off-balance. Bach uses this to create the sense of a melody that doesn't quite resolve when expected.

4
Sequences — Same Shape, Different Position

A sequence takes a short melodic idea — a 2–4 note motif — and repeats it starting on a different scale degree, usually one step down or up. The shape stays the same; only the starting pitch moves. Three repetitions, then break the pattern.

The sequence creates a sense of inevitability — your ear locks onto the pattern after the second repetition and knows where the third is going. Breaking the pattern after the third is what makes the phrase breathe and arrive somewhere. If you never break it, the sequence just continues like wallpaper — your ear stops listening. If you break it on the second repetition, you haven't established the pattern yet.

In D minor, a descending sequence by step looks like: motif starting on D → same motif starting on C → same motif starting on Bb → break into cadence. That's three instances, then out.

The sequence creates the expectation. The break creates the arrival. Without both, you have neither tension nor release — just repetition.
5
Constant Motion with Hidden Structure

Bach's fast runs — strings of sixteenth notes that seem to flow continuously — aren't random. They have a skeleton: chord tones fall on strong beats, passing tones fill the weak beats. Your ear hears the top level (a continuous stream) and the bottom level (the chord) simultaneously, without consciously parsing the difference.

Strong beats vs weak beats

In a bar of 4/4 sixteenth notes, beats 1, 2, 3, and 4 are strong (the downbeats of each quarter). The "ands," "e's," and "ahs" are weak. In a bar of eighth notes, beats 1, 2, 3, 4 are strong, and the "ands" are weak.

Chord tones on strong beats. Scale-step passing tones on weak beats. The melody implies harmony at its skeleton even without a bassline.

The principle in action — an 8-note run

The same logic applies whether you're writing eighth-note lines or sixteenth-note runs. Take a Dm chord (D, F, A). Lay the chord tones on beats 1, 2, 3, 4 of a bar of eighth notes. Then fill the gaps between them with adjacent scale steps. You now have an 8-note run that implies Dm throughout, but sounds like fluid motion. The listener hears melody — you know it's a chord with decoration.

Why this matters for ambient/loop music

When you build an 8-note arpeggio loop in Ableton, this principle tells you which notes to put where. Put the chord root (D) and fifth (A) on the downbeats. Fill the rest with scale steps. The loop will imply harmony even with no bass, no pad — just the single melodic line cycling.

The run is a chord in disguise. The strong beats are the chord. The weak beats are the path between them. Your ear strips away the path and hears the skeleton underneath.
✦
Build It — 4-Bar Melody in Ableton

Open a new MIDI clip in Ableton. One MIDI track, no effects, no instrument loaded yet — just map to any piano sound. Set the clip to 4 bars, 120 BPM, 4/4. You're writing the seed motif that will grow into everything in subsequent chapters.

The seed motif (step–step–leap–step)

Write this 4-note motif in bar 1, starting on beat 1:

  • Beat 1: D4 — quarter note
  • Beat 2: E4 — eighth note
  • Beat 2+: F4 — eighth note
  • Beat 3: A4 — quarter note (leap of a third — the event)
  • Beat 4: G4 — quarter note (step back down after the leap)

That's your motif: D E F A G. Step–step–leap–step.

Bar 2 — sequence it down

Same shape, starting one step lower (C4). Bach's sequences stay in the key — the leap shrinks from a major third to a minor third to fit the scale, but the contour stays the same:

  • Beat 1: C4 — quarter note
  • Beat 2: D4 — eighth
  • Beat 2+: E4 — eighth
  • Beat 3: G4 — quarter (leap)
  • Beat 4: F4 — quarter (step back)

Bar 3 — sequence again (Bb)

Same shape, starting on Bb3:

  • Beat 1: Bb3 — quarter
  • Beat 2: C4 — eighth
  • Beat 2+: D4 — eighth
  • Beat 3: F4 — quarter (leap)
  • Beat 4: E4 — quarter (step back)

Bar 4 — break the pattern, land on D

Don't sequence again. Change direction entirely to signal arrival:

  • Beat 1: A4 — quarter (jump up — unexpected)
  • Beat 2: G4 — eighth
  • Beat 2+: F4 — eighth
  • Beat 3: E4 — quarter
  • Beat 4: D4 — quarter note (land and hold)

The final D4 signals resolution. The pattern stopped; the sequence ended; we're home.

What to listen for

Play the 4 bars back. Notice how bars 1–3 feel like the same phrase spinning — same shape, lower each time, pulling downward. Bar 4 suddenly goes up before stepping down to rest. That upward surprise is the "break." The final D4 is the exhale. This 4-bar phrase is the seed of everything that follows. Save the clip — you'll add to it in every subsequent chapter.
