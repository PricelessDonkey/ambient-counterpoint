CHAPTER A14 ◇ INCONCLUSIVE ENDINGS ◇ PHRASES THAT DON'T RESOLVE ◇ THE MID-THOUGHT LANDING ◇ HOVERING ◇ SILENCE AS ARRIVAL Inconclusive Endings Not every phrase needs to resolve. Some just stop. The natural endpoint of a descending phrase in D minor is D4 — root position, closed, done. But a closed ending carries finality, and autumnal music rarely wants to feel finished. This chapter is about where else a phrase can stop: on the third, the fifth, the ninth — or not at all, letting silence carry the ending instead.

When the melody in A13 arrived on D4 in bar 8, it felt resolved. The falling line had done its work; the phrase was complete. In another genre this would be exactly right: the listener hears the descent, arrives at the root, and knows the phrase is over.

In autumnal music, this is a problem. A phrase ending on D4 — root position, tonic, low register — creates a small sense of punctuation. The music has made a statement and closed it. It says: that thought is finished.

Autumnal music doesn't want to finish thoughts. It wants to leave them in the air — considered, not concluded. The phrase needs to stop without landing, to end without a period. This means thinking carefully about where the melody's last note falls.

The patterns that feel too closed: arriving on the root (D4) in a low register; ending a descending scale on its final degree; completing a phrase that began on an upper chord tone and falls all the way to the root. Compare how Bach handles this in half cadences and interrupted cadences — the phrase stops short of full resolution, and the silence after feels pregnant rather than settled: Ch 05 — Cadences. The autumnal version operates on the same principle, extended over much longer durations at much slower tempos.

Rather than landing on the root, the phrase can stop at three other positions — each with a different emotional quality. All three share a single property: the music clearly means to stop here, but the listener doesn't feel that the thought has been completed.

The Third: F4 — Mid-Thought

F4 is the minor third — the note that makes this chord minor, the color note of Dm. A phrase ending on F4 feels as though it has arrived inside the chord but not completed it. F is warm and familiar: it belongs here, it is the right neighborhood. But it is not a landing place.

Think of ending a sentence with a dependent clause. The grammar is correct, the words are right, but the statement remains open. F4 has this quality. It is the most comfortable of the inconclusive endings — the one that feels most like a natural pause rather than an interruption. It says something more to say without urgency.

The Fifth: A4 — Hovering

A4 is the perfect fifth — stable, open, high in the register relative to D4. A phrase arriving on A4 feels like a question mark. The fifth is stable enough to feel intentional: this is not an accident or an error, the phrase means to stop here. But it is unresolved enough to feel like an invitation for something more.

A4 hovers. It is the note the phrase arrives at when it hasn't quite decided where to go. Evans uses this frequently in Peace Piece — phrases that hold at the fifth before gently descending again. The hovering quality gives autumnal music its characteristic sense of suspension: time continues, but the music waits.

The Ninth: E4 — Floating

E4 — the added ninth in Dm(add9) — is a color tone not part of the basic triad. A phrase ending on E4 floats. It is not dissonant enough to demand resolution, not stable enough to feel like a choice. It exists in a kind of suspension above the harmony, belonging to it without being anchored by it.

The ninth is the most ambiguous of the options. It sounds as though the phrase dissolved rather than ended — it simply grew quieter and stopped, the thought trailing off mid-sentence. For very slow, very sparse textures, this is the most ghostly of the options. The listener waits for a D4 that never arrives.

Open-form treatment of non-resolution in minimalist music — where the ending is deliberately withheld or deferred across long durations — connects directly to this technique: M15 — Open Form.

The arrival note is the emotional signature of the phrase. Choose it last — after the approach is written — and the phrase will tell you which ending it wants.

The most powerful inconclusive ending is not a note — it is silence. The phrase stops on F4 or A4, and then nothing happens for two, three, four bars. The harmonic bed continues; the reverb tail decays. The melody is absent. And in that absence, the ending occurs.

This is how Evans, Sibelius, and Grouper handle phrase endings. The phrase does not conclude — it stops, and time continues around it. The silence is not a rest in the conventional sense (a measured pause before the next event). It is the ending. What the phrase said hangs in the air, and the silence is where the listener hears it.

The mechanism is simple: let the phrase end on an unresolved pitch, then wait. Three bars is usually enough. One bar feels like a breath; two bars feel like a pause; three bars feel like a full stop that doesn't close. More than four bars risks losing the thread entirely, unless the texture is extremely slow.

The distinction between a rest and a silence matters here. A rest is a gap between events, a measured space the listener crosses quickly to reach the next phrase. A silence is load-bearing — the listener inhabits it. In autumnal music, silence after an unresolved phrase is not empty time. It is where the phrase lives after it ends.

Silence after an unresolved phrase is not emptiness — it is the ending itself. The phrase stops; the silence listens to what was just said.
