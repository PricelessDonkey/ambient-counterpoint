Sound Like Two Still monophonic — but the ear hears polyphony. The ear is a grouping machine. Give it two streams of pitch information — even interleaved inside a single line — and it will organize them into separate voices automatically. Bach exploits this constantly: one violin or keyboard line zigzags between high and low registers, and the listener hears a melody above and a bass below. This chapter shows you how to write that way, and why it's the foundation for everything that comes next in counterpoint.

A compound melody is a single melodic line that the ear parses as two (or more) independent voices. The mechanism: alternate between two distinct pitch registers quickly enough that the brain groups same-register notes into separate streams. One instrument, two perceived voices — no accompaniment needed.

Auditory stream segregation

When notes are separated by more than roughly a sixth, the auditory system stops hearing them as a single continuous line and begins tracking them as parallel threads. This is auditory stream segregation — the same mechanism that lets you follow a single voice in a crowd. Bach's compound melodies put multiple streams into one instrumental part and trust the listener's brain to do the sorting.

The illusion holds as long as register separation is consistent. If the "upper voice" notes are always above C5 and the "lower voice" notes are always below C4, the grouping is unambiguous. Let them drift into the same range and the two-voice illusion collapses into a single line again.

One line, written as one line — but heard as two. The two-voice illusion is a gift from psychoacoustics. Your only job is to keep the register separation consistent so the brain can do its grouping work.

Compound melody stands or falls on register discipline. Assign each implied voice a home octave and keep it there. Notes that stray into shared territory muddy the grouping. Notes that cross — where the "upper" voice dips below the "lower" voice — shatter it entirely.

The two-octave gap

The most reliable division: upper voice in octave 5, lower voice in octave 3. This leaves all of octave 4 as empty space between them — a buffer zone the ear treats as proof that two independent streams exist. In Ableton's piano roll, you'll see two horizontal bands of notes with a clear gap between. That visual clarity maps directly to sonic clarity.

You don't need two full octaves of separation for the effect to work — a consistent sixth can be enough — but the two-octave gap is the safest starting point while you're learning the technique. Once the two-voice illusion is intuitive, you can bring the registers closer together.

Don't let the voices meet

Even within their own lanes, the voices shouldn't land on the same pitch-class at the same time. If the upper voice lands on D5 and the lower voice immediately follows with D3, the two streams briefly merge — the ear hears "D" twice, not two independent voices both passing through D. When both voices need to arrive at D, fine — but separate them rhythmically so one has already moved on before the other arrives.

Alternation patterns

The most natural pattern is strict alternation: upper, lower, upper, lower. But you can also hold in one register for two notes before switching: upper, upper, lower, upper, lower, lower. The brain will still group them correctly as long as the register assignment is consistent. What you can't do is stay in one register for so long that the listener forgets the other voice exists — return to each voice at least every 2–3 notes or the grouping dissolves.

The register boundary is load-bearing structure. Keep the voices in their lanes and the listener's brain builds the polyphony for free — effortlessly, no extra notes required.

Beyond two-voice illusion, there's a second way a single line implies something larger: arpeggiation. Play D–F–A in rapid succession and your ear doesn't hear three notes — it hears D minor. The intervals between adjacent notes activate a harmonic template, and the brain stores the whole group as a single unit: a chord.

Chords spread across time

When you arpeggiate through three chord tones — root, third, fifth — in quick succession, the ear integrates them into a harmony even though no two notes sound simultaneously. String several arpeggios together and you have a chord progression implied by a single unaccompanied line. D–F–A → C–E–G → Bb–D–F implies i → VII → VI in D minor. Three chords, one voice, no bass, no pad.

This is the "circle" contour shape from phrase-shapes.html — the arpeggio-to-step pattern where you climb through a chord and then step to the next one. Bach uses it constantly in the solo violin and cello works. You'll use it to build self-contained loop patterns that imply a full harmony.

Root on a strong beat

For the harmonic implication to be unambiguous, put the chord root on beat 1. Start the D minor arpeggio on D and the bar reads as Dm. Start it on F and the bar might feel like F major or just harmonically ambiguous. The strong-beat rule from Chapter 1 applies at the chord level: the ear accepts the first strong beat as the "label" for what chord it's hearing, and interprets everything that follows in that bar as an elaboration of that harmony.

Why this matters for ambient loops

When you build an 8-note arpeggio loop in Ableton, you're not writing a melody — you're writing a chord in motion. The loop will imply a harmony even with no bass, no pad, no second instrument. The loop from Chapter 1 (D–E–F–A–G and its sequences) already contains this: D on beat 1 anchors each bar as Dm. Change one note and you shift the implied chord. This is why single-voice loop music can feel harmonically rich: every note change is also a harmonic statement.

The arpeggio doesn't decorate a chord — it is the chord, unfolded in time. Your ear integrates the notes into a harmonic unit just as readily as if they were played simultaneously.

Open your Ch 01 session. You have a 4-bar single melody line in D minor: the D–E–F–A–G motif sequenced twice, then a break cadencing to D4. Now rewrite the entire thing as a compound melody: the same melodic content but split across octave 5 (upper voice) and octave 3 (lower voice). Keep the original rhythm — quarter, eighth, eighth, quarter, quarter per bar — but assign each note to one of the two register bands. Delete the original version or mute it; this is a replacement, not a layer.

How to assign the notes

Pattern: melodic notes → octave 5 (transpose up by one octave), harmonic anchor notes → octave 3 (chord tones that outline the implied bass). In each bar, beats 1, 2+, and 4 go to the upper voice; beats 2 and 3 go to the lower voice. This gives you a strict alternating pattern: upper, lower, upper, lower, upper.

Bar 1 — Dm

  • Beat 1: D5 — quarter (upper voice, root)
  • Beat 2: A3 — eighth (lower voice, fifth of Dm)
  • Beat 2+: E5 — eighth (upper, stepwise)
  • Beat 3: G3 — quarter (lower, inner voice)
  • Beat 4: F5 — quarter (upper, stepwise)

Upper voice hears: D5–E5–F5 (ascending step). Lower voice hears: A3–G3 (descending step). Two independent motions in one line.

Bar 2 — moving toward C

  • Beat 1: C5 — quarter (upper, sequence step down)
  • Beat 2: G3 — eighth (lower, fifth of C)
  • Beat 2+: D5 — eighth (upper)
  • Beat 3: F3 — quarter (lower)
  • Beat 4: E5 — quarter (upper)

Bar 3 — Bb area

  • Beat 1: D5 — quarter (upper)
  • Beat 2: Bb3 — eighth (lower, root of Bb)
  • Beat 2+: C5 — eighth (upper, stepwise)
  • Beat 3: F3 — quarter (lower, fifth of Bb)
  • Beat 4: E5 — quarter (upper)

Bar 4 — break and land

  • Beat 1: A5 — quarter (upper, leap up to peak)
  • Beat 2: D3 — eighth (lower, root anchor)
  • Beat 2+: F5 — eighth (upper, stepping down)
  • Beat 3: A3 — quarter (lower)
  • Beat 4: D5 — quarter note (upper, final arrival — hold)

The D5 at the end is the arrival point. Upper voice traces the arc of the original melody, now at full height. Lower voice reinforces D minor at the root. The two-voice illusion should be clear when you play it back.

What to listen for

Play it back and listen closely. You should hear:

  • An upper melody descending gradually from D5 through the sequence
  • A lower voice outlining chord tones (A, G, Bb, D) in octave 3
  • The whole thing implying a Dm → C → Bb → Dm progression
  • The huge leap on bar 4 beat 1 (A5) as a climactic peak before the descent to D5

If it sounds like one wide-ranging melody rather than two voices, zoom in and make sure no notes have wandered into octave 4. The gap is what makes the illusion work. Save this session — the compound melody becomes the raw material for Chapter 3, where you'll add a real second voice underneath it.
