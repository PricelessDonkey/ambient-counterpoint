CHAPTER M13 ◇ SLOW HARMONIC RHYTHM ◇ CHAPTER M13 ◇ SLOW HARMONIC RHYTHM ◇ CHAPTER M13 ◇ SLOW HARMONIC RHYTHM Slow Harmonic Rhythm Chords that hold for 8 or 16 bars — not stasis, but patience. Bach changes chords every beat or bar; Glass and Reich change chords every 4, 8, or 16 bars. This chapter explains why slow harmonic rhythm is a compositional strength, not a limitation — and what melodic work must compensate when harmony holds still.

Harmonic rhythm is simply how often the harmony changes. In Bach's sequences, chords can change on every beat — the harmonic rhythm is very fast. In a normal Bach phrase, harmony changes every half-bar or bar. In Glass and Reich, a single chord might hold for an entire 8 or 16-bar loop.

Every piece has a harmonic rhythm, even if it's "never." A 16-bar drone is a valid harmonic rhythm choice — it means one chord per 16 bars. The question isn't whether harmonic rhythm is fast or slow; it's whether it matches the compositional intent.

In a loop-based structure, the listener isn't tracking a harmonic narrative — they're listening to texture and surface detail. A chord that lasts 8 bars becomes a room the listener inhabits, not a signpost they're passing.

This is why slow harmonic rhythm isn't an absence of craft — it's a different kind of craft. The composer says: "I trust this sound world enough to stay in it." That trust, when earned, creates the characteristic minimalist quality of immersive presence. The listener stops waiting for the next chord and starts hearing the chord.

Fast harmonic rhythm creates momentum and expectation. Slow harmonic rhythm creates depth and settledness. Minimalism is built on the second.

With slow harmony, melodic and rhythmic interest must do more work. The surface of the music — rhythm, texture, register, micro-variation — has to supply what harmony would normally provide in a faster-moving piece.

Techniques for sustaining interest across 8 bars of the same chord:

  • Phase shifting — let two voices drift apart and create new coincidences (M06)
  • Rhythmic displacement — shift the pattern by 8th-note offsets every few bars (M07)
  • Micro-variation — small pitch or rhythm changes every 4–8 repetitions (M03)
  • Register shifts — move the same pattern up an octave for contrast
  • Dynamic shaping — a slow crescendo across 8 bars creates movement without harmonic change

If you feel the urge to change the chord at bar 5, resist it until bar 8. Then assess. The discomfort is part of the training.

Faster chord changes feel like more energy and forward momentum. Even if the tempo stays constant, a sequence of chord changes creates a sense of velocity — the harmony is "going somewhere."

As a piece evolves, slowing the harmonic rhythm creates a sense of settling — even if the notes keep moving at the same pace. The piece arrives at a slower harmonic rhythm, and the listener feels it has deepened or resolved, without the melody stopping.

This is how many Glass pieces end: the arpeggio pattern continues at the same tempo, but the chord changes get further apart. The music doesn't slow down — it opens up. The harmonic rhythm is doing structural work that would otherwise require a fermata or a ritardando.

Pieces with fast harmonic rhythm feel like they're traveling — moving through a landscape of chords toward a destination. Pieces with slow harmonic rhythm feel like they're hovering — suspended in a sonic space, examining it from different angles.

Both are valid compositional postures. The important thing is to choose consciously. A piece that travels without meaning to — because the composer couldn't resist changing chords — will feel unfocused. A piece that hovers without meaning to — because the composer was avoiding decisions — will feel stagnant. Know which one you're writing.

For Bach's use of harmonic momentum and frequent chord changes as structural narrative, see Ch 05 — Harmonic Foundations. The Bach approach treats rapid harmonic rhythm as the engine of forward motion; minimalism treats harmonic stillness as a different kind of strength.
From M12: expand the vamp to 16 bars and practice harmonic patience

Open your M12 session. You have 6 active layers: Hocket A, Hocket B, Phase B, M08 poly, pulse, and the 8-bar arpeggiated vamp (Dm7 × 4 bars → Gsus4 × 4 bars).

Step 1 — Expand the arpeggio clip to 16 bars

Edit the arpeggiated chord clip to 16 bars: Dm7 for bars 1–8, then Gsus4 for bars 9–16. The 6-note arpeggio pattern (D4–F4–A4–C5–A4–F4 cycling, then G3–C4–D4–G4–C4–D4 cycling) continues unbroken through both halves. The arpeggio is now making 16 complete laps before the chord changes.

Step 2 — Add a micro-variation in bars 9–12

In Hocket A or Hocket B, introduce a small pitch change during bars 9–12 — the mid-point of the Gsus4 section. This is the moment when the listener may need something new. Options: raise one note by a step, add a grace note, leave a rest where there was a note. Make it small enough that it feels like an acknowledgment, not a departure.

Step 3 — Return to the original melody for bars 13–16

Bars 13–16 return to the original unvaried melody. The full shape is: Dm7 (bars 1–8, unchanged) → Gsus4 (bars 9–12, melody serves the listener with one small gesture) → Gsus4 (bars 13–16, return to original). This is the principle of harmonic patience in action.

Result

You now have a 16-bar chord cycle where harmony moves once, the melody acknowledges the change with a single micro-variation, and then settles back. This is harmonic patience + melodic service — the core of how Glass and Reich sustain listener interest across long harmonic durations.
