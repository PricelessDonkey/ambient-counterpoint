Melodic Toolkit Compositional idioms for music that blends Bach-style voice-leading rigor with minimalist loop evolution. Beauty through the interaction of simple things.
Voice-Leading Inside Arpeggios

Every arpeggio pattern is actually two or more melodies happening simultaneously. When you write an 8-note loop, the odd-numbered notes should form a coherent melody and the even-numbered notes should form a different one. Test by soloing each — if each line moves by steps, has contour, and implies harmony on its own, the arpeggio will sound rich. If either sounds random, the whole pattern will feel hollow.

Register as Structure

Assign each voice its own octave range and keep it there. Upper voice in octave 5, lower voice in octave 3, middle voice (if any) in octave 4. Don't let them cross. The vertical separation lets the ear parse a single stream as multiple voices. When you eventually let the voices converge into the same octave, it sounds like arrival — save it for the end.
One Note Per Cycle

When evolving a loop, change exactly one note every 2–4 repetitions. Not two notes, not a rhythm change — one pitch. Alternate between changing a note in the upper voice and the lower voice, so the two implied melodies evolve at different rates. The mismatch in their evolution speeds is where the interest lives.

Sequence as Meditation

Take any short figure and repeat it starting one scale step lower (or higher). Do this three times maximum. Three descending statements create momentum and expectation. On the fourth iteration, break the pattern. In a looping context, spread a three-step sequence across 12–16 bars so the descent is felt rather than heard.
Contrary Motion as Default

Whenever you're unsure which direction to move a note, move it opposite to whatever the other voice just did. Upper voice stepped up? Lower voice steps down. Make this the default and break it intentionally when you want convergence or divergence for dramatic effect.

The Pedal Freeze

When tension needs to build, stop one voice entirely and let it repeat the same note while the other keeps evolving. The stillness amplifies the movement. The longer the freeze, the more weight the moment carries when the frozen voice finally moves again.

Suspension Across Loops

When a note in one loop clashes with a note in another, don't fix it — let it ring and resolve naturally on the next cycle. These momentary dissonances are Bach's suspensions happening organically through phasing rather than composed beat by beat. If both loops move by steps, most collisions resolve themselves within a beat or two.
Chromatic Visitors, Not Residents

Notes outside the scale should appear in a weak rhythmic position (an "and," not a downbeat) and resolve by step to a chord tone on the next strong beat. One chromatic note per pattern is enough. Two sounds bebop. Zero sounds folk.

The Leading Tone as Emotional Climax

Save the raised 7th (C♯ in D minor) for the moment of highest tension. Everything before it should be natural minor or Dorian — no raised 7th anywhere. When it finally appears, it transforms the mood by introducing a gravitational pull that didn't exist before. One note, deployed once, at the right moment.
Phase Displacement for Free Complexity

Layer two loops of different lengths. A 7-note loop against an 8-note loop takes 56 notes before exact realignment. Minutes of unique vertical combinations from two simple patterns. Both loops must be harmonically compatible in most alignments — stick to the same scale or mode.

Velocity as a Third Voice

Alternate loud and soft notes so accented notes form their own rhythmic pattern independent of pitch. If your pitch loop is 8 notes and your accent pattern is 3 (loud, soft, soft…), the accents cycle against the pitches and create a third evolving layer. Sinephro gets this naturally from harp technique — emphasis drifts.
The Subtraction Arc

Structure a piece by removing rather than adding. Start with all loops running, let the listener absorb the full texture, then pull voices out one at a time. The ghost of the removed voice lingers in memory. Alternatively: add voices in the first half, subtract in reverse order in the second half. Arch form — the ending mirrors the beginning but sounds completely different.

Complexity comes from the interaction of simple things rather than from any single part being complex. Every individual element should be almost boring on its own. The music lives in the relationships between them.
