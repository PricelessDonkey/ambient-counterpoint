Adding a Second Voice

Real counterpoint begins the moment two independent lines think for themselves.

Chapter 2 gave you one line that sounded like two. Now you add the real second voice — a separate MIDI track with its own pitch, rhythm, and trajectory. The craft here is keeping those two voices genuinely independent: each one should make melodic sense on its own, and together they should create something neither could say alone. Four motion types govern how two voices can move relative to each other — knowing them by ear is the whole skill.
1
The Four Motion Types

How any two voices can move

At any moment, two voices can relate to each other in exactly four ways. Contrary motion is the default — when in doubt, send them in opposite directions. The other three are tools for specific musical effects.

In practice: use contrary motion whenever both voices need to move. Use parallel when you want warmth (see card 2). Use oblique when one voice should freeze and listen (card 3). Use voice exchange at cadences for a fresh harmonic perspective without new notes (card 4).

2
Parallel Thirds and Sixths

The sweet-sounding exception

Parallel motion is usually discouraged — but parallel thirds and sixths are the exception. When two voices move in the same direction while staying a third or sixth apart, the effect is warm, sweet, and full. Bach uses it constantly for expressive high points.

Why thirds and sixths — not fifths or octaves

A third or sixth leaves room for harmonic color: the interval can be major or minor as the melody dictates, keeping each note sounding like it belongs to the scale. A fifth or octave has no such flexibility — it just doubles the same harmonic function. Paralleling them merges the voices into one thick strand instead of two.

Parallel thirds and sixths are the sound of agreement without merger. The voices stay distinct in pitch but move as a unit — use them for passages where the two voices share an emotional intention.
3
Oblique Motion — The Pedal Freeze

One voice holds, one voice moves

In oblique motion, one voice sustains a single pitch — the pedal point — while the other voice moves freely above or below it. The name comes from organ playing: the organist's foot holds a low bass note while the hands spin out melody and harmony above.

What it does to the listener

Stillness amplifies motion. When one voice stops, the ear focuses entirely on the voice that keeps moving. Every pitch the moving voice lands on is heard against the held note — some consonant, some dissonant — creating a slow build of harmonic tension. When the pedal finally resolves or the held voice finally releases, the relief is real.

In ambient music

The pedal point is the drone. Your bass synth holds a D for 8 bars while a melodic line wanders above it. The tension you feel when the melody lands on an E or a C# against that D? That's oblique motion working exactly as Bach intended.

The pedal point does nothing — and that nothing says everything. Freeze the bass and the upper voice suddenly has all the attention. Every clash against the drone earns its resolution.
4
Voice Exchange

Turning the chord inside out

A voice exchange happens when two voices swap their notes: whatever pitch was in the top voice moves to the bottom, and vice versa. The harmony stays the same — same notes, same chord — but the perspective rotates completely. Bach uses this at cadence points to refresh a phrase without introducing new harmonic material.

In D minor at the cadence

Your piece has been sitting on a Dm chord with A4 on top and D3 on the bottom. A voice exchange puts D4 on top and A3 on the bottom — the interval inverts from a fifth to a fourth, and the chord suddenly sounds like it shifted gear. Then the lower voice drops a fourth to D3 while the upper holds D4 — both arrive on D for the final landing.

Voice exchange refreshes a phrase without adding new notes — same chord, turned inside out. Use it at cadence points when the harmony needs to breathe before the final landing.
5
What to Avoid — Parallel Fifths and Octaves

Why parallel fifths and octaves collapse the voices

When two voices move in parallel perfect fifths or perfect octaves, they stop sounding like independent lines and merge into a single doubled voice. The counterpoint collapses — instead of two characters in conversation, you get one character speaking in unison with its echo. The voices lose their identities.

How to spot them

Play each voice alone. Now play them together. If you can't tell which is which — if they sound like one heavy line — you probably have parallel fifths or octaves. The quick check: if both voices moved in the same direction and the interval between them didn't change, name that interval. P5 or P8? Rewrite.

The exception

A unison D at the very end of a piece — both voices arriving on D together — is fine and deliberate. That's a unison cadence, not careless parallel motion. The rule applies to motion through a phrase, not the final note.

If you can't hear two separate voices, the counterpoint has collapsed into one. Parallel P5s and P8s are the sound of that collapse — they don't just break a rule, they defeat the whole point of writing two voices.

Build It — Add the Second Voice

Open your Ch 02 session — the 4-bar compound melody zigzagging between octave 3 and octave 5. Now split it into two real MIDI tracks: Track 1 (Upper Voice) gets the octave-5 notes, Track 2 (Lower Voice) gets the octave-3 notes. You already have the material; the task now is to treat them as two genuinely independent voices and shape the lower voice to respond to the upper using the techniques from this chapter.

Bars 1–2 — Contrary motion

The upper voice from Ch 02 ascends D5→E5→F5 in bar 1, then peaks and turns back down in bar 2. Shape the lower voice to mirror that arc in opposite direction — descending in bar 1 as the upper rises, ascending in bar 2 as the upper falls. Use the enriched note pairs as your reference:

  • Beat 1: Upper D5 / Lower A3 — voices at a 5th, both strong
  • Beat 2: Upper E5 / Lower G3 — upper steps up, lower steps down
  • Beat 3: Upper F5 / Lower F3 — voices meet at F two octaves apart
  • Beat 4: Upper A4 / Lower D3 — upper leaps down, lower arrives at D root

Bar 2: upper descends G4→F4→E4→D4 while lower ascends E3→F3→G3→A3. Listen — as one voice falls, the other rises. The voices diverge and converge like two hands on a keyboard.

Bar 3 — Pedal freeze (lower voice)

Lower voice: place a D3 whole note — four beats, unmoving. Upper voice: let it climb freely, F4→G4→A4→C5, building tension above the pedal. Notice how the C5 against the D3 drone creates a palpable dissonance. That tension is what makes bar 4 feel like release.

Bar 4 — Voice exchange at the cadence

End of bar 3: Upper is at C5, Lower is holding D3. Now execute the exchange:

  • Beat 1–2: Upper drops to D4 (half note) / Lower rises to A3 (half note) — positions swapped
  • Beat 3–4: Upper holds D4 / Lower drops to D3 (half note) — both land on D

The orange notes are the exchange — Upper took the D, Lower took the A. Play back both voices together and notice how the cadence has weight: the voice exchange gives it a sense of rotation before the final unison landing.

Two tracks, two voices, one idea. When you loop this 4-bar piece, you should hear the lower voice as a distinct musical personality — not just a bass note under a melody, but a line that has its own direction and intention. That's counterpoint.
