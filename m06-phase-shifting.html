Phase shifting is Reich's core discovery: when two identical loops play simultaneously and one is nudged slightly ahead, their combined onsets create rhythmic and harmonic patterns that neither loop contains alone. No additional composition required — the process generates the material.
What phase shifting is

Take any repeating loop and copy it to a second track. Both tracks play the same notes. Now nudge one track slightly ahead — by a 16th note, an 8th note, a quarter note. The two identical loops begin to sound like something entirely new: rhythmic combinations and implied melodies that neither loop contains on its own.

Reich discovered this in the 1960s using tape loops: two recordings of the same speech fragment, one playing fractionally faster, gradually drifting out of alignment and back into it. The emergent patterns were the composition. This is the mechanism behind Piano Phase, It's Gonna Rain, and Come Out.

Phase shifting is a rhythmic form of stretto — the same material overlapping with itself at a time delay. For the Bach counterpart, see the stretto canon section in Ch 08 — Motivic Development and the phase displacement discussion in Ch 11 — Ambient Counterpoint.
Tape phase: continuous drift

In Reich's original technique, phase was gradual: two tape machines running at slightly different speeds. The loop drifted continuously through every possible alignment — from unison to maximum displacement and back. Every intermediate state was heard in sequence, over the course of minutes.

DAW phase: stepped offsets

In a DAW you implement stepped phase: nudge one clip forward by a specific grid increment (one 16th, one 8th, one quarter) and leave it there. You choose a specific alignment and compose with that interference pattern. This trades continuous evolution for precise, repeatable control.

The emergent melody — offset 4 8ths

At exactly 4 8th notes offset (half a bar), Loop A and Loop B perfectly interlock: Loop A plays notes on beats 1–2, Loop B fills beats 3–4, and together they create a continuous 8-note stream — D–F–G–A–D–F–G–A — spanning the entire bar without any gaps. This is a pattern that neither loop plays alone.

The ascending D–F–G–A shape appears twice within this single arc — first from Loop A (blue dots, beats 1–2), then from Loop B (green dots, beats 3–4) — producing one continuous 8-note stream. Neither loop plays this on its own; it exists only in their combination. Listen for it: it will feel like a third voice has appeared.
When do two loops realign?

Two loops of the same length will always return to unison after exactly one loop cycle — boring. The interesting case is when the loop length doesn't divide evenly into the bar. A 7-note loop in an 8-beat grid won't realign with the bar until 7 × 8 = 56 beats have passed. That's 14 bars of slow drift before the pattern resets.

This is the fundamental structure of Piano Phase: a 12-note pattern creates a 12-step phase cycle. For the D minor seed: a 5-note variant (D–F–G–A–G) in a 4-beat bar takes 5 × 4 = 20 beats before both loops align on beat 1 together.

Choosing loop lengths

For the longest phase cycle (most variety before reset), choose loop lengths that share no common factors. Prime-length loops (5, 7, 11 notes) resist quick realignment. A 7-note loop against a 4-beat bar: 28 beats. A 5-note loop against a 3-beat bar: 15 beats. For shorter, more predictable structures — like a 4-note loop over a 4-beat bar — the loops realign every single bar, which produces steady periodic motion rather than evolving drift.

The loop length and the phase offset should not share obvious common factors. A 4-note loop with a 2-note offset realigns every 2 bars; a 4-note loop with a 3-note offset takes 4 bars. Choose deliberately.
Setting up stepped phase

In Ableton, stepped phase is simple:

  1. Duplicate your melody MIDI track (Cmd+D on Mac). Rename the copy Phase B.
  2. Open the Phase B clip. Select all notes (Cmd+A). Use the nudge command to shift them by your chosen offset — hold Option and press ← / → to nudge by the current grid increment. Set the grid to sixteenth notes first to get a one-16th offset.
  3. Alternatively: right-click the clip's start marker in the clip view and enter the beat offset manually. This keeps the original MIDI data intact.
  4. Route both tracks to different instruments — or to the same instrument with a slightly different reverb tail — to let the two voices breathe separately in the mix.
Warping for gradual phase

To approximate tape-style continuous drift: enable Warp on the Phase B clip and set its tempo to 100.5 BPM while the project runs at 100. Phase B will drift at roughly 0.5% per bar — very slowly, like Reich's original tape technique. Over 200 bars, the loop drifts approximately one full cycle, depending on loop length.

For a live arrangement, launch Phase B manually with a deliberate delay after Loop A to lock in your chosen offset. This gives you full control without automation. For the relationship between phase and structural displacement, see the discussion in Ch 11 — Ambient Counterpoint.
Add Phase B to your session

Open your M05 session. You have the four additive clips (M05-A1 through M05-A4) and the M04 pulse track running throughout a 32-bar arc.

  1. Duplicate the M05-A4 track (the full D–F–G–A pattern). Rename the duplicate Phase B.
  2. In the Phase B clip, select all notes and nudge them forward by exactly one 16th note. This is your starting offset.
  3. Play both tracks — M05-A4 and Phase B — simultaneously in Session View. Listen to what the 1-sixteenth offset produces. Notice the slight delay between corresponding notes: the doubled texture feels wider and more alive than either loop alone.
  4. Experiment: nudge Phase B by one more 16th (now 2 total). Then try 4 8ths. At offset 4 8ths, hear the emergent continuous stream that appears — the two loops interlocking to fill the entire bar.
  5. Choose the offset that sounds most interesting to you. Leave Phase B at that position.

The pulse track (M04) continues running throughout as rhythmic constant. Your session now has three layers: the M05 additive arc, Phase B running alongside it, and the pulse as anchor. This is the core of your working piece — enough texture for the form-building that starts in M07.
