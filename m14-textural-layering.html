Glass and Reich never pile up melodies. When a new voice enters, it brings something new to the texture — a different register, a different timbre, a different rhythmic density. The result is richness without complexity. This chapter covers how to assign each layer its own territory, how to sequence their entries, and why removing a layer can be the most dramatic event in a minimalist piece.
One dimension per layer

In a minimalist texture, each new layer brings exactly one new thing: a different register, a new timbre, or a new rhythmic density. It does not bring a new melody. The loop material may be derived from the same cell as every other layer — just displaced, transposed, or thinned.

The goal is density without clutter. When every layer occupies its own territory, the listener can hold the full texture in mind. When two layers compete for the same space, the texture collapses into mud.

Register assignment

Decide in advance which octave range each layer owns. A four-layer texture in D Dorian divides naturally:

  • Bass drone — D1–D2 (felt more than heard; establishes the root)
  • Arpeggiated field — D3–D4 (mid-background harmonic motion)
  • Melodic hocket — D4–D5 (foreground; the listener's ear follows this)
  • High shimmer — D5–D6 (sparse ornament at the edge of attention)

Layers may cross registers at deliberate moments — a descending arpeggio dipping into the bass range, a hocket note reaching into shimmer territory — but these crossings should be intentional events, not accidents of voicing.

If two layers share a similar register, they must have clearly different rhythms to stay distinct. The ear separates tracks by register, timbre, and rhythmic profile. When two of those three factors match, the layers blur together.

Compatible pairs
  • Whole notes vs. 8th notes — maximal contrast; the sustained note creates a harmonic bed while the 8th notes carry motion
  • Sparse (1 note/bar) vs. dense (8 notes/bar) — the sparse layer sounds like punctuation against the dense layer's continuous motion
  • Quarter notes vs. a displaced triplet — rhythmic contrast without register separation
Problematic pairs
  • Two 8th-note arpeggios in the same octave — they fight for the same space and merge into a muddy mass. Fix: move one up an octave, or change one to quarter notes.
  • Two sustained pads in overlapping registers — indistinct. Fix: assign non-overlapping registers or differentiate timbres dramatically.

When in doubt: if two tracks sound like one track, collapse them or separate them. There is no middle ground.

The order in which layers enter is a compositional decision. It shapes what the listener hears as "foundation," "motion," and "ornament." A standard minimalist entry sequence:

  1. Bass first — establishes the root and the modal center. Nothing else can be heard as "high" or "low" until the bass is present.
  2. Arpeggio or pulse — establishes the rhythmic grid and harmonic color. The listener's internal clock locks onto this layer.
  3. Melodic voice (hocket) — now that foundation and motion are present, the melody has a context to emerge from.
  4. Shimmer last — the highest, sparsest voice floats above a texture that is already stable. It needs that stability to register as an addition rather than a distraction.

Each entry should be an event — something the listener notices. Don't slip layers in underneath the listener's attention. A sudden quiet entrance should be felt.

Adding a layer is obvious. Removing one is counterintuitive — and often more powerful. In a dense minimalist texture, the ear quickly adjusts to what is present. When a layer drops out, the remaining layers suddenly sound clearer, more exposed, more intimate. The silence a removed layer leaves behind becomes foreground.

Philip Glass uses this constantly: a full four-layer texture plays for 16 bars, the arpeggio drops out for 8, and the bass and hocket suddenly feel raw and direct. When the arpeggio re-enters, it sounds fresh — as if heard for the first time again.

Reduction strategies
  • Drop the densest layer — removing the arpeggio (the most rhythmically busy voice) immediately opens up space. Bass and shimmer, which were nearly inaudible in the full texture, become clearly defined.
  • Drop to a single voice — a moment of solo bass or solo melody before a new section is one of the most effective transitions in minimalist music.
  • Stagger removals — remove layers one at a time over 8–16 bars. The texture thins gradually, building tension by subtraction.

For Bach's equivalent technique — giving voices rhythmic breathing room so individual lines remain audible — see Ch 10: Space & Breathing. The principle is structurally identical; the time scale is different.

In minimalism, subtraction is composition. The moment when a layer drops out is as carefully chosen as the moment it entered. A texture you can add to is a texture you can subtract from — and the subtraction often lands harder.

Open your M13 session (100 BPM, with Hocket A, Hocket B, Phase B, M08 poly, pulse, and the 16-bar arpeggiated Dm7→Gsus4 chord cycle). Today you assemble a clean 4-layer texture and hear the full architecture you've been building toward.

Step 1 — Consolidate the session

Mute or delete Phase B and the M08 poly track. Those layers served their purpose in earlier chapters; today you're building a cleaner, hierarchical texture. Keep active: Hocket A, Hocket B, the M12 arpeggio vamp, and the M04 pulse (or mute the pulse if the arpeggio's rhythm is sufficient).

Step 2 — Add the bass drone

Create a new MIDI track below the arpeggio. Choose a sustained sub-bass or bass pad timbre (low sine, bass synth, or muted sub). Create a 16-bar MIDI clip: D2 whole notes, one per bar. Set the track volume to −6dB relative to your other tracks — the bass should be felt more than heard, a low-end presence rather than a melody.

Step 3 — Set the arpeggio as mid-background

Your M12 arpeggio (the 6-note Dm7/Gsus4 cycling 8th-note pattern over 8 bars per chord) is already present. Pull it back to −3dB relative to the hocket. It provides the harmonic weather; the listener should sense it rather than consciously follow it.

Step 4 — Confirm the hocket as foreground

Your interlocking Hocket A and Hocket B tracks are the melody. Set them to 0dB (your reference level). Everything else serves the hocket. Check that it is clearly the loudest and most present voice.

Step 5 — Add the high shimmer

Create a new MIDI track at the top of the stack. Choose a sparse, high timbre: a bell, music box, or filtered high synth. Create a 4-bar clip: D5 on beat 1 of bar 1, A5 on beat 1 of bar 3, silence for bars 3–4. Set note velocity to 40–55 (very soft). Set volume so it barely registers — a detail that rewards close listening, not a statement.

Step 6 — Balance and test

Run all four layers over the full 16-bar chord progression. Ask: can you hear each layer as distinct? Is the hocket clearly the foreground? Does the bass feel like a foundation rather than a melody? Now mute the arpeggio for 8 bars — notice how the texture changes, how the bass and hocket suddenly feel exposed. Restore the arpeggio and listen again. That gap is the technique.
