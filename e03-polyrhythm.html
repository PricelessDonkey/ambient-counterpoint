Two patterns playing simultaneously at different internal speeds. Neither is faster — they simply divide time differently, and where they coincide, the ear hears an accent neither voice placed deliberately. Polyrhythm is how ecstatic music generates felt momentum without increasing tempo: the groove accelerates not because the notes get faster but because the coincidence points get farther apart.

When two patterns with different internal lengths play simultaneously, the listener's ear tracks both at once and hears their intersection. A pattern of 2 against a pattern of 3 doesn't produce 5 notes — it produces 5 events with a joint structure: two rhythms, one listener, and the moment they briefly land together.

That shared landing point is felt as an accent neither voice placed deliberately. It emerges from the relationship between them. This is the source of the complexity: not the individual voices, which may be simple, but the grid they collectively produce.

The result is a felt sense of momentum and propulsion that neither pattern generates alone. The listener isn't just tracking rhythm — they're tracking the phase relationship between two rhythms, anticipating when the next coincidence will arrive.

The simplest polyrhythm: two evenly-spaced notes against three evenly-spaced notes over the same span. In a 6/8 context, the "2" voice plays two dotted-quarter notes (one per half-bar); the "3" voice plays three quarter notes (one per third-bar).

They coincide only at beat 1. That single shared downbeat becomes the anchor — the moment both voices breathe together before diverging again. Pianists often use the phrase "pass the salt, please" to internalize the pattern: "pass the" for the 3, "salt" for the 2.

Yellow = coincidence point (both voices land together). Blue = 2-note voice. Green = 3-note voice. The bar has 6 eighth-note slots; the two patterns divide them differently.

Hocket (from the medieval Latin hoquetus, "hiccup") is a technique where two voices alternate in the spaces each leaves. Neither voice sounds complete alone. Together they create a continuous interlocking texture — a single rhythm distributed across two performers or two tracks.

This is the logic behind house music's bass-melody interaction, Reich's Clapping Music, and much African polyrhythm. The incompleteness of each voice is the point: the listener's brain composes the whole from the parts.

In production terms, hocket is one of the most effective tools for groove density without frequency crowding — the voices share time rather than layering into the same sonic space.

The seed groove meets the new pattern

Blue = seed groove (beats 1-and through 3). Orange = new 3-note pattern (beats 1, 2-and, 4). Grey = overlap (both voices). The combined row shows a near-continuous stream with one gap — every slot filled except beat 4-and.

A 3-beat pattern repeating inside a 4-beat bar takes 4 bars to realign — a 12-beat cycle. During those 12 beats, the 3-note pattern visits 4 different beat positions across successive bars, never landing on the downbeat until bar 5.

This long-period realignment is the structural engine of Reich's Music for 18 Musicians and much of Riley's work. The listener's ear tracks the 3-note pattern sliding through the bar, accumulating tension as it drifts further from the downbeat, and finally releasing when the two patterns land together.

The sense of the two patterns "chasing" each other across bars — that anticipatory feeling of an upcoming coincidence — is a core component of ecstatic propulsion. It isn't tension in the harmonic sense; it's temporal tension.

Yellow = coincidence (both patterns land together). Blue = 4-beat pulse. Purple = 3-note repeating pattern. The 12-beat cycle runs three full bars before the two patterns realign — beat 13 is where the ecstatic arrival happens.

For phase shifting — a related technique using two identical loops slightly offset in time — see M06 — Phase Shifting. Phase shifting is a kind of polyrhythm with an extremely long cycle period.

These terms are often confused. The distinction matters for production:

Polyrhythm — two different rhythmic patterns within the same meter. Both voices share a common downbeat. One voice plays 3 notes where the other plays 4, but they agree on where bar 1 falls. The complexity is rhythmic; the structure is shared.

Polymetrism — two different meters simultaneously. One part is genuinely in 3/4 while another is in 4/4. They may agree on bar 1 but diverge immediately and won't realign until bar 13 (a 12-beat cycle). The complexity is structural; the effect is more disorienting.

Ecstatic music almost always uses polyrhythm within a shared 4/4 pulse, not polymetrism. The shared downbeat is the anchor that makes the cross-rhythms feel exciting rather than confusing. Losing the shared downbeat loses the listener's ability to track the coincidence — and without the anticipation of arrival, there is no ecstatic release.

Keep the downbeat shared. Polyrhythm gives the listener two threads to follow — the rhythm and the meta-rhythm of when they meet. Polymetrism gives the listener a puzzle. Ecstatic music chooses complexity that resolves, not complexity that disorients.
Continue your E01-Ecstatic session — add the hocket track

Open your E01-Ecstatic.als session. You currently have one MIDI track: the seed groove (D4–F#4–A4–C4, starting on beat 1-and, looping at 108 BPM).

1. Add a second MIDI track. Assign a percussive or plucked sound — something with a sharp attack and short decay (a marimba, plucked synth, or short-attack pad works well). Name it "Hocket."

2. Program the 3-note pattern:

  • D3 on beat 1 (the downbeat)
  • A3 on beat 2-and (the 8th after beat 2)
  • G3 on beat 4 (the last quarter note of the bar)

Each note should be an 8th note duration. Set velocities around 90 — slightly softer than the seed groove to let it sit underneath.

3. Loop both tracks together. Listen for the point where the two tracks "arrive" together on a strong shared downbeat. Count how many bars it takes. That moment of shared arrival — where both tracks breathe on beat 1 simultaneously — should feel like a small ecstatic event even at this early stage.

4. Note what you hear. Does the new pattern make the seed groove feel busier, or more grounded? Which of the three new notes — D3, A3, or G3 — has the most impact when it arrives?

The 3-against-4 relationship means the hocket pattern will visit each beat position across 4 bars before realigning. That sliding quality — notes appearing in unexpected places — is the polyrhythmic momentum working. The session now has two independent voices; the groove is no longer just one idea repeating.
